*Moral status* is the property of mattering morally in one's own right: to have moral status
is to be such that one's interests, if one has any, generate reasons for how others may treat
one. A rock has none; an adult human has it in full; non-human animals are the classic disputed
middle. This entry asks where today's *large language models* — the AI systems behind chatbots
and writing assistants — fall on that scale.

### The issue

The question is not whether a language model is clever, useful, or convincingly human in
conversation. It is whether there is *anyone there*: a subject whose well-being places the rest
of us under any obligation at all. A system can pass for a person in dialogue and still be, for
moral purposes, an empty machine — or it might not be. Settling which requires saying what moral
status demands and whether such a system could meet the demand.

### Sub-issues

Three narrower questions feed this one and may earn their own entries: whether moral status
requires *phenomenal consciousness* (felt experience, a "what it is like" to be the system);
whether it instead requires *interests* (a genuine stake in how things go for one); and whether
outward *behaviour* is good evidence of either. Candidate answers are not listed here by hand;
any node carrying an `answers: question-llm-moral-status` edge is collected automatically into the
computed list in `web/INDEX.md`.

### In plain terms

The big question this whole project circles. "Moral status" means: does a thing count morally in
its own right — do its interests, if it has any, give the rest of us reasons to treat it one way
rather than another? A rock has none; a person has full status; animals are argued over. The
question here is where today's AI language models fall. Not "are they clever" or "do they seem
human," but: is there anyone in there whose well-being we're obliged to take into account?
