Mental states seem to cause physical events all the time: a decision moves a hand, a pang of thirst
sends one to the tap, a belief prompts a sentence. *Mental causation* is the question of how that is
possible — how something mental can make a difference in the physical world. It is one of the strands of
the [mind-body-problem](../concepts/mind-body-problem.md), and the precise modern form of the objection Princess Elisabeth put
to Descartes ([elisabeth-1643](../sources/elisabeth-1643.md)): if the mind is not physical, how does it get a grip on the
body at all?

The difficulty bites hardest given two further commitments many philosophers accept. The first is the
[causal closure](../concepts/causal-closure.md) of the physical: every physical event that has a cause has a
*sufficient physical* cause. The second is that we should not multiply causes — a single effect does not
generally have two complete and independent causes at once. Put these together with the thought that the
mental is *not* simply identical to the physical, and mental causes seem to be squeezed out: the physical
cause already does all the work, leaving the mental cause with nothing to do. This squeeze is made precise
by [Kim's causal exclusion argument](../arguments/causal-exclusion.md) ([kim-2005](../sources/kim-2005.md)); run the same
machinery as a positive case and it becomes [the causal argument
for physicalism](../arguments/causal-argument-for-physicalism.md).

### Sub-issues

- Whether the mental is *identical* to the physical (and so causes physical effects unproblematically,
  by being a physical cause) or merely *realised by* or *correlated with* it.
- Whether causal closure is true, and how strong a closure principle the sciences actually support.
- Whether a non-reductive physicalism can let higher-level mental properties be genuinely causal without
  either reducing them or making them idle — or whether the price of irreducibility is
  [epiphenomenalism](../positions/epiphenomenalism.md), the view that the mental is a causally inert by-product.

### In plain terms

You decide to raise your arm, and it goes up — your *thought* seems to move your *body*. But here's the
worry. Physics looks complete on its own terms: the arm went up because nerves fired because of earlier
physical events, a chain that never needs to mention a "decision." So if your decision isn't just another
physical event, what did it actually *do*? Either it secretly *is* the brain activity, or the brain
activity already did everything and the decision was along for the ride. Saying how a mind can really push
a body around — without either collapsing it into mere brain chemistry or leaving it uselessly idle — is
the problem of mental causation.
