Ordinary life treats some actions as *up to us*: you chose the salad, you could have chosen the
soup, and praise, blame, and regret all presuppose that the choosing was genuinely yours. The
free-will question asks whether anything answers to that picture — whether agents ever act freely,
and if so, what their freedom consists in.

The question bites because every action also sits inside the causal order. Each decision issues
from prior states — desires, deliberations, neural events — themselves the products of earlier
causes stretching back before the agent existed. If *determinism* is true, the laws of nature and
the distant past jointly fix exactly one future. The central dispute is then whether freedom is
**compatible** with such fixing ([freedom-compatible-with-determinism](../claims/freedom-compatible-with-determinism.md)): compatibilists
locate freedom in properties of the actual sequence — acting on one's own reasons, free of
compulsion — while incompatibilists insist freedom requires that more than one course of action be
genuinely open ([alternatives-genuinely-open](../claims/alternatives-genuinely-open.md)), whether secured by indeterminism, by
[agent causation](../concepts/agent-causation.md), or by nothing (in which case freedom is an illusion).
Indeterminism alone seems no help — a decision settled by quantum chance is no more *yours* than
one settled by the past — which is the *luck objection* that squeezes the libertarian from the
other side.

### Sub-issues

- Whether freedom requires genuinely open alternatives or only the right kind of actual-sequence
  control ([frankfurt-cases](../concepts/frankfurt-cases.md)).
- Whether the concept at stake is a metaphysical power, a condition of moral responsibility, or
  both — and whether those come apart.
- Whether the felt experience of choosing is evidence of origination or a construction after the
  fact.
- How this question mirrors the consciousness debate under the
  [consciousness–free-will duality](../concepts/consciousness-freewill-duality.md).
