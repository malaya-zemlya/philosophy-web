Thoughts are *about* things: a belief that Paris is in France reaches out to Paris, a fear is a fear *of*
something, a desire is *for* water. This directedness — [intentionality](../concepts/intentionality.md), the
"aboutness" of the mental — was called "the mark of the mental" by Franz Brentano
([brentano-1874](../sources/brentano-1874.md)). The question is how any merely physical state could have it. A pattern of
neurons, or of voltages, is just a configuration of matter; how does it come to *mean*, *represent*, or
*point beyond itself* to something it may never be in contact with, or that may not even exist?

The puzzle is sharpened by a distinction between *original* (or intrinsic) and *derived* intentionality
([intrinsic-derived-intentionality](../concepts/intrinsic-derived-intentionality.md)). The words on this page are about things only because
readers interpret them — their aboutness is *derived*, borrowed from minds that already mean things. The
challenge is to say where *original* aboutness comes from: the kind a mind has on its own, not in virtue
of being read by another mind. If a thought's content were derived in the same way a word's is, the
question would only regress to whoever does the deriving.

### Sub-issues

- Whether original intentionality can be *naturalised* — explained in terms of causal, informational, or
  biological-teleological relations between a state and what it is about — or whether it is primitive.
- Whether a computer's internal states have original intentionality or only the derived kind we read
  into them, the crux of [the Chinese Room](../concepts/chinese-room.md) and of
  [whether syntax suffices for semantics](../claims/syntax-insufficient-for-semantics.md).
- How intentionality relates to consciousness: whether aboutness requires a subject for whom the state
  means something, or can be wholly a matter of functional and causal role.

### In plain terms

Your thought about your grandmother is *about* her — it points at her, even if she's far away or no
longer alive. That pointing is strange when you remember a thought is, physically, just brain activity:
how does a lump of firing cells manage to be *about* anything outside itself? Ink and pixels are "about"
things too, but only because we read them that way — borrowed meaning. The hard part is explaining the
*original* meaning in a mind, the meaning that isn't just lent to it by some other mind. And it bears
directly on AI: when a chatbot writes a sentence about Paris, is it really thinking about Paris, or just
shuffling symbols we interpret as being about Paris?
