You are, in some sense, the same person who woke up this morning, and the same person as the child in
your oldest photograph — even though almost every cell has been replaced, your beliefs have changed, and
your memories have faded and shifted. *Personal identity* is the question of what that sameness consists
in: what makes a person at one time *numerically identical* to a person at another, the *same one*
persisting rather than a mere successor. It is the diachronic question — identity *over time* — as
opposed to the synchronic question of what makes something a person at a moment.

The historically central answer is the *psychological-continuity* criterion descending from John Locke
([locke-1689](../sources/locke-1689.md)), who located identity not in the persistence of a soul or a body but in continuity
of consciousness and memory: you are the person whose past experiences you can, in principle, remember as
your own. Its rival is some *physical* or *biological* criterion — same body, or same living organism (the
"animalist" view). Each is tested by imagined cases that pull identity apart from its usual companions:
brain transplants, teleportation, fission into two equally-continuous successors, and the gradual
replacement of one psychology by another.

### Sub-issues

- Which criterion, if any, is correct — psychological continuity, biological continuity, or a bare
  further fact (a soul or Cartesian ego).
- Whether identity can be a matter of *degree* or be *indeterminate*, rather than always sharply yes or
  no — Derek Parfit's conclusion from his spectrum cases ([parfit-1984](../sources/parfit-1984.md);
  [personal-identity-admits-of-degree](../claims/personal-identity-admits-of-degree.md)), reached by treating the question as an instance of the
  [no-sharp-line schema](../concepts/continuity-argument-schema.md) ([parfit-rewiring](../arguments/parfit-rewiring.md)).
- Whether identity is even *what matters* in survival, or whether what we care about (continuity of
  memory, character, projects) could be preserved even where strict identity fails.
- How the question bears on uploading, copying, and gradual silicon replacement — whether a faithful
  copy, or a slowly converted brain, preserves the person.

### In plain terms

The atoms in your body, most of your cells, many of your opinions, and a lot of your memories are
different from those of the toddler in your family's old photos — yet that toddler was *you*. What makes
that true? One classic answer: it's the thread of memory and psychological connection running through your
life, not the matter you're made of (so you'd go where your memories go, even into a new body). A rival
says it's being the same living animal. Philosophers test these with brain swaps, teleporters, and
"what if there were two of you?" cases — and some, like Parfit, conclude that being "the same person"
isn't always a clean yes-or-no but can come in degrees. The same puzzle returns, sharpened, for mind
uploading: is the upload *you*, or just a very good copy?
