*Are mental states substrate-independent?* That is: is whether a system can be in a given
mental state fixed entirely by which functional or causal role it realises, with no further
constraint from the physical material — carbon, silicon, and so on — that realises the role?
This is the governing issue behind every dispute over whether an artificial system could have
a mind.

The "substrate-independent" answer says the material drops out: realise the role, in any
medium, and you have the state. The denial says the material itself can constrain which minds
are possible, so that no pattern of cause and effect in the wrong stuff would suffice.

### Terms to pin down

Two terms carry the weight. First, *mental state*: does the question range over all mental
states uniformly, or do phenomenally-conscious states (what an experience feels like) behave
differently from merely functional or intentional ones such as belief? Second, *role*: how
finely is the realised role individuated — a coarse input-output profile, or a fine-grained
internal causal organisation? This is the question of [grain](../concepts/grain.md), and the answer shifts
what "realising the role" demands. Behind both lies [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md), which
individuates the mental by causal role in the first place.

### How it sits in the debate

The issue is narrower than [the moral-status question](llm-moral-status.md) and
logically upstream of it. If mental states are *not* substrate-independent, then no behavioural
parity argument can carry a machine to mental states at all. If they *are*,
substrate-independence is still only an enabling premise, not yet a verdict on any actual
system. Sub-issues that may deserve their own question nodes: whether phenomenal consciousness
is separable from functional organisation, and whether multiple-realizability entails
substrate-independence.

### In plain terms

Could a mind be made of something other than brain-stuff? That's the question. "Substrate" just
means what a thing is built from. The issue isn't whether a machine *acts* mind-like — it's whether
being a mind depends on the material at all, or only on the pattern of cause-and-effect that the
material happens to carry out. If minds do depend on the material, then no amount of acting human
ever gets a computer there. If they don't, the door is open — though actually showing that some
particular machine has a mind would still be a further step.
