When you introspect a current experience — notice a mild ache, attend to the colours in front of you
— what is the epistemic status of what introspection delivers? Is it a genuine *observation*, a
direct datum, or is it itself a *representation* of the experience, a constructed report that could be
mistaken? The question asks which, and a great deal in the philosophy of mind turns on the answer.

### Two answers

**Observation (the datum view).** Introspection is direct acquaintance with the experience itself — a
datum prior to, and independent of, any report. On this answer the experience is *observed* in the
strongest sense (compare [unobservability-in-principle](../concepts/unobservability-in-principle.md), absolute unobservability) by
exactly one observer per case: each subject possesses a single instance, known from the inside, and
the evidential standards built for never-observed theoretical posits do not govern the feel.

**Representation.** Introspection delivers a higher-order representation *of* the experience, of the
same broad kind as a report: fallible, constructed, possibly confabulated. On this answer the
first-person/third-person wall ([first-person-access](../concepts/first-person-access.md)) carries no special evidential cargo.
Introspection is one more report channel, and whatever discount rules govern outward reports (for
instance phenomenality-neutrality arguments) apply inward as well.

### Who answers which way

These are positions in the literature, not stances of any participant in this web. The datum answer
is taken by acquaintance theorists and phenomenal realists: Russell's knowledge by acquaintance
([russell-1911](../sources/russell-1911.md)), Searle's first-person ontology ([searle-1992](../sources/searle-1992.md)), and Chalmers on
direct phenomenal concepts ([chalmers-2003b](../sources/chalmers-2003b.md)). The representation answer is taken by
illusionists, heterophenomenologists, and attention-schema theorists: Dennett, treating introspection
as a theorist's-fiction report ([dennett-1991](../sources/dennett-1991.md)); Frankish, on the introspected feel as a
user-illusion ([frankish-2016](../sources/frankish-2016.md)); and Graziano, whose self-model "paints in" the glow of
awareness ([graziano-2013](../sources/graziano-2013.md)).

### Why it is a crux

Most evidential standards in the neighbouring debates presuppose an answer here. A
coherence-plus-usefulness standard for in-principle unobservables
([unobservables-judged-by-coherence-and-usefulness](../claims/unobservables-judged-by-coherence-and-usefulness.md)) reaches the feel only if introspective
deliverances are *not* observations; an appeal to acquaintance as the bedrock datum —
[privileged-first-person-access](../claims/privileged-first-person-access.md) read metaphysically — blocks that standard only if they
*are*. Hence the standoff on both sides of the AI hard-problem exchange: arguments from either
standard "relocate rather than refute" when aimed at an opponent who answers this question the other
way.

### A deeper sub-issue

By what *shared* standard could the question itself be settled? Each side's preferred instrument seems
to presuppose its own answer: applying the idleness-of-unobservables standard to the question
presupposes the representation answer, while citing acquaintance as evidence presupposes the datum
answer. Whether any neutral ground exists, or whether the question marks a genuine bedrock divergence,
is itself open — a sub-issue that may deserve its own node if pressed.

### In plain terms

Right now you're aware of your own experience — say, a mild ache, or the colors in front of you.
Question: is that awareness like *seeing* the experience itself, directly — the one thing in the world
you get without any middleman? Or is it more like reading a *report* your brain wrote about itself — a
summary that could be simplified, spun, or just wrong? It sounds like a small distinction, but huge
debates turn on it. If it's direct seeing, then every person has rock-solid front-row evidence that
feelings exist, evidence no outside test could ever replace. If it's a report, then your "inner eye"
deserves the same skepticism as any other testimony — including a chatbot's eloquent claims about its
inner life. And the question has a frustrating twist: each side's favorite method for settling debates
already assumes its own answer here, which is why this one keeps ending in a stare-down.
