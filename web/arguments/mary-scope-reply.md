Raises thought-experiment CQ6 (scope) — see the [thought-experiment pattern](../../patterns/thought-experiment.md)
— against the [knowledge argument](knowledge-argument.md). CQ6 grants the verdict and asks exactly
which theses the case threatens. Suppose the freed colour scientist does learn a truth not deducible
from the complete physical description. What follows is that *a-priori* (deducibility) physicalism —
the thesis that every truth is derivable from the physical account — is false. It does not follow that
the learned truth reports a *non-physical fact*: a truth's non-deducibility from physical premises no
more marks a further fact than the non-deducibility of an indexical ("I am here now") marks a further
fact. So the conclusion is consistent with an ontology in which the missing truth is a physical fact
grasped under a new mode of presentation. Horgan presses exactly this containment
([horgan-1984](../sources/horgan-1984.md)).

### The inference

The reply bounds rather than defeats. It accepts the argument's premises and its official conclusion,
[some truths about experience are not physically deducible](../claims/some-truths-about-experience-not-physically-deducible.md),
and denies only the slide from that epistemic gap to an ontological one. The wedge is the existence of
*a-posteriori necessities* ([some-necessities-knowable-only-a-posteriori](../claims/some-necessities-knowable-only-a-posteriori.md)):
if a phenomenal–physical identity can be necessary yet knowable only a posteriori, then a fact can be
physical while the truth stating it is undeducible from the physical description couched in other
concepts. The case then impugns the deducibility thesis, i.e. type-A physicalism, and leaves type-B
(a-posteriori, token-identity) physicalism untouched ([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md);
[type-b-materialism](../positions/type-b-materialism.md)).

### The weakest premise

The load-bearing move is the claim that the phenomenal case is relevantly like other a-posteriori
necessities — that "pain is such-and-such neural state" can be a strong a-posteriori identity in the
way "water is H₂O" is. This is contested by the two-dimensional rejoinder, on which phenomenal concepts
have coinciding primary and secondary intensions and so admit no appearance/reality wedge to exploit.
Whether the phenomenal is disanalogous to water is where the reply is pressed.

### Distinct from old-fact/new-guise

This is not the [old-fact/new-guise reply](old-fact-new-guise.md), which asserts that Mary re-cognises
one physical fact under a new (phenomenal) concept. The scope reply is weaker and prior: it need not
say *what* the learned truth is, only that non-deducibility bounds a deducibility thesis and does not
by itself deliver a non-physical fact. It marks the room a-posteriori physicalism occupies; the
old-fact/new-guise move is one way of furnishing that room.

### In plain terms

Grant that Mary, seeing red for the first time, learns something she couldn't have worked out from all
the physics. What exactly does that prove? Only that you can't *deduce* every truth from physical
descriptions — not that the new thing is spooky and non-physical. Compare "I am here now": you can't
squeeze that out of a complete physics-in-the-third-person either, but nobody thinks it names a ghostly
fact. Maybe Mary's new knowledge is an old physical fact met in a new way — something we had to
discover rather than derive. If so, the case sinks only the strong claim that everything is deducible
from physics, and the more modest physicalism, on which mind-brain identities are discovered rather
than deduced, sails on.
