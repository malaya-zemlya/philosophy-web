The **disappearing-agent objection** (Pereboom, [2014](../sources/pereboom-2014.md), ch. 2) is the
central contemporary attack on event-causal libertarianism — the version of
[libertarianism](../positions/libertarianism.md) on which a free decision is an undetermined event caused
by the agent's own mental states, with no exotic metaphysics added. The objection argues that this
picture buys indeterminism at the price of the agent: openness arrives, authorship does not.

**Inference form:** deductive.

1. On the event-causal picture, the agent's entire contribution to a decision consists of prior
   agent-involving events — her desires, beliefs, and deliberative states — which leave the outcome
   undetermined (the presupposed openness of
   [alternatives-genuinely-open](../claims/alternatives-genuinely-open.md)).
2. Given the complete prior state, either option can still occur; so no fact about the agent
   determines which option does.
3. An agent *settles* a decision only if some fact about her determines which option occurs; what
   settles nothing exercises no more control than a bystander to a coin toss.

∴ In an undetermined event-causal decision, nothing about the agent settles which option occurs
([agent-does-not-settle-undetermined-decisions](../claims/agent-does-not-settle-undetermined-decisions.md)) — the agent, as originator, disappears.

The objection generalizes the *luck objection* and squeezes the libertarian from the side opposite
the Consequence Argument: determinism would make the past settle the choice, but indeterminism, on
this picture, lets *nothing* settle it. The standard escapes each carry a price. Retreat to
[agent causation](../concepts/agent-causation.md) restores a settler by positing substance-causation —
and inherits that doctrine's coherence worries, including
[the certificate objection](agent-causation-certificate.md) to its armchair possibility.
Alternatively, accept the conclusion and give up libertarian freedom — Pereboom's own route to
hard incompatibilism ([hard-determinism](../positions/hard-determinism.md)).

Within the [consciousness–free-will duality](../concepts/consciousness-freewill-duality.md), this
objection is the arrow-reversed image of Kim's
[causal-exclusion argument](causal-exclusion.md)
([exclusion-dualizes-to-disappearing-agent](../claims/exclusion-dualizes-to-disappearing-agent.md)): exclusion makes consciousness all sink and no
source, the disappearing agent makes the would-be originator all source and no settler — prior
causes crowd out the agent just as physical causes crowd out qualia. Concretely, the arrows are
relations of causal settling among states: epiphenomenalism's shape is a node with only incoming
arrows, libertarian origination's a node with only outgoing ones — literal reversals of one
another — and each argument shows its shape unreachable. Exclusion finds every outgoing arrow of
the mental already drawn from its physical base; this objection finds no arrow from the agent that
settles what her prior states leave open.

**Weakest premise:** the settling requirement (3). Event-causal libertarians reply that it covertly
demands agent-causation — that "some fact about the agent determines the outcome" just *is*
substance-causal settling under another name — and that control consists in the decision's being
non-deviantly caused by the agent's own reasons-states, which premise 1 grants. Whether
reasons-responsive probabilistic causation amounts to settling, or merely to influence, is the live
crux.

### In plain terms

Some philosophers want free will to work like this: your reasons and desires push toward each
option without forcing either, and then — undetermined by anything — one option happens. The
objection asks: where are *you* in that final step? Everything about you already did its pushing
before the outcome; the last gap is filled by nothing at all. An unforced outcome that nothing
about you fixes isn't your act of settling; it's a coin toss inside your head. To really place the
chooser at the origin you'd need something stronger — like [the view that
persons themselves, not events, cause choices](../concepts/agent-causation.md) — or you give up on this kind of freedom.
