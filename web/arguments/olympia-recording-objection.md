The *recording objection* answers Maudlin's [olympia](olympia.md) by asking where Olympia came from.
Building her requires the trace log of the program π running on the input *r* — and a trace log can
only be produced by actually *performing* that computation. So every buildable Olympia has, in her
causal ancestry, a genuine, counterfactually-live run of π on *r*, and by the computationalist's own
sufficiency thesis that run may very well be a conscious episode. Maudlin's argument compares Klara
with Olympia as two free-standing systems and invites the verdict that the second, a mere recording,
is obviously not conscious. The objection charges that this comparison skips the process that made
Olympia. Once the recording run is on the ledger, no experience is missing from the story and
nothing miraculous is ever switched on or off; whether the replay *also* hosts an experience remains
an open question — it may or may not — and no answer to it embarrasses computationalism. In the
vocabulary of the thought-experiment pattern, the objection raises **CQ2 (verdict)** — the intuition
"the recording is not conscious" is either mundane and already honoured, or not robust at all when
the case is stated fairly — and **CQ6 (scope)**: whatever the case shows, it is not that
consciousness fails to be computational.

The move runs:

1. Olympia is assembled from the trace log Λ of π's run on *r*, and the log is obtainable only by
   performing the computation of π on *r* — on Klara, on a simulator, or by hand. A program's run
   is in general computationally irreducible: there is no shortcut to the values the log records
   (the *Effectiveness* corollary of [olympia-trace-specialization](olympia-trace-specialization.md)).
2. Any such trace-producing run is a genuine, counterfactually exercised run of π on *r*; by the
   computationalist's own *Sufficiency* thesis it supports the experience φ. So in every world
   containing an Olympia, φ has occurred.
3. The argument's absurdity-generators — "a recording obviously isn't conscious", "inert add-ons
   cannot summon experience", "rusting chains would make a toothache wink out" — all presuppose
   that the replay is the only candidate bearer of experience in the story, so that its status
   decides whether any experience exists at all. Premise 2 removes that presupposition: one cannot
   say Klara's run is conscious and Olympia's story contains no consciousness, because Olympia's
   story *includes* a run of the very kind Klara's is.
4. What remains open is only whether the replay hosts an *additional* experience — and about that,
   honestly posed, intuition is not decisive either way.

∴ The trilemma loses its force against computationalism: each of its horns, taken for replay runs
specifically, is a position a computationalist can occupy without absurdity, because the experience
the thought experiment trades on demonstrably occurred — at recording time.

### The recording run

Maudlin presents Olympia as if she were conjured whole; the formal reconstruction shows what her
construction consumes. The reordered troughs, the float presets, the placement of every blocked
copy — all of it is read off the log Λ, and Λ *is* the run. Whoever builds Olympia must first be,
or employ, a system that genuinely computes π on *r*, reading every value it acts on, its
counterfactual sensitivity exercised at every step. By Sufficiency, that is precisely the kind of
process the computationalist says supports φ. Nothing phenomenal is conjured from nothing, and
nothing phenomenal is missing from the world: at least one experience occurred, at recording; the
replay re-presents it.

Maudlin's own movie analogy, offered against computationalism, supports this reading when turned
around. A film is a film *of a real performance*. Nobody is puzzled that the scene genuinely
happened at filming, and nobody concludes from the projector's lack of experience that acting is
not a bodily process. The parallel diagnosis is familiar from the lookup-table literature: Block's
verdict on [lookup-table-mind](../concepts/lookup-table-mind.md) is that the machine has "the intelligence of a jukebox",
its apparent wit belonging to the programmers who anticipated the conversation ([block](../sources/block.md)).
The recording objection reads Olympia's apparent experience the same way — it belongs, at minimum,
to the recording run — with the difference that where Blockhead's table needed exponential
provenance, Olympia's script needed exactly one genuine run.

### Nothing winks in or out

The trilemma is formally sound: Sufficiency, Necessity, and Supervenience, applied to the replay,
cannot all stand, so the computationalist must still classify replay runs one way or another. The
objection's work is to show that *every* classification, made with the recording run in view, is
tolerable — the absurdity each horn was supposed to carry evaporates.

- *Grant the replay consciousness* (give up Necessity for replays). This no longer means crediting
  experience to "a trivial constant function": the replay is not an arbitrary trivial machine but a
  complete, step-for-step re-enactment nomologically downstream of a genuine φ-run. Granting such a
  process a derivative experience extends consciousness along a very narrow channel, not to every
  jukebox.
- *Deny the replay consciousness* (restrict Sufficiency to non-replay runs). This is now the
  mundane playback verdict — the tape is not the performance — and it carries no anti-computational
  moral, because the experience it declines to find in the replay is found where computationalism
  itself locates it: in the run that produced the script.
- *The addition, subtraction, and rust arguments.* Their sting — consciousness toggled by an inert
  block, a toothache winking out as a chain rusts through, with no physical event to mark it —
  presupposed that the toothache in question is the only one in the story, so that idle hardware
  holds a mind hostage. With the recording run on the ledger, what the inert machinery toggles is
  only whether the replay counts as an implementation, and hence whether a *second*, derivative
  experience-token is present. That question's presence conditions are exactly as sharp or as vague
  as implementation itself — no more mysterious than whether a rusted tape deck still counts as
  playing — and nothing whose existence matters hangs on it.

### The weakest premise

The objection removes the absurdity of the horns; it does not dissolve the trilemma, and its
load-bearing premise is step 4 — that intuition, honestly consulted, is genuinely undecided about
the replay. A defender of Maudlin will press from two sides. First, the supervenience argument
never claimed to need intuitions about replays as such; it needs only that physically
near-identical processes match in experience, and the objection must still say *which* way they
match — the openness of step 4 must survive Maudlin's insistence that some answer be given.
Second, the palliative work is done by the replay's *descent* from a genuine run, and descent is a
historical property: if it is allowed to soften verdicts about the replay, the view drifts toward a
provenance criterion of experience, and provenance criteria are unstable. A trace log assembled by
sheer luck (a Boltzmann-Olympia), or copied from *another Olympia's replay* — once one Olympia
exists, unlimited replays-of-replays descend from a single root episode — yields machines
physically identical to the original Olympia but with thinned or absent ancestry; if identical
replays get different verdicts by history alone, experience at a time has been made to depend on
events outside that time, which is a supervenience violation of exactly the kind the objection
elsewhere respects. The stable reading keeps the history in its evidential role — the recording run
proves an experience occurred and thereby disarms the "no consciousness anywhere" rhetoric —
without letting history itself decide what the replay feels.

### In plain terms

Someone records an interview with a human being. Later they play the recording back, with the
interviewer repeating the same questions live — and the answers come out right, on cue, as if a
conversation were happening. Then they complain: *this recording is not conscious!* Well — of
course the complaint feels right, and of course it proves nothing: the human was conscious, during
the interview, and nobody concludes from a tape's blankness that consciousness has nothing to do
with brains. To rebuild Maudlin's machine ([olympia](olympia.md)) in full, add a monitor silently
comparing the interviewer's live questions against the script, and the original human on standby,
ready to take over the moment the interviewer ad-libs. Maudlin's definitions then count this rig as
*having the conversation*, and its blankness becomes his paradox. The recording objection says the
paradox is bookkeeping: you cannot tally the experiences in this story while skipping the interview
itself. The experience happened; the tape re-presents it; and the leftover question — does the
playback *also* feel something? — is one nobody has a firm answer to, and nothing rides on it.

One honest complication keeps the objection honest: a tape replays only the human's *outputs* —
sound. Olympia replays the computer's entire *internal* activity, every memory operation, step for
step. The fair analogy would replay the interview into a duplicate brain, every recorded neuron
firing triggered by electrodes on schedule, the brain's own machinery bypassed but standing by.
Whether *that* replayed brain feels anything is genuinely unobvious — it may or may not — and that
is exactly the objection's point: the confident verdict Maudlin needs is available only for the
tape-recorder version of the case, where it is mundane and already accounted for; for the fair
version there is no confident verdict to build a refutation on.
