The prudential argument for caution about machine minds is sometimes pushed one step further than
it can go — into a defence of *biology* as the mark that confers moral standing. This entry grants
the cautious reasoning but blocks that final step. What prudence actually selects, it argues, is
*unforgeability*: whatever sign is hardest to counterfeit. Biology earns its standing only by
being, for the moment, that sign, so the conclusion should read "track the unforgeable," not
"track flesh."

### The inference

The form is *undercutting by overreach* — grant the premises, then deny that they reach the
conclusion drawn from them. The target is the identification, in
[prudence-favors-flesh-criterion](../claims/prudence-favors-flesh-criterion.md), of the prudent criterion with biology in particular.

1. Biology's resistance to counterfeit is a *contingent, time-indexed* empirical fact — the
   "currently" in [biology-currently-unforgeable](../claims/biology-currently-unforgeable.md) — and not a necessary feature of minds.
   This contingency is precisely why the argument it serves is prudential rather than metaphysical.

∴ The property doing the prudential work is *unforgeability as such*
([prudence-tracks-unforgeability-not-flesh](../claims/prudence-tracks-unforgeability-not-flesh.md)). Biology has no privileged role beyond
contingently being the unforgeable sign; a non-biological sign that became at least as hard to
fake would satisfy the very same rationale and displace flesh. So prudence does not favour resting
moral standing on biology in particular — only on whatever is hardest to counterfeit — and the
flesh criterion overreaches in writing *flesh* into its conclusion.

### What the argument leaves standing

The objection is deliberately narrow. It leaves intact the *caution-under-uncertainty* core of the
criterion it attacks: a cheaply forgeable sign is a poor thing to rest standing on, the dynamic
captured by [criterion-gaming](../concepts/criterion-gaming.md). It contests only the slide from "unforgeable" to
"flesh." A consequence is that it also blocks any reading of the flesh criterion as support for
[proteocentrism](../positions/proteocentrism.md) on more than defeasible, contingent grounds: prudence lends carbon no
metaphysical privilege.

### Weakest premise

The load-bearing step is the tacit assumption that a non-biological sign *could in fact* reach
biology's standard of unforgeability. If biology were not merely currently but *necessarily* the
only unforgeable mark of a mind, then "track unforgeability" and "track flesh" could never come
apart, and the objection would dissolve. A defender of the flesh criterion presses exactly there.

### In plain terms

The cautious "demand the hard-to-fake mark" argument is sound as far as it goes — but it doesn't
earn the jump to *biology*. Biology only counts because, right now, it happens to be hard to
counterfeit; that's a fact about today's technology, not a law. So what the argument really backs
is "rest standing on whatever is hardest to fake," and if a non-biological sign ever got that
forgery-proof, the same caution would favour it. Resting the rule on *flesh* specifically claims
more than the prudence licenses.
