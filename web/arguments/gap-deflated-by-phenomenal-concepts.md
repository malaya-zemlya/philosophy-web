This is the strong-functionalist reply to the [explanatory-gap
argument](explanatory-gap.md). Where that argument treats the lingering "but is anything actually *felt*?" question
as evidence that feeling outruns function, this reply argues the reverse: strong functionalism
positively *predicts* that the question will linger, so the gap is no evidence against it. It then
adds an independent reason to think feeling cannot float free of function.

The argument is two-pronged. *Prong A* is a likelihood-defeater — it attacks the probabilistic
premise of the explanatory-gap argument. *Prong B* is a reductio that gives positive abductive
support for supervenience.

### Prong A — deflating the evidence

The explanatory-gap argument rests on $P(E\mid \text{H1})$ being low, where E is the
post-theoretic intelligibility of "but is there anything it is like to be *that*?" and H1 is
strong [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md). The *phenomenal-concept strategy*
([loar-1990](../sources/loar-1990.md)) denies that it is low. On that strategy a creature grasps one and the same
state under two cognitively isolated guises — a third-person theoretical or *role* concept and a
first-person demonstrative or *phenomenal* concept — and the two concepts share no inferential
corridor down which an analysis could travel.

1. If H1 is true, beings with isolated phenomenal concepts would *still* feel the gap, because the
   felt gap tracks concept architecture, not metaphysical structure.
2. So $P(E\mid \text{H1})$ is high, not low.

∴ The inequality $P(E\mid \text{H2}) > P(E\mid \text{H1})$ that the explanatory-gap argument needs
fails to obtain; E does not confirm the [residue](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md) over functionalism.
(Compare a lingering "but are these *really* the same number?" about an unproven-but-necessary
identity: that is evidence about the thinker's epistemic position, not about the numbers.)

### Prong B — fading and dancing qualia

A positive reason for supervenience that does not run through conceivability, owed to Chalmers
([chalmers-1996](../sources/chalmers-1996.md)) but pressed here in the service of functionalism rather than against it.

3. Replace a conscious brain's neurons one by one with functional silicon duplicates, preserving
   every counterfactual input-output relation among components.
4. If phenomenal character does *not* supervene on functional organisation (H2, the
   [phenomenal residue](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md)), qualia must fade or invert somewhere along the
   series.
5. By construction the functional profile — including all introspective judgements, reports, and
   dispositions to notice change — is held fixed throughout.
6. So H2 commits us to a subject whose experience drains away or flips while every belief about its
   own experience stays put: introspection decoupled from experience by metaphysical fiat, with no
   fact that could ever reconcile them.

∴ H2 entails systematically deceived introspectors as a matter of course, so supervenience of the
phenomenal on organisational role is the more credible option. This directly answers the residue
theorist's demand for an argument against the zombie that is not a mere redescription of it, and so
it supports [substrate-independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md).

### The weakest premise

Prong B's premise (4): that the only options under failed supervenience are fade or invert. A
residue theorist might seek a story in which experience and introspective judgement come apart
*coherently*; if such a story can be told without incoherence, the reductio loses its sting. Prong
A's exposure is different — it stands or falls with the phenomenal-concept strategy itself, so if
some E can be identified that isolated-concept creatures should *not* expect, the deflation fails.

### Relation to the continuity schema

Prong B is the gradual-neural-prosthesis instance of the [continuity argument schema](../concepts/continuity-argument-schema.md) (with X = neuron-by-neuron carbon→silicon replacement and Y =
{same qualia, faded or inverted}). It argues for the *f*-constant horn: premises (4)–(5) deny that
any admissible one-neuron step can flip or fade qualia without the absurd consequence in (6), so
the verdict is constant across the whole path and supervenience holds. The
[dancing-qualia circularity objection](dancing-qualia-begs-the-question.md) contests
precisely whether this has *closed* the discontinuity and disconnection horns or merely assumed
them shut.

This is distinct from the [behavioural-parity argument](behavioral-parity.md), which argues
*for* mental status from behaviour; here the claim is that a specific anti-functionalist
evidential argument misprices its evidence and that supervenience holds. It is also distinct from
the [grain dilemma](role-grain-dilemma.md)'s reply ([role-grain-is-organisational](../claims/role-grain-is-organisational.md)):
this concerns the phenomenal residue while granting a fixed role, whereas that concerns the
individuation of role itself.

### In plain terms

This is the "feeling really is just function" side answering the explanatory-gap argument — the
charge that a leftover "but is anything actually felt?" question shows feeling is something extra
([explanatory-gap](explanatory-gap.md)). It replies in two moves.

**Move A — explain the gap away.** The other side said: the fact that "but is anything actually
*felt*?" still sounds like a live question, even after the complete scientific story, is evidence
that feeling is something extra. Not so, says this argument. We think about our own experiences with
two completely separate mental "handles" — a from-the-outside, scientific one and a from-the-inside,
"*this!*" one — and those two handles just aren't wired to talk to each other. So "are these two the
same thing?" will *always* feel like an open question, whatever the truth is. The lingering question
shows how our concepts are built, not that there's an extra ingredient in the world.

**Move B — a positive reason feeling can't float free of function.** Imagine swapping someone's
neurons one at a time for chips that preserve every functional connection — including every judgment
they make about their own experience. If feeling *weren't* fixed by function, then somewhere along
the way their inner experience would fade or flip — yet, since we kept all their self-reports
identical, they'd keep sincerely insisting "nothing has changed." That hands you a person
systematically wrong about their own experience, with no possible fact to set them straight — which
seems absurd. Better to conclude feeling tracks function after all. (Weak spot of Move B: it assumes
the only options are "fade" or "flip" — and a critic argues it quietly assumes that *noticing your
own experience* is itself just a function, which is the very thing in dispute.)
