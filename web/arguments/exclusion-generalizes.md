The **generalization objection** (Ned Block, [2003](../sources/block-2003.md)) turns the
[causal exclusion argument](causal-exclusion.md) against itself: nothing in the exclusion
reasoning is specific to the mind, so if it works at all, it works everywhere.

**Inference form:** deductive (a reductio of the exclusion principle's unrestricted use).

1. The exclusion argument holds that when a property supervenes on a lower-level base, the base's
   causal sufficiency pre-empts the supervening property: the higher-level property is excluded.
2. The mental supervenes on the neurobiological in just the way the neurobiological supervenes on
   the chemical, the chemical on the atomic, and the atomic on whatever physics finds beneath it.
   The exclusion reasoning applies at each step with equal force.
3. So if exclusion is sound, causal power drains downward without stopping: neural, chemical, and
   biological properties are excluded by *their* bases just as the mental is excluded by its —
   synapses no more cause than decisions do.
4. Worse, physics itself may have no fundamental level (each theory's entities so far having
   dissolved into a deeper layer); if so, the causal power drains away entirely and *nothing*
   causes anything.
5. Since chemistry, biology, and neuroscience manifestly traffic in genuine causes — and since
   "nothing causes anything" is absurd — the exclusion principle must be wrong about what
   supervenience does to higher-level causation.

∴ The exclusion argument overreaches, and higher-level causes — mental ones included — can be
genuine ([mental-causes-are-difference-makers](../claims/mental-causes-are-difference-makers.md)).

**Weakest premise:** step 2, the claim of *no relevant disanalogy*. Kim's reply is that genuine
higher-level properties are either functionalizable (and then their causal powers are inherited,
not excluded) or micro-based structural properties that exist at the lower level itself, so the
drainage stops wherever reduction succeeds; the exclusion argument was only ever aimed at
properties held to be *irreducible*. The rejoinder then disputes whether chemistry and biology are
"reducible" in any sense that mentality is not.

### In plain terms

A famous argument (the [exclusion argument](causal-exclusion.md)) says: your brain chemistry
already fully explains your arm going up, so your *decision* — if it's something over and above the
chemistry — has no work left to do. This reply says: careful, that knife cuts everything. The
chemistry is likewise "already fully explained" by atomic physics, and the atomic by something
deeper. If "the lower level does all the work" ever kills a cause, it kills chemistry's and
biology's causes too, all the way down — and if there is no bottom floor, causation vanishes from
the world altogether. Since that's absurd, the knife must be blunt: sitting on top of lower-level
machinery doesn't stop something from being a real cause.
