The **fission argument** is the classic internal objection to the
[psychological continuity theory](../positions/psychological-continuity-theory.md), pressed to its
sharpest form by Parfit ([1984](../sources/parfit-1984.md)).

**Inference form:** counterexample (thought experiment) against a sufficiency thesis.

1. **Scenario.** A person's brain is divided and each hemisphere transplanted into a separate
   body; each resulting person is fully psychologically continuous with the original — same
   memories, character, intentions. (Each step is medically conceivable: people survive with one
   hemisphere, and hemispheres can in principle be separated.)
2. **Intuition.** Both successors stand to the original exactly as an ordinary survivor stands to
   their yesterday-self; there is nothing to choose between them.
3. **Bridge.** Identity is a one-one relation: the original cannot be identical to *both*
   successors (they are two, and identity is transitive), and identifying him with either one
   arbitrarily is untenable given their perfect symmetry.
4. ∴ Psychological continuity, which here holds one-to-many, cannot *be* personal identity
   ([identity-is-psychological-continuity](../claims/identity-is-psychological-continuity.md) fails as it stands).

The neo-Lockean's standard repair is a **no-branching clause**: continuity constitutes identity
only when it holds uniquely. But this makes whether *you* survive depend on what happens to someone
else — "how can a double success be a failure?" — since a single-hemisphere transplant would
preserve you, and the *other* hemisphere's survival, an event elsewhere, would then annihilate you.
Parfit's own conclusion takes the case the further step: since each fission product gives the
original everything that ordinary survival gives, and yet identity fails, identity was never the
carrier of prudential concern — [identity-not-what-matters](../claims/identity-not-what-matters.md). The web's record of *actual*
boundary-shifting cases — split brains, craniopagus twins
([subjects-actually-merge-and-divide](../claims/subjects-actually-merge-and-divide.md)) — keeps the scenario from being dismissed as idle
fantasy.

**Weakest premise:** the bridge (3), for those willing to bite bullets: a four-dimensionalist can
say there were *two* persons all along, sharing a body until the split, so identity's one-one logic
is preserved and the case shows nothing about continuity. The intuition (2) is also pressed by
[further-fact theorists](../claims/identity-is-further-fact.md), for whom exactly one successor (or
neither) is the original, symmetry notwithstanding — we just cannot know which.

### In plain terms

Split a brain in half and give each half a new body; suppose each wakes up with all the original
person's memories and personality. Each is connected to the original exactly the way you're
connected to yourself of yesterday. But they can't *both* be him — there are two of them. And
picking one over the other is absurd — they're perfectly symmetrical. So the mental-thread theory
of identity ([identity-is-psychological-continuity](../claims/identity-is-psychological-continuity.md)) breaks: the thread can split, but
identity can't. You can patch the theory with fine print ("the thread counts only if it doesn't
split") — but then surviving would depend on what happens to the *other* half of your brain, far
away, which seems crazy. Parfit's verdict: stop asking "which one is really him?" — both
continuations give him everything survival ever gave anyone
([identity-not-what-matters](../claims/identity-not-what-matters.md)).
