Raises consequences CQ4 (value) — see the [consequences pattern](../../patterns/consequences.md) —
against the [forgeable rights-sign argument](rights-sign-forgeable.md) at its self-flagged weakest
premise, [that a forgeable sign is unfit as the criterion](../claims/forgeable-sign-unfit-as-criterion.md).
CQ4 asks whether the good the argument pursues is the *relevant* good. The premise treats
resistance-to-forgery as the property that qualifies a criterion for moral standing. But
unforgeability is a value about *epistemic security against deception* — how hard the mark is to
counterfeit — and that is not the same as *tracking suffering*, the property that actually grounds
standing. Selecting the criterion that is hardest to fake can therefore enshrine a morally
irrelevant feature (biological substrate) precisely at the expense of the feature that matters.

### The inference

The move is a value-critique internal to the target's own argument-from-consequences. Grant that a
public behavioural criterion will be gamed; it does not follow that the *disqualifying* defect of a
gamed sign is its forgeability as such. What a rights criterion must do is track the presence of a
subject who can be wronged. Forgeability bears on that only instrumentally — a sign that is trivially
faked stops correlating with the state — but "hardest to fake" is not "best correlated with
suffering": a mark can be uncounterfeitable and yet morally beside the point. Birch's framework fixes
the operative value as minimising the moral costs of *mis*-assigning sentience in both directions
([birch-2024](../sources/birch-2024.md)); against that value, "prefer the unforgeable sign"
substitutes a proxy for the thing itself.

### The weakest premise

The objection's own soft point is that unforgeability and suffering-tracking may not come apart in
practice: if, as things stand, biology is both the hardest mark to fake *and* the best available
correlate of sentience, the proxy and the value coincide and the criticism is idle. The reply is that
the target argument's own concession — that a forgeable sign (fingerprints) can remain useful
evidence — already admits the two can separate, so the disqualification cannot rest on forgeability
alone.

### Distinct from the base-rate reply

The [claim it attacks](../claims/forgeable-sign-unfit-as-criterion.md) already records a
false-positive/base-rate reply: that forgeability does not make a sign worthless evidence, as forgeable
fingerprints remain useful. This objection is different. It does not dispute the *evidential* weight
of a forgeable sign; it disputes that *unforgeability* is the value a rights criterion should be
selected to maximise in the first place.

### In plain terms

The argument says a rights test has to be hard to fake, or people will cheat it. Fair enough that
cheating is bad — but "hard to fake" isn't the same as "actually tracks who can suffer." A test can be
impossible to forge and still measure the wrong thing. Picking biology because it's the toughest mark
to counterfeit is a bit like choosing a lock because it's pick-proof without asking whether it's on
the right door: security against fakers is a real value, but it isn't the value — protecting beings
who can be hurt — that moral standing is supposed to be about.
