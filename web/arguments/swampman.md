**Swampman** is the standard counterexample to history-based theories of content. The case is
Donald Davidson's ([1987](../sources/davidson-1987.md)): lightning strikes a dead tree in a swamp and, by
sheer chemical accident, assembles a molecule-for-molecule duplicate of a man at the instant the
original is vaporized. Swampman walks home, greets the original's friends by name, and writes
articles indistinguishable from the original's. Deployed against
[teleosemantics](../positions/teleosemantics.md), the case runs on the thought-experiment scheme:

1. **Scenario.** Swampman is a physical duplicate of a thinking person, but has *no* history — no
   evolution, no learning, no selection; none of his mechanisms has ever had a biological function.
2. **Intuition.** Swampman thinks and means things: when he says "I'll take the usual", something
   is going on in him with content, exactly as in the original.
3. **Bridge.** If content is fixed by the biological function of producing and consuming
   mechanisms ([intentional-content-from-biological-function](../claims/intentional-content-from-biological-function.md)), Swampman's states have no
   content at all — his mechanisms have no functions.
4. ∴ Content is not fixed by biological function; whatever fixes it must be available to a
   creature's *current* constitution, not only its lineage.

**Inference form:** counterexample (defeasible), against a sufficiency-and-necessity thesis about
content.

**Weakest premise:** the intuition (2) — thought-experiment CQ2 (verdict). Davidson himself, and
Millikan following him, simply deny it: on their view Swampman *refers to nothing* until he has
accumulated the right history, and the contrary intuition is a prejudice in favour of the inner
feel of thought over its worldly credentials. The objector's rejoinder is that biting this bullet
costs the theory its data: a theory of meaning on which a perfect functional duplicate of a
philosopher means nothing has explained meaning away. There is also a CQ1 (coherence) worry —
whether a chance assembly of a person is even nomologically possible — usually waived, since the
case needs only metaphysical possibility.

### In plain terms

Imagine lightning freakishly assembles an exact atom-for-atom copy of a person in a swamp — same
brain, same dispositions, but zero history: no childhood, no evolution, nothing it was ever "built
for." The copy chats, plans, and reminisces (falsely, but convincingly). The
["meaning comes from evolution" theory](../positions/teleosemantics.md) has to say the copy's brain
states mean *nothing* — no history, no biological job, no content. To many that verdict is absurd:
whatever the copy is doing, it certainly seems to be thinking. So, the argument concludes, meaning
can't depend on history alone. Defenders of the theory reply by accepting the strange verdict:
the swamp-copy really doesn't mean anything — yet.
