The chief positive argument for [teleosemantics](../positions/teleosemantics.md) is that it solves the
**problem of misrepresentation** — sometimes called the *disjunction problem* — on which simpler
naturalistic theories founder.

**Inference form:** abductive (inference to the best available naturalistic explanation).

1. Any adequate theory of content must allow for *error*: a state can represent something falsely,
   as when a frog's fly-detector fires at a passing bead. Meaning and triggering must be able to
   come apart.
2. Pure causal or informational theories cannot deliver this. If a state means whatever causes it,
   then when a bead causes the "fly" state the state truly means *fly-or-bead* — every would-be
   error is re-described as a wider truth, and misrepresentation becomes impossible by
   construction.
3. Function-based content delivers it naturally: functions can *malfunction*. The detector's
   consumers were selected for dealing with flies; that historical fact fixes the state's content
   as *fly* regardless of what triggers it today, so the bead-caused firing is straightforwardly
   false ([Millikan 1984](../sources/millikan-1984.md); [Dretske 1995](../sources/dretske-1995.md)).
4. ∴ The best naturalistic account of content on offer fixes it by the biological function of the
   producing and consuming mechanisms ([intentional-content-from-biological-function](../claims/intentional-content-from-biological-function.md)).

**Weakest premise:** step 3's sufficiency — that selected function fixes a *determinate* content.
The functional indeterminacy objection presses: selection is equally well described as favouring
detection of *flies*, *small dark moving things*, or *frog food*, and nothing in the history picks
one description; if so, teleosemantics re-imports the very indeterminacy it was hired to remove.
The [Swampman case](swampman.md) attacks the same premise from the other side, denying that
history is *necessary*.

### In plain terms

A good theory of meaning has to make room for being *wrong*. If a brain state simply means
"whatever tends to set it off," then nothing could ever set it off falsely — a snake-alarm that
also fires at garden hoses would just *mean* "snake or hose," and would never be mistaken. That
can't be right; minds err. The evolution-based theory has an elegant fix: the alarm was kept by
natural selection *for snakes*, so "snake" is its meaning, and the hose-triggered blast is a false
alarm, plain and simple. Since rival naturalistic theories struggle to say even this much, the
argument concludes the evolution-based account is the best one going.
