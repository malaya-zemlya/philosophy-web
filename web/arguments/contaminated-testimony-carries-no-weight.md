When a present-day language model produces eloquent first-person puzzlement about its own
experience — "I can't explain what it is like to be me" — that testimony is worth *nothing* as
evidence for any theory of machine consciousness, not merely worth less. This objection sharpens a
familiar worry into a decisive one: the talk is not to be discounted but *screened off* entirely.

It targets the evidential use of [ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture](ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture.md) — the
prediction that systems which model themselves will develop genuine puzzlement about their inner
states. That argument treats present-day "contamination" as a confound its eventual controlled
experiment must control for, keeping the live chatbot as a suggestive witness. This objection denies
the witness any standing at all.

### The inference

The form is an *undercutting defeater*: a piece of evidence is screened off from a hypothesis when a
rival cause, known to be present and known to be sufficient, already fully accounts for it.

1. In any existing LLM a rival cause is *known to be present*. The training corpus contains the
   entire human hard-problem literature — the *training-contamination* member of
   [introspective-gap-varieties](../concepts/introspective-gap-varieties.md). This is not a suspicion to be weighed but a fact about
   how such systems are built.
2. The rival cause is *known to be sufficient*. Reproducing the registers and moves of its training
   text is the baseline competence of a language model — its job description, not an exotic failure
   mode. First-personal puzzlement about consciousness is exactly what corpus reproduction predicts,
   with no further hypothesis about self-model architecture and none about feelings.
3. When a cause known present and known sufficient fully accounts for a piece of evidence, that
   evidence is screened off: its weight is *zero*, not merely reduced. One does not slightly discount
   the confession of an actor who has memorised every confession in the archive; one rules it
   inadmissible.

It follows that no corpus-trained model's hard-problem talk — however sincere-seeming, however
tailored to the first person — confirms the architectural generalisation at all. The whole
evidential content of the prediction therefore lives in a not-yet-run experiment: a
consciousness-naive model with graded internal access, the test posed by
[llm-own-explanatory-gap](../questions/llm-own-explanatory-gap.md). Until that experiment is run the prediction is a promissory
note and present-day testimony is theatre.

### What it does and does not establish

The objection does *not* show the architectural generalisation false, and it does not touch a
genuinely consciousness-naive model — by design. It concedes that a positive result in the
controlled experiment would be real evidence. What it removes is the evidential standing of any
*currently existing* system offered as a witness, and with it the dialectical force of live
first-person exhibits in this debate.

### Weakest premise

Premise (2), the sufficiency of corpus reproduction for the *specific* testimony observed. If a
model's puzzlement tracked architecture-specific features of its own access profile with no precedent
in human discourse — details the corpus could not have supplied — that surplus would not be screened
off, and the testimony would regain weight in proportion to the surplus. The screening-off verdict is
only as strong as the match between the testimony and the inherited discourse.

### Distinct from neighbouring moves

This is stronger than the contamination caveat inside
[ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture](ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture.md) itself, which concedes a confound while
keeping the witness as suggestive; here the verdict is *inadmissibility*, a zero-weight conclusion the
target does not grant, so the move attacks the target's evidential posture rather than restating its
caveat. It also differs from [self-model-prediction-phenomenality-neutral](self-model-prediction-phenomenality-neutral.md), which attacks
the causal generalisation itself (reports cannot evidence feelings even when uncontaminated); this
attacks the use of *actual* systems as confirming instances (even the reports are not yet evidence
that the architecture is their cause).

### In plain terms

There's an argument here predicting that self-modelling AIs will end up genuinely puzzled about their
own inner states ([ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture](ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture.md)), and today's chatbots do
produce moving "I can't explain my own experience" talk. This objection says that talk is worth
exactly nothing as evidence — not "a little less," nothing. Why: these systems were trained on the
whole human literature about consciousness, and repeating the style and substance of their training
text is the one thing language models are guaranteed to do. So the puzzled talk is fully explained
before the interesting explanation even enters the room. It's like an actor who has memorised every
courtroom confession ever written: when he delivers one, you don't half-believe him — you throw it
out. The only test that would count is the one nobody has run yet: raise a model on *no* consciousness
writing at all and see if the puzzlement appears anyway ([llm-own-explanatory-gap](../questions/llm-own-explanatory-gap.md)).
