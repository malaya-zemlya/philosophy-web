Derek Parfit's rewiring argument applies the [continuity-argument-schema](../concepts/continuity-argument-schema.md) to personal
identity and, unlike the bare paradox, selects a definite horn ([parfit-1984](../sources/parfit-1984.md)). Imagine a
connected spectrum of cases that gradually transforms one person's psychology into another's —
rewiring a mind, one connection at a time, into (say) a famous stranger's — with each case differing
from its neighbour by a single edit. At one end the subject is determinately themselves; at the
other, determinately someone else. Parfit's conclusion is that identity must come in *degrees*.

### The reasoning

Model the spectrum as $f : X \to Y$, where $X$ is the connected path of psychological configurations
under one-connection edits and $Y$ = {me, not-me}.

1. At the start of the series the subject is determinately themselves; at the far end, determinately
   someone else — so the endpoints differ ($f$ is not constant).
2. No single one-connection edit is the step at which they stop being themselves.
   ([the tolerance premise](../claims/psychological-rewiring-tolerance.md) — the verdict makes no jump
   across an admissible step.)
3. The series is a connected path of such edits ($X$ is connected).

∴ A discrete, bivalent identity-verdict cannot be maintained. By the schema the verdict must instead
come in degrees: [personal identity admits of degree](../claims/personal-identity-admits-of-degree.md).

### Which horn, and where it is pressed

The argument takes the *non-discrete-$Y$* (degree) horn explicitly — in contrast with the
horn-neutral [sorites paradox](sorites-paradox.md), which only proves that some horn must
give. Parfit's diagnosis is neither a hidden sharp boundary (the discontinuity horn) nor a mere
truth-value gap (the indeterminacy horn) but that identity genuinely holds to a degree.

The weakest point is premise 2 read as forcing *degree* specifically. A discontinuity-horn theorist
denies premise 2 outright (some edit is the breaking point); an indeterminacy-horn theorist accepts
it but reads the middle as gappy rather than graded. The argument's selection of the degree horn over
those rivals is where it is contested.

### In plain terms

Imagine slowly rewiring your mind, one tiny change at a time, until your psychology matches someone
else's — say, a famous stranger's. At the start it's clearly you; at the far end it's clearly not.
But no single tiny tweak is *the* moment you stopped being yourself. Sound familiar? It's the heap
paradox again, with "me / not-me" standing in for "heap / not-heap." Parfit's move is to take one of
the exits: personal identity isn't a hard yes-or-no, it's a matter of *degree* — you can be partly
the same person, more or less continuous with who you were, rather than flatly the same or flatly a
different person. (Other thinkers grab different exits — a hidden exact cutoff, or a stretch where
there's just no fact of the matter — which is where this argument gets contested.)
