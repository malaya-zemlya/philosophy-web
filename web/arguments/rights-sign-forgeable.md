This is a *prudential* argument — a recommendation about policy under uncertainty, explicitly
"caution, not metaphysics." It does not try to settle what an AI is on the inside. It argues
that, given what we can and cannot reliably detect, the safe place to rest moral standing is on a
mark that cannot yet be faked.

### The inference

The form is practical rather than deductive, with an abductive core about what a gameable sign
can be taken to indicate.

1. *[Any rights-granting behavioural sign will be gamed](../claims/rights-criterion-will-be-gamed.md).*
   Once a behavioural sign is made the public criterion for rights, it will be optimized against
   until it no longer tracks suffering ([criterion-gaming](../concepts/criterion-gaming.md); [goodhart](../sources/goodhart.md),
   [specification-gaming](../sources/specification-gaming.md), [gambarian](../sources/gambarian.md) §2).
2. *[A cheaply forgeable sign is unfit as the
   criterion](../claims/forgeable-sign-unfit-as-criterion.md).* A sign that is cheap to forge cannot bear the weight of moral standing as the
   criterion for it.
3. *[Biology cannot currently be counterfeited to that
   standard](../claims/biology-currently-unforgeable.md).*

Therefore ([prudence favours the flesh criterion](../claims/prudence-favors-flesh-criterion.md)) the
cautious policy is to rest moral standing on currently-unforgeable biology and to place the
burden of proof on the non-biological mind.

### What it attacks

The argument bears against [the behavioural-parity argument](behavioral-parity.md) at that
argument's evidential step. If a behavioural profile is being optimized against, then displaying
the profile is no longer *best explained* by the system's actually realizing the underlying
mental role. Gaming corrodes precisely the abductive link — from behaviour to inner state — on
which parity reasoning depends.

### Weakest premise

The soft point is premise 2, [that a forgeable sign is
disqualified](../claims/forgeable-sign-unfit-as-criterion.md). A false-positive / base-rate reply concedes the sign can be forged yet denies
that forgeability makes it worthless evidence: fingerprints can be forged and remain useful. So
the step from "gameable" to "disqualified as a criterion" is where the argument is most exposed.

### A recorded limitation

The premises favour *unforgeability*, not *flesh* as such. Biology wins only contingently,
because premise 3 is time-indexed to what can be counterfeited now. The argument therefore does
not establish [proteocentrism](../positions/proteocentrism.md): a future unforgeable *non*-biological sign would
displace flesh without disturbing the reasoning. This is why its conclusion is held as contested.
The companion move that would close the gap — "unforgeability is not flesh" — is taken up in
[unforgeability-is-not-flesh](unforgeability-is-not-flesh.md).

### In plain terms

This is a caution argument, not a claim about what machines really are inside. Suppose we picked
some outward sign — "it acts like it's suffering" — as the official test for granting AIs rights.
The moment that sign becomes the rule, there's a reward for producing it, so it gets gamed and
faked until it no longer reliably means real suffering. A sign that cheap to fake, the argument
says, can't safely be the thing we hang moral and legal standing on. Biology, by contrast, can't
yet be counterfeited to that standard. So as a careful *policy under uncertainty*, play it safe:
rest rights on the hard-to-fake biological mark, and put the burden on the non-biological case to
prove itself. Two honest caveats are built in: "hard to fake" isn't the same as "must be flesh" —
a future unforgeable *non*-biological sign would do the job too — and critics note that plenty of
forgeable signs (fingerprints, say) are still useful evidence rather than worthless.
