The *continuity argument* is a second route to [panpsychism](../concepts/panpsychism.md), pressed by Thomas
Nagel ([1979](../sources/nagel-1979.md)). Where the [genetic argument](genetic-panpsychism.md) reasons
from the impossibility of brute emergence, this one reasons from the absence of any principled boundary:
somewhere between a rock and a human being, consciousness seems to appear, yet there is no non-arbitrary
place to draw the line. Rather than draw one anyway, the panpsychist lets experience reach all the way
down.

### The argument

The inference is abductive and eliminative — an inference to the most principled of the surviving
options, not a deductive proof.

1. ([no-nonarbitrary-threshold-for-experience](../claims/no-nonarbitrary-threshold-for-experience.md)) If experience were confined to brains, or to
   life, or to systems above some complexity threshold, there should be a principled place where it
   first switches on; but no such non-arbitrary line is in view — the relevant differences across the
   supposed boundary look like differences of degree and organisation.
2. Drawing the line anyway, at an arbitrary point, is unmotivated; and denying that experience is real
   is ruled out ([phenomenal-consciousness-is-real](../claims/phenomenal-consciousness-is-real.md)).
3. The remaining option that avoids an arbitrary threshold is that experience, in some attenuated form,
   is present all the way down.

∴ This *supports* [consciousness-fundamental-ubiquitous](../claims/consciousness-fundamental-ubiquitous.md): the line-free, parsimonious position is
that experience pervades nature rather than appearing abruptly at one favoured level.

### The weak point

The argument leans on premise (1). An opponent can hold that there *is* a principled threshold — a level
of integrated information, say, or of functional organisation, at which experience genuinely (if
strongly) emerges ([strong-emergence-is-acceptable](../claims/strong-emergence-is-acceptable.md)) — in which case the "no non-arbitrary line"
premise is simply false. The argument is also only abductive: it shows that panpsychism avoids an awkward
commitment, not that its rivals are incoherent, so it carries less weight than the genetic argument aims
to.

### In plain terms

Somewhere between a rock and a human being, consciousness seems to show up. But where, exactly? Pick any
line — "only brains," "only living things," "only things this complex" — and it looks arbitrary, because
the differences on either side are matters of more-or-less, not a sudden all-or-nothing switch. Rather
than draw an unmotivated line in the sand, the panpsychist says: do not draw one at all — let a faint
glimmer of experience reach all the way down, growing richer as things get more organised. It is offered
as the tidier option, not a knock-down proof; an opponent who can point to a *principled* switch-on point
blocks it.
