This is a reply to the [combination problem](combination-problem.md), the standing objection to
[panpsychism](../concepts/panpsychism.md). That objection rests on the claim that distinct subjects, each with
its own private point of view, simply cannot sum into a further subject ([micro-subjects-do-not-sum](../claims/micro-subjects-do-not-sum.md)).
The reply answers that we already have cases of subjects merging and dividing, and that subject-count is
graded rather than discrete — so summing is not impossible but actual, and what we see at the macro scale
can run all the way down. The view is developed at book length by Luke Roelofs ([roelofs-2019](../sources/roelofs-2019.md)).

### The argument

The dominant form is an argument from analogy, with the relevant respect $R$ being *degree of integration
among parts*: the base cases are macroscopic systems, the target case is the micro-composition panpsychism
needs.

1. ([subjects-actually-merge-and-divide](../claims/subjects-actually-merge-and-divide.md)) There are actual cases — split-brain patients,
   craniopagus twins, and groups in deep synchrony — in which the boundary around a subject shifts with how
   tightly the parts are integrated.
2. ([subject-unity-graded-by-integration](../claims/subject-unity-graded-by-integration.md)) Running the split-brain case through the
   [continuity argument](../concepts/continuity-argument-schema.md) — restore the severed fibres one at a time —
   shows the subject-count is not strictly discrete but a graded function of integration "bandwidth."
3. The dependence of subject-unity on degree of integration is the relevant respect $R$, and nothing in it
   is tied to a particular scale (suppressed premise): a macro-subject just *is* sufficiently integrated
   smaller subjects, which are integrated groups of yet-smaller ones, and so on down.

∴ Micro-subjects *can* compose a macro-subject once integration is high enough, so
[micro-subjects-do-not-sum](../claims/micro-subjects-do-not-sum.md) is false and the combination problem does not refute panpsychism. The
unified subject you are now is the high-bandwidth limit of the very same integration-dependent merging we
observe in split brains and synced groups.

### The weak point

Per the analogy pattern, the live attack is **CQ-disanalogy**, and it lands on premise (3). The macro cases
plausibly show graded *functional, psychological, or access* unity, whereas the combination problem is about
*phenomenal* subjects with genuinely private perspectives — so the inference risks equivocating between
integration of function and identity of a felt point of view, the very gap the objection trades on. A
related disanalogy: hemispheres and persons are already richly structured near-subjects, not the bare
micro-subjects the conclusion needs, so the cases may establish only that *largish* subjects merge. The
defender of the objection will also press [phenomenal-determinacy](../claims/phenomenal-determinacy.md) against premise (2)'s graded
count. The reply's best support here is that Nagel's split-brain indeterminacy ([nagel-1971](../sources/nagel-1971.md)) is a
substantive, independently argued position, not an ad hoc move — so whether (3) equivocates is a genuine
open dispute rather than a settled fallacy.

### In plain terms

The combination problem says: tiny separate experiences can't merge into one big experience, so panpsychism
can't build your mind out of micro-minds. This reply says: but merging is a thing we actually see. Cut the
bridge between your brain's two halves and you get something close to two centres of experience; restore it
and there's one. Conjoined twins sharing a brain-bridge seem to share some sensing. People deep in
meditation, or musicians locked into a groove, report many minds briefly feeling like one. And if you
imagine reconnecting a split brain one wire at a time, there's no single wire that flips "two people" to
"one" — so the headcount looks like a dial, not a switch. Turn the dial up high and many become one; turn it
down and one becomes many. If that's how it works for brains and groups, maybe it works the same way at the
smallest scale, with big minds being well-connected teams of little ones. The catch is that "feeling like
one" and "really being one subject" might come apart — and that gap is exactly what the objection was about.
