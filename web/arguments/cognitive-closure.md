inference form: eliminative / transcendental — rule out the only routes by which we could grasp the
property, given that one exists.

1. ([consciousness-has-natural-explanation](../claims/consciousness-has-natural-explanation.md)) There is some natural property P of the brain in
   virtue of which it gives rise to consciousness.
2. ([cognitive-closure-exists](../claims/cognitive-closure-exists.md)) A mind can be constitutively closed to certain properties.
3. We could form the concept of P only by *introspection* or by *outer perception* of the brain (and
   inference from it). [body premise — the exhaustive-routes assumption]
4. Introspection presents experience but not its neural basis; perception of the brain yields only
   spatial/physical concepts, which cannot capture the subjective (the explanatory gap,
   [explanatory-gap](explanatory-gap.md)). So neither route reaches P. [body premise]
∴ ([humans-closed-to-psychophysical-link](../claims/humans-closed-to-psychophysical-link.md)) P is cognitively closed to us: the mind-body
   problem is soluble in itself but insoluble by us. This is the support for [mysterianism](../positions/mysterianism.md).

weakest premise: (3)–(4), the closure of the two routes. Replies: (a) science routinely forms
*theoretical* concepts that are neither introspected nor perceived (charge, spacetime curvature) by
abduction and conceptual innovation — so the two-route dichotomy is too narrow, and McGinn under-rates
concept-creation; (b) we cannot reliably know our own representational limits *in advance*, so the
confident verdict of closure is itself suspect; (c) if the explanatory gap is *merely* cognitive
closure, that tells against reading it as metaphysical residue — so mysterianism and the residue view
([phenomenal-residue](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md)) cannot both take the gap at face value.

Distinct from [explanatory-gap](explanatory-gap.md): that treats the gap as evidence about *metaphysics* (the
phenomenal is not exhausted by role); this treats the very same gap as evidence about *our cognitive
limits* — the problem is real and natural, just humanly unsolvable. Distinct from
[decomposition-indeterminacy](decomposition-indeterminacy.md) and [structure-and-dynamics](structure-and-dynamics.md), which argue function
*omits* or *fails to fix* the phenomenal; mysterianism grants a natural answer exists and denies only
our access to it.

### In plain terms

The argument goes: (1) there's some real, physical reason the brain produces consciousness; (2) minds
can be permanently unable to understand certain things; (3) the only ways we could ever get hold of
that reason are by looking inward (introspection) or by studying the brain from outside; (4) but
looking inward shows you the feeling, not the wiring, and studying the brain from outside only ever
gives you physical/spatial facts that never add up to a feeling. So neither route can deliver the
answer — meaning it's locked away from us for good. The main pushback: science invents concepts all the
time that we neither feel nor see directly (like "electron"), so maybe the two routes aren't the only
ones.
