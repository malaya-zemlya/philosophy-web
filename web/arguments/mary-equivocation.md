Raises thought-experiment CQ5 (begs-the-question) — see the [thought-experiment pattern](../../patterns/thought-experiment.md)
— against the [knowledge argument](knowledge-argument.md). The case runs on two premises about
knowing: that Mary [knows all the physical facts](../claims/mary-knows-all-physical.md) before release
and [learns something on release](../claims/mary-learns-on-release.md). The objection charges that the
inference is valid only if "knows/learns" keeps a single sense across both — and that the sense on
which the conclusion needs a *new fact* is available only if physicalism is already assumed false.
"Knowledge by description" (propositional knowledge-that) and "knowledge by acquaintance"
(a representational or recognitional relation to a state) are different things; Mary may have complete
knowledge-that while gaining a new acquaintance, with no proposition left unknown. Reading her gain as a
*new fact* rather than a new mode of access equivocates, and choosing the fact-reading is choosing the
anti-physicalist premise ([churchland-1985](../sources/churchland-1985.md)).

### The inference

The move is a circularity diagnosis, not a re-identification. It does not assert what Mary gains (that
is the work of the [ability](ability-hypothesis.md), [acquaintance](acquaintance-hypothesis.md), and
[old-fact/new-guise](old-fact-new-guise.md) replies); it says only that the step from "she learns
something" to "she learns a *fact* beyond the physical facts" is licensed by no neutral reading of
"knows." On any disambiguation that keeps the premises true — complete knowledge-*that* plus a new
acquaintance — the conclusion does not follow; on the disambiguation that makes it follow, facts are
individuated so finely that a phenomenal item outstrips every physical fact, which is just to presume
physicalism false. The argument therefore adds no independent reason against physicalism.

### The weakest premise

The pressure point is whether "knowledge by acquaintance" is really *factually empty* — whether coming
to stand in the new relation could not itself be, or entail, learning a truth. If acquaintance brings
propositional knowledge with it (as the bike-rider-style reply urges, that know-how is a species of
knowledge-that), then Mary's new acquaintance may carry a new truth after all, and the equivocation
charge lapses. So the reply leans on a clean fact/acquaintance separation that the opposing camp
denies.

### Distinct from the ability and acquaintance replies

Those replies *characterise* Mary's gain positively — as know-how, or as a new acquaintance-relation —
and thereby deny a new fact. The equivocation reply is meta to them: it need not settle what Mary gains,
only that the argument cannot reach its conclusion without covertly reading "learns" as "learns a new
fact," which begs the question. It targets the inference, not the description.

### In plain terms

The Mary story leans on the word "knows": she *knew* all the physical facts, then she *learns*
something new — so (it concludes) there's a fact physics left out. But "know" slips between two very
different things. There's knowing *that* something is so (facts you could write down) and knowing
*what it's like* by finally meeting it first-hand. Maybe Mary had every writable fact and simply gained
the first-hand acquaintance — no missing fact, just a new way of contacting an old one. To insist the
new thing must be a *fact* beyond physics is to help yourself to the very conclusion you were trying to
prove.
