The *explanatory gap* is the persistent sense that, even after a complete physical and
functional account of a mental state has been given, a further question stays open: "but is
there anything it is *like* to undergo that state?" This entry treats that lingering gap not as
a proof but as a standing piece of evidence, and argues that it tells against the claim that
*every* kind of mental state is [substrate-independent](../claims/substrate-independence.md). The term
and the puzzle were introduced by Joseph Levine ([levine-1983](../sources/levine-1983.md)).

The argument is *abductive* — an inference to the best explanation — and more precisely
*evidential*: a Swinburne-style comparison of how well two rival hypotheses predict the same
observation. It does not claim to demonstrate that there is a phenomenal residue; it claims that
the explanatory gap is evidence the uniform reading of substrate-independence has never managed
to accommodate.

### The likelihood comparison

Let the two hypotheses be:

- **H1** — phenomenal states are *exhausted* by functional role (strong
  [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md));
- **H2** — they are not (the [phenomenal residue](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md));

and let **E** be the evidence: the persistent, post-theoretic intelligibility of the question
"but is there anything it is like to be *that*?" after the entire functional story has been told.

1. On H1, E ought to feel *confused* — the way "but is the bachelor *really* unmarried?" feels
   confused once the analysis is in. It conspicuously does not.
2. The conceivability of a functional isomorph that lacks phenomenal character — the *zombie*, or
   the inverted-spectrum twin ([chalmers-1996](../sources/chalmers-1996.md)) — is intelligible in a way that, on H1,
   it should not be.
3. Hence $P(E\mid \text{H2})$ comfortably exceeds $P(E\mid \text{H1})$.

∴ E confirms H2 over H1: phenomenal character does not metaphysically supervene on functional
role, so "realises the role ⟹ has the state" fails *for the phenomenal case*. That undercuts the
uniform ("all mental states") scope of substrate-independence — the smuggled "all" — while
leaving its intentional applications untouched.

### The weakest premise

Premise (2): conceivability is not transparently a guide to possibility, and the advocate's best
reply — deny the zombie's possibility — lands here. The argument is calibrated for that pressure.
It asserts only a likelihood inequality, not metaphysical necessity, so denying (2) outright
requires showing that E is *no more* expected on H2 than on H1, a heavier burden than merely
doubting zombies. The argument would be retracted by a genuine answer to the zombie — a
demonstration that the phenomenal supervenes on role — rather than by a mere redescription of it.

This is distinct from the [behavioural-parity argument](behavioral-parity.md), which is an
abduction *for* mental status from behaviour; this one is an abduction *against* the uniform scope
of substrate-independence's enabling premise. It is also distinct from the
[grain dilemma](role-grain-dilemma.md), which attacks the individuation of "role" itself:
this argument grants a fixed role and presses what role leaves out.

### In plain terms

Suppose you had a complete scientific account of everything your brain *does* when you see red —
every signal, every reaction, all of it. A strange question still seems to make sense: "fine, but is
there actually something it's *like* to see red? Is anyone home in there?" Compare: once you know
"a bachelor is an unmarried man," asking "yes, but is he *really* unmarried?" is just confused —
there's no question left over. The point here is that with consciousness the leftover question
stubbornly does *not* feel confused. We can even picture a being that does everything we do yet feels
nothing inside (philosophers call it a "zombie"), and that picture seems to hang together. If feeling
were nothing more than function, none of that should make sense. So this lingering gap is treated as
evidence that the *felt* side of mind isn't fully captured by function alone. (It's offered as
evidence, not proof — the weak point being that managing to *imagine* something doesn't by itself
show it's genuinely possible.)
