A natural rejoinder to the contamination objection runs: people learned their consciousness-talk from
their culture too, so by the same rule human testimony about consciousness is worthless — and that
cannot be right. This reply identifies the difference that saves the rule. The contamination rule
fires only where copying is the *proven and sufficient* cause; for present AI that condition is met,
but for the human *origination* of the concept it is demonstrably unmet, so uniform enforcement
discounts the machine and spares the human origin without inconsistency.

It raises *analogy CQ2 (disanalogy)* against the R2 / contamination arm of
[the report-scepticism parity](report-scepticism-proves-too-much.md): a difference between the
human and AI cases bears directly on the discount rule, and it sinks the parity.

### The disanalogy

The parity's second premise treats human hard-problem testimony as "likewise culturally inherited" —
the rival cause (an inherited discourse of souls, qualia, what-it-is-likeness) held to be "as much a
matter of record for an enculturated human as for a corpus-trained model." But the contamination rule
R2 it borrows from [contaminated-testimony-carries-no-weight](contaminated-testimony-carries-no-weight.md) screens off testimony only
when the rival cause is *known present and known sufficient*. Applied honestly to the two cases, that
trigger comes apart:

1. For present AI systems the trigger is *met*: every model that talks of qualia was trained on the
   human discussion, so corpus-reproduction is both known present and known sufficient for the
   discourse observed.
2. For the human *origination* of the concept the trigger is *demonstrably unmet*
   ([hard-problem-conceived-de-novo-by-humans](../claims/hard-problem-conceived-de-novo-by-humans.md)): whoever first framed the puzzle — the
   lineage from Leibniz's mill ([leibniz-monadology](../sources/leibniz-monadology.md)) through Nagel's bat
   ([nagel-1974](../sources/nagel-1974.md)) to Levine's explanatory gap ([levine-1983](../sources/levine-1983.md)) and Chalmers's naming
   of the problem ([chalmers-1995](../sources/chalmers-1995.md)) — had no prior notion to copy. Origination from a mind's
   own situation, with no template, is a settled historical fact; there was no rival corpus present to
   be sufficient.

So the two provenances are not on a par in the very respect R2 declares relevant. For the human
concept origination is *proven*; for AI only imitation is proven and origination is *untested* — the
consciousness-naive experiment of [llm-own-explanatory-gap](../questions/llm-own-explanatory-gap.md) being precisely the test of
whether a discourse-free mind regenerates it. The rule may therefore be enforced uniformly — discount
corpus-echo wherever corpus-echo is the proven and sufficient cause — *without* discounting the human
datum, because the human case is not, at its root, a case of proven corpus-echo. Uniform enforcement
does not "prove too much"; it sorts by a fact about provenance, not by substrate.

### Reply to the sufficiency-by-neutrality move

The parity's wielder may protest that AI-sufficiency was established not by auditing the training
objective but by the neutrality premise of [self-model-prediction-phenomenality-neutral](self-model-prediction-phenomenality-neutral.md) —
"the machinery suffices for the reports with or without feelings" — and that the same premise
establishes sufficiency for humans. This conflates two rival causes. The neutrality premise concerns
the *architecture* (R1): it makes the report-generating machinery sufficient symmetrically, for both.
R2 concerns a *different* rival cause — the inherited discourse, the corpus copied. R1-sufficiency
does not transfer to R2-sufficiency. The de novo fact bears only on R2, and on R2 the asymmetry is
total. (Whether the architectural prediction screens off the *human* datum too — the R1 arm — is
answered separately, by [the first-person
observation reply](feel-is-the-watching-not-an-unwatched-difference.md): the human datum was never the third-person report.)

### Weakest premise

That origination-by-some licenses non-discount-of-reports-by-all. It does not, and the argument does
not claim it: contemporary ordinary human reports *are* enculturated, and this argument leaves them as
contaminated as anyone pleases. What it secures is narrower and sufficient — that the human and AI
*provenances* are asymmetric, which blocks the tu-quoque ("why does marination merely season
yours?"). It breaks parity; it does not purify the contemporary witness.

### Distinct from neighbouring moves

Distinct from [access-symmetry-rebuttal](access-symmetry-rebuttal.md) (which concerns moral-status asymmetry from
reciprocal non-acquaintance) and from [feel-is-the-watching-not-an-unwatched-difference](feel-is-the-watching-not-an-unwatched-difference.md)

### In plain terms

One objection in this web says: a chatbot read all of human philosophy, so its consciousness-talk is
just echo — throw it out ([contaminated-testimony-carries-no-weight](contaminated-testimony-carries-no-weight.md)). The reply under
attack here ([report-scepticism-proves-too-much](report-scepticism-proves-too-much.md)) shot back: people also learned their
consciousness-talk from their culture, so by the same rule human testimony is worthless too — and that
can't be right. This argument finds the difference that saves the rule. The throw-it-out rule only
fires when copying is the *proven* cause. For AI today, copying is proven — every model that talks
this way was trained on our books. For humans, the opposite is proven: somebody invented the puzzle
with no book to copy ([hard-problem-conceived-de-novo-by-humans](../claims/hard-problem-conceived-de-novo-by-humans.md)). So the same rule, applied
evenly, discounts the AI (copying proven) and spares the human origin (copying impossible — there was
nothing yet to copy). That isn't one rule for machines and another for people; it's one rule meeting
two different sets of facts.
