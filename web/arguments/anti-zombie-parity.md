Raises thought-experiment CQ5 (begs-the-question) — see the [thought-experiment pattern](../../patterns/thought-experiment.md)
— against the [zombie argument](zombie-conceivability.md) at its modal bridge. The zombie argument
runs a conceivability intuition through the bridge [conceivability-entails-possibility](../claims/conceivability-entails-possibility.md)
to reach a metaphysically possible zombie, and thence the falsity of physicalism. The objection points
out that an *anti-zombie* — a full physical duplicate of us whose physical nature *necessitates*
consciousness — is equally conceivable ([anti-zombie-conceivable](../claims/anti-zombie-conceivable.md);
[frankish-2007](../sources/frankish-2007.md)). Run through the same bridge, the anti-zombie is
metaphysically possible; and an anti-zombie's possibility entails that consciousness is necessitated by
the physical in some world, which for a necessitation thesis means physicalism is possibly — hence
actually — true. The same rule that is supposed to refute physicalism thereby establishes it. Two
contradictory conclusions from one principle show the principle over-generates, so asserting the zombie
side privileges one symmetric intuition over its twin without warrant.

### The inference

This is a parity reductio, not a Kripkean counterexample. It grants the zombie's conceivability and the
bridge *as the zombie argument states them*, and derives a contradiction by feeding the mirror-image
scenario through the identical machinery. The upshot is aimed at the bridge premise: since
conceivability licenses both a zombie world (no consciousness despite the physics) and an anti-zombie
world (consciousness guaranteed by the physics), and these cannot both be metaphysically possible if
either thesis is necessary, conceivability cannot be a reliable guide to possibility here. The zombie
theorist must break the symmetry — show the anti-zombie only *apparently* conceivable — and doing so
without already assuming the falsity of physicalism is the burden the objection imposes.

### The weakest premise

The load-bearing claim is the alleged *symmetry*: that the anti-zombie is conceivable in exactly the
sense, and to exactly the degree, that the zombie is. A zombie theorist replies that the cases are
disanalogous — that reflection on a full physical duplicate leaves the *presence* of consciousness open
(so a zombie is genuinely conceivable) while an anti-zombie covertly builds in the necessitation it is
supposed to reveal, making its conceivability shallow. Whether such an asymmetry can be drawn without
begging the question is where the argument is contested.

### Distinct from the a-posteriori-necessity objection

This is not [conceivability-not-guide-to-possibility](conceivability-not-guide-to-possibility.md),
which attacks the same bridge by *counterexample* — Kripkean a-posteriori necessities (water is H₂O)
whose negations are conceivable yet impossible. The present move needs no such external cases: it turns
the bridge on itself by symmetry, showing it proves too much from within. The two are complementary
attacks on one premise. The anti-zombie symmetry is noted in passing by
[unwarrantable-zombie-premise](unwarrantable-zombie-premise.md) without being developed into a distinct
line; this node instantiates it.

### In plain terms

The zombie argument says: I can coherently imagine a physical twin of me with no inner experience, so
such a thing is really possible, so consciousness must be something extra beyond the physical. Turn it
around. I can *equally* coherently imagine a physical twin of me who is conscious *precisely because* of
how it's physically built — an "anti-zombie." By the very same "imaginable, therefore possible" rule,
that's possible too — and if it's possible that the physical guarantees consciousness, then physicalism
is true. One rule, two opposite conclusions: the rule is broken. The zombie fan has to explain why
their imagined case counts and the mirror-image one doesn't — and it's hard to do that without just
assuming from the start that consciousness is non-physical.
