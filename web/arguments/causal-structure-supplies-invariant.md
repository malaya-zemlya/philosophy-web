The *causal-structure reply* defends [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md) about consciousness
against the charge that nothing fixes how a physical system is to be divided into functional parts.
Its move is to deny that the carving is up to us: a system's objective web of cause and effect
already settles which decomposition is the real one, so the observer-independent invariant the critic
demands is something nature supplies for free.

### The argument

The reply is *deductive* — it runs from a constraint on what implementation requires, plus the
objectivity of that constraint, to the existence of the demanded invariant.

1. **Implementation needs robust counterfactuals.** To genuinely carry out a computation, or
   realise a functional organisation, a system must have the right counterfactual dispositions
   across its whole transition table — "had this state obtained, that one would have followed" — and
   not merely for the states that happened to occur. This excludes the gerrymandered, after-the-fact
   mappings that make triviality theorems run ([implementation-requires-counterfactual-structure](../claims/implementation-requires-counterfactual-structure.md)).
   The point is the heart of Chalmers's reply ([chalmers-1996b](../sources/chalmers-1996b.md)) to Putnam's theorem that
   every ordinary open system implements every finite-state automaton ([putnam-1988](../sources/putnam-1988.md)).
2. **Those facts are objective.** The counterfactual-causal facts about a system hold independently
   of any observer's choice of description ([counterfactual-structure-objective](../claims/counterfactual-structure-objective.md)).

∴ The admissible decomposition is fixed by objective causal structure
([causal-structure-fixes-decomposition](../claims/causal-structure-fixes-decomposition.md)). The invariant that
[the indeterminacy objection](decomposition-indeterminacy.md) said functionalism still owed
therefore already exists, and it is the *organisational grain* — the level of [grain](../concepts/grain.md)
at which the system's roles are individuated ([role-grain-is-organisational](../claims/role-grain-is-organisational.md)). The reply
attacks the indeterminacy objection at its first premise, the claim that
[functional decomposition is description-relative](../claims/functional-decomposition-relative.md).

### Bearing and the weakest step

The reply answers the indeterminacy objection on the functionalist's behalf and ties the demanded
invariant to the organisational-grain view already developed in the web. It is distinct from
[role-grain-is-organisational](../claims/role-grain-is-organisational.md), which it supports: that claim fixes *which* grain is the
right one, whereas this argument contends only that *some* grain is fixed at all.

The vulnerable move is the inference from (1) and (2) to the conclusion, which arguably overreaches.
The anti-triviality result secures *non-triviality*, not *uniqueness*: ruling out the silly mappings
need not leave exactly one respectable carving. And on an interventionist reading the causal facts
are objective only relative to a chosen set of variables. So the conclusion is contested from two
directions — by [the
non-triviality-without-uniqueness reply](counterfactuals-yield-non-triviality-not-uniqueness.md), which argues that a whole family of carvings survives, and
by the [relevance-filter regress](relevance-filter-regress.md), which argues that causal
structure alone does not single out the *mind-relevant* relations (the "squishy-noise" problem).

### In plain terms

This answers the "who gets to decide how to slice the system up?" worry — the objection that says a
definite consciousness can't rest on an observer's choice of carving
([decomposition-indeterminacy](decomposition-indeterminacy.md)). The reply: you actually can't slice it just any way you
please. To genuinely *carry out* a given process, a system has to have the right web of
cause-and-effect — "if this had fired, that would have followed" — holding across all the possible
cases, not only the ones that happened to come up. Cooked-up, after-the-fact relabelings don't have
that robust causal backbone, so they don't count as real organizations. And those cause-and-effect
facts are out there in the world, not a matter of how an observer chooses to look. So nature *does*
hand you one real organization after all — the "one true carving" the worry demanded exists, and
it's the level cognitive science already works at. Where it gets pushed back: ruling out the silly
slicings might still leave a whole *family* of respectable ones rather than exactly one; and raw
cause-and-effect includes plenty of irrelevant stuff (a brain's warmth, its squishiness), so picking
out the specifically *mind-relevant* causes may need a further rule this reply hasn't supplied.
