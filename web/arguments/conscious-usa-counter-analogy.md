Raises analogy CQ4 (counter-analogy) — see the [analogy pattern](../../patterns/analogy.md) — against
the [combining-subjects argument](subjects-can-combine.md). That argument's engine is a suppressed
scale-free premise: because subject-unity is a graded function of *degree of integration*, and nothing
in integration is tied to a scale, sufficiently integrated smaller subjects compose a macro-subject
all the way down. A counter-analogy exhibits a third case at least as integrated as the argument's
targets in which subjecthood intuitively *fails*. Schwitzgebel's case is a densely
information-integrated group agent — the population of the United States, exchanging and processing
information at enormous bandwidth — which satisfies the integration condition yet which almost no one
credits with a unified felt point of view ([schwitzgebel-2015](../sources/schwitzgebel-2015.md)).

### The inference

The move attacks the scale-free premise (premise 3) at its inference step, not a premise claim: if
high integration can be present while phenomenal subjecthood is intuitively absent, then integration
bandwidth tracks *functional* unity rather than the identity of a point of view — the very gap the
combination problem trades on. The counter-analysis thus reinstates CQ-disanalogy from the other
direction: the combining argument needs integration to *carry* subjecthood across scales, and here is
a maximally integrated system where, by the same intuitions the argument relies on elsewhere, it does
not.

An attribution caveat matters here (and is recorded on the source): Schwitzgebel himself runs the
case as a *modus ponens* — *if* materialism is true, the United States is *probably* conscious — and
does not reject the consequent. The objection does not borrow his conclusion; it borrows the widely
shared intuitive verdict that the nation is not a felt subject, and uses it as the counter-analogy the
combining argument must answer.

### The weakest premise

The soft point is that a panpsychist committed to graded subjects may simply *accept* the consequent —
bite the bullet and grant the United States some faint, low-grade subjecthood — in which case the case
is not a counterexample but a confirming instance. The objection's force then reduces to a burden
shift: the combining argument leaned on the intuition that integration produces a *unified* subject,
and cannot both invoke that intuition for brains and disown it for nations without an independent mark
of which integrated systems host a point of view.

### In plain terms

The combining-subjects reply says: crank up the integration between little minds and you get one big
mind — that's how your brain is built from smaller parts, and maybe how everything is. The catch is a
famous embarrassing case: the whole United States is a gigantic web of people passing information
around at staggering volume — extremely "integrated" — and yet it seems obvious there's no single
*someone* who is the country, feeling things. If cranking up integration doesn't conjure a felt
subject *there*, then integration by itself isn't what makes a unified mind, and the argument can't
lean on it. (The philosopher who made the case actually says: if materialism is true, maybe the USA
*is* a little conscious — but most people take the opposite intuition, and that's what the objection
uses.)
