The **thinking-animal argument** ("too many thinkers") is [animalism](../positions/animalism.md)'s
engine (Olson, [1997](../sources/olson-1997.md)). It needs no science fiction — only the chair you are
sitting in.

**Inference form:** deductive (a counting argument).

1. There is a human animal sitting in your chair — a living organism of *Homo sapiens*. (Denying
   this means denying that animals exist where we all suppose they do.)
2. That animal is thinking your thoughts: it has your working brain, your senses, your history of
   stimulation — everything anything needs to think. A thing with a functioning human brain that
   *cannot* think would be a mystery.
3. You are thinking your thoughts, and there are not two thinkers — you and a numerically distinct
   animal — each thinking them. (Two thinkers per thought would double the population, and worse:
   the animal, thinking exactly what you think, would believe *it* is you, and you could never
   tell you are not the one making that mistake.)
4. ∴ You are the animal — and so your persistence conditions are the animal's: biological
   continuity ([identity-is-biological-continuity](../claims/identity-is-biological-continuity.md)), not the psychological criterion
   ([identity-is-psychological-continuity](../claims/identity-is-psychological-continuity.md)), which assigns you and the animal different
   persistence conditions and thereby makes you two things.

**Weakest premise:** (2), that the *animal* is a thinker. Neo-Lockeans respond in several ways:
constitutionalists say the animal thinks only derivatively, by constituting a person, as a statue's
shape belongs primarily to the statue and not the lump; others deny that animals think at all in
the relevant first-person way; still others (temporal-part theorists) accept overlapping thinkers
and dissolve the counting worry by making person and animal share their current stages. Each escape
carries its own metaphysical bill, which is precisely the argument's point: the cheap, commonsense
bookkeeping belongs to animalism.

### In plain terms

Simple question: is there an animal in your chair right now? Obviously — a living human body,
yours. Second question: is that animal thinking? It has a working brain, so it's hard to say no.
But *you* are the one thinking your thoughts. So either you just *are* that animal (the
animalist's conclusion), or there are two things reading this sentence — you and an animal double —
which is one reader too many. And if there were two, how would you know you're the person and not
the animal, since the animal believes exactly what you believe? The rival
[mind-thread theory](../claims/identity-is-psychological-continuity.md) has replies (maybe only the
*person* strictly thinks, the way only the statue, not the clay, is a masterpiece) — but each reply
buys complexity, while the animalist's answer stays as plain as the chair.
