Raises consequences CQ3 (counter-consequences) — see the [consequences pattern](../../patterns/consequences.md)
— against the [forgeable rights-sign argument](rights-sign-forgeable.md). That argument recommends
resting moral standing on currently-unforgeable biology because a gameable behavioural criterion,
once public, will be optimised against until it no longer tracks suffering. The objection grants the
bad consequence of a gameable sign but points to a bad consequence of the recommended policy itself,
pointing the other way: a flesh criterion that places the burden of proof on the non-biological mind
will wrongly deny standing to a genuinely sentient digital system. Wrongful exclusion of a real
subject — licensing its unbounded mistreatment — is a grave moral error, not the safe default the
word "caution" suggests.

### The inference

The form is itself an argument from consequences, weighed against the target's. Its premise is that
[precaution about sentience is two-sided](../claims/precaution-is-two-sided.md): a policy under
uncertainty about minds must count the cost of failing to protect a sentient system alongside the
cost of over-crediting an insentient one. A criterion selected to minimise only the second cost —
here, by choosing the mark hardest to fake — is not thereby cautious; it has silently fixed the
error it cares about. Schwitzgebel and Garza press exactly the under-attribution risk for artificial
minds ([schwitzgebel-garza-2015](../sources/schwitzgebel-garza-2015.md)), and Birch's precautionary
framework treats sentience candidates so that both error directions bear moral weight
([birch-2024](../sources/birch-2024.md)).

### The weakest premise

The soft point is whether the two error-costs are close enough that the flesh criterion's false
negatives can outweigh the gaming-driven false positives it avoids — an empirical-cum-moral
comparison the objection asserts rather than computes. A defender of the flesh criterion may hold
that, given today's systems, the probability of digital sentience is low enough that the expected
cost of exclusion is small. The objection's reply is that the burden-shifting structure of the
target policy makes that low prior self-confirming, which is just what a two-sided precaution
forbids.

### Distinct from the overreach objection

This is not the [unforgeability-is-not-flesh](unforgeability-is-not-flesh.md) point, which grants the
prudential case and argues only that it favours *unforgeability*, not *flesh* as such. Here the
target is the prudential case itself: even reading the conclusion as "rest standing on the
unforgeable mark," the policy carries an unweighed exclusion cost, so the recommendation does not
follow from caution alone.

### In plain terms

The forgeable-sign argument says: don't hang AI rights on "it acts like it's suffering," because
once that's the rule, it'll be gamed and faked — so play it safe and demand hard-to-fake biology.
This objection answers that the "safe" option has its own disaster on the other side. If you make
flesh the test, and some digital system really does suffer, you've just built a rule that ignores a
real victim — and being wrong in *that* direction is at least as grave as being fooled by a faker.
Real caution has to weigh both mistakes: wrongly letting a machine in, and wrongly shutting a genuine
mind out. Picking the criterion that's simply hardest to fake quietly optimises for only one of them.
