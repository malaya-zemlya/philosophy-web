A disanalogy pressed against the [strict duality
thesis](../claims/consciousness-freewill-strict-duality.md): the two poles do not have the same modal status. The perceptual streams entering
experience are *actual* and co-present; the action options confronting the will are *possibilia*,
of which exactly one actualizes. A duality within a single category relates constructions among
things of the same kind; it should not straddle the seam between the actual and the merely
possible.

**Inference form:** deductive — a structural mismatch between the two sides as characterized by
[conscious-content-is-product-like](../claims/conscious-content-is-product-like.md) and [action-content-is-coproduct-like](../claims/action-content-is-coproduct-like.md).

1. The product on the input side is a product of actual, simultaneously realized channels.
2. The coproduct on the output side is (on the natural reading) a coproduct over unactualized
   alternatives — mere possibilia.
3. An actual tuple and a menu of possibilia are not dual objects in any one category; the "duality"
   equivocates between a structure of occurrences and a structure of options.

∴ The strict duality thesis fails as stated.

**The reply the objection invites — and its cost.** The seam does not refute the framework; it
*localizes* the free-will dispute. On a deflationist reading the "options" are actual,
simultaneously tokened action-representations during deliberation: the coproduct is over actual
candidate plans, both sides live in the category of representational states, and the asymmetry
dissolves. Only on the libertarian reading — a coproduct over metaphysical possibilia
([alternatives-genuinely-open](../claims/alternatives-genuinely-open.md)) — does the seam open. Whether the action-coproduct is
representational or modal *is* the free-will question, restated. Nor is the input side purely
actual: binocular rivalry and Necker-style bistability are input-side exclusive selection among
alternatives only one of which becomes actual-in-experience, and predictive processing treats
percepts as selected hypotheses. The actual/possible line does not cleanly track the input/output
line — which blunts premise 1 and premise 2 simultaneously.

**Weakest premise:** premise 2. It assumes the libertarian reading of options; on the
representational reading the objection's mismatch never arises, so the argument's force against the
strict thesis is exactly as strong as the case for leeway libertarianism.

### In plain terms

What you see and hear right now is all really happening at once; the things you *might do next*
mostly never happen at all. Mirroring the two — real inputs against merely-possible outputs — looks
like comparing apples to hypothetical oranges. The interesting twist: whether the "options" in your
head are real brain events (candidate plans, all actually there) or genuinely open forks in the
world is itself the oldest question about free will. So this objection doesn't break the mirror so
much as show where the old question reappears inside it.
