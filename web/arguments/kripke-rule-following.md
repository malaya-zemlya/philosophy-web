The *rule-following* argument — Saul Kripke's reconstruction of Wittgenstein, often called "Kripke's
Wittgenstein" or "Kripkenstein" ([kripke-1982](../sources/kripke-1982.md), reading [wittgenstein-1953](../sources/wittgenstein-1953.md)) — is a
skeptical paradox about meaning. It asks what *fact* makes it the case that a person means one thing
rather than another by a word, and argues that no candidate fact can do the job. Its conclusion is
[that there is no constitutive fact of meaning](../claims/no-constitutive-fact-of-meaning.md).

### The challenge: plus or quus?

Suppose you have computed many sums but, being finite, never "68 + 57." Define a deviant function: *quus*,
which agrees with plus on every pair you have actually added but yields 5 for arguments larger than any you
have met. The skeptic grants every fact about your past and asks: in virtue of what did you mean *plus*,
so that the correct answer now is 125, rather than *quus*, so that it is 5? The question is not how you
*know* which you meant; it is what *makes it true* that you meant either — what fact your meaning consists
in. The demand is for a fact that (i) is something about *you*, and (ii) is *normative*: it must say what
you *ought* to answer, not merely predict what you will.

### The elimination

The argument is an *argument by elimination*: each candidate for the meaning-constituting fact is shown to
fail.

1. **Past usage.** Your actual past applications are a finite set, equally compatible with plus and with
   indefinitely many deviant rules. A finite record cannot single out one infinite function.
2. **Dispositions.** "You are disposed to answer 125" fares no better. Your dispositions are also finite
   (for huge inputs you would be unable to answer, or would die first); they include *mistakes* (you are
   disposed to misadd sometimes), so they cannot define the *correct* answer; and they are merely
   descriptive — they say what you *would* do, not what you *should* — so they miss the normativity meaning
   requires.
3. **Mental images or qualia.** Picturing a rule, or an inner feeling of "meaning addition," is just
   another sign that itself stands in need of interpretation; it does not reach out and fix the answer for
   cases never considered.
4. **A primitive state of meaning.** Positing a sui generis, irreducible state of *meaning-plus* names the
   problem rather than solving it: such a state would have to be a finite item in the mind that somehow
   determines indefinitely many correct applications, which is the very magic in question.

∴ No fact about the speaker constitutes meaning one thing rather than another
([no-constitutive-fact-of-meaning](../claims/no-constitutive-fact-of-meaning.md)).

### The skeptical solution

Kripke's Wittgenstein does not conclude that language is empty. It abandons the demand for a
*truth-conditional* fact of meaning and offers a *skeptical solution*: talk of meaning has
**assertibility conditions**, not fact-stating ones. To mean addition is to be a member of a community
whose shared practice licenses counting "125" as correct and corrects deviations — meaning as *use*,
sustained by communal agreement, with no private fact underneath. This makes the account constitutively
*social*, and connects the paradox to the private-language argument: a would-be solitary rule-follower has
nothing to distinguish "following the rule" from "seeming to."

### Weakest premise

The eliminative survey's treatment of **dispositions** (premise 2), and behind it the *demand for a
normative, infinitary constituting fact*. Dispositionalists reply that meaning is fixed by dispositions
under *idealised* or *optimal* conditions (bracketing finitude and error), so the normativity and the
infinite reach are recovered. Others reject the framing outright: meaning need not be *constituted* by any
further fact to be real, or it is fixed by causal/teleological relations the survey did not consider. The
argument is only as strong as its claim that *every* such repair fails.

### Bearing on the web

The argument generalises beyond arithmetic to all content, so it is a direct pressure on
[how any physical state could be about something determinate](../questions/intentionality.md). Kripke notes
that a *machine* is no better off than a person: a physical device does not determine the function it
computes, since it is finite and can malfunction — which makes the rule-following paradox a close cousin of
[the indeterminacy of computational decomposition](decomposition-indeterminacy.md) and of the
worry that "which function a system computes" is not fixed by its physical facts alone. It also sharpens
the [original/derived intentionality](../concepts/intrinsic-derived-intentionality.md) distinction:
if even a mind has no private fact fixing its content, the hope of grounding *original* intentionality in
an inner state looks dim, and the [syntax-to-semantics gap](../claims/syntax-insufficient-for-semantics.md) gets
a further twist — the trouble may be not just that symbols need interpreting, but that nothing fixes the
interpretation as uniquely correct.

### In plain terms

You've done lots of addition, but only finitely many sums. Could you have secretly meant a weird rule all
along — "normal addition, except answer 5 for any numbers bigger than I've ever tried" — that fits
everything you've ever actually done? Kripke hunts for the fact that rules this out — that makes it true you
meant ordinary *plus* — and finds nothing that works: your past sums are finite and fit both rules; your
habits are finite and error-prone (so they can't define the *right* answer); and no mental picture or
feeling spells out the answer for cases you've never met. Spooky conclusion: there's no inner fact that
pins down what you mean. His escape isn't "nothing means anything" but "meaning isn't a private fact at
all — it lives in the shared practice of a community that agrees on what counts as going on the same way."
The same problem haunts computers: nothing about a finite, breakable machine settles which exact rule it's
really following.
