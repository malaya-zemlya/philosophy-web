The *grain dilemma* is an objection to [substrate independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md)
arguing that its key term, "role," admits no grain of individuation that secures the desired
conclusion without absurdity — at least, none its advocate has drawn in a principled way. It is a
constructive dilemma: a mental state's individuating functional role must be specified at *some*
grain, and both available grains lead somewhere unacceptable. The squeeze is due to Ned Block,
whose "Troubles with Functionalism" pressed the liberal/chauvinist dilemma against any functional
account of mind ([block-1978](../sources/block-1978.md)).

**Horn 1 — coarse-grained.** Specify the role at the level of input–output behaviour, the classic
Turing profile. Then functionalism is *too liberal*: a vast [lookup
table](../concepts/lookup-table-mind.md), or Block's homunculi-headed [China](../concepts/chinese-nation.md), realises that profile and so
must, on substrate independence, have a mind. (The lookup-table version is Block's "blockhead,"
from his "Psychologism and Behaviorism," [block](../sources/block.md).) Going coarse enough to admit the
silicon admits the absurd alongside it — this is the soft underbelly that
[the behavioural-parity argument](behavioral-parity.md) itself flags in its own premise.

**Horn 2 — fine-grained.** Specify the role at the level of full causal microstructure: spike
timing, neuromodulator diffusion, and the rest. Then functionalism risks being *too chauvinistic*,
the role becoming so biologically specific that nothing but a brain, or a near-molecular emulation
of one, can realise it — and substrate independence quietly collapses toward the neighbourhood of
[proteocentrism](../positions/proteocentrism.md), with the carbon doing real individuative work after all.

Therefore the advocate of substrate independence owes a grain *coarse enough to be multiply
realisable in silicon yet fine enough to exclude the blockhead*, together with a principled reason
for drawing the line there rather than for the convenience of the conclusion. The claim as written
simply helps itself to the existence of such a line.

### Weakest premise

The dilemma's exhaustiveness — its assumption that no *intermediate* grain escapes both horns. A
reply that specifies a principled middle grain, and motivates it independently of the desired
verdict on silicon, would dissolve the dilemma; that is exactly the move its exhaustiveness premise
leaves open, taken up by [the organisational-grain
middle](organisational-grain-dissolves-dilemma.md).

Distinct from [the explanatory gap](explanatory-gap.md), which grants a fixed role and
presses what role *leaves out* (phenomenal character): the grain dilemma attacks the *individuation*
of role itself, and so applies even to intentional states. It responds to
[behavioural parity](behavioral-parity.md) because horn 1 sharpens the blockhead objection
that argument names against its own premise.

### In plain terms

Suppose you say a mind is defined by what it does, so a machine could have one too. Fair enough —
but *how detailed* is your description of "what it does"? You're caught between two bad answers. Go
coarse ("right answers in, right answers out") and you let in obvious frauds: a giant lookup table
that just spits back pre-stored replies would count as having a mind. Go fine (exact brain
chemistry, the precise timing of neurons) and you've quietly made a *brain* the only thing that
qualifies — so machines are excluded after all, and you've smuggled biology back in. To make
machine minds work you need a description loose enough to fit silicon yet strict enough to shut out
the cheat-sheet — *and* an honest reason for drawing the line right there, not a reason picked just
because it delivers the verdict you wanted. The complaint: nobody has actually drawn that
principled line.
