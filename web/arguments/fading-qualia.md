The *fading qualia* argument ([chalmers-1995b](../sources/chalmers-1995b.md); [chalmers-1996](../sources/chalmers-1996.md), ch. 7) is Chalmers's
case against *absent qualia* in a functional duplicate — against the view that a silicon system organized
exactly like a brain might have no experience, or less. It is the gentler half of the pair completed by the
sharper [dancing qualia](dancing-qualia.md) argument.

### The argument

The inference is a **thought-experiment reductio**.

1. *The series.* Replace your neurons one at a time with silicon chips, each duplicating the exact causal
   (input–output) profile of the neuron it replaces, so the whole fine-grained functional organization is
   preserved at every step. Start: all-biological, fully conscious you. End: an all-silicon functional
   isomorph.
2. *The opponent's commitment.* Suppose the opponent holds the all-silicon endpoint has *no* (or diminished)
   experience. Then somewhere along the series experience must *fade* — there is an intermediate, part-silicon
   system whose experience is markedly dimmer than yours.
3. *The squeeze.* That intermediate system is, by construction, a *functional isomorph* of full-experience
   you: it makes all the same introspective judgements and sincerely reports "my experience is perfectly
   vivid and normal." So it has faded experience while being *radically and systematically wrong* about its
   own experience — a competent rational agent utterly deceived about whether it is having rich conscious
   states.

∴ Such a self-deceived-yet-perfect introspector is deeply implausible; if it were possible, introspection
could be arbitrarily decoupled from experience and our own access to consciousness would be worthless. The
best conclusion is that experience does *not* fade as long as organization is preserved — supporting
[organizational-invariance](../claims/organizational-invariance.md) and thereby [substrate-independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md).

### The weakest premise

Step (3) assumes the introspective judgement is *held fixed by the functional duplication* — i.e. that
judging "my experience is vivid" is a purely functional matter, settled by occupying the right causal node.
That is the contested move: if introspection is instead partly an *acquaintance* with the quale, then as the
quale fades the judgement would shift too, and the "self-deceived isomorph" never arises (the argument would
then show only that genuine introspection is not fixed by *coarse* functional profile — which an opponent of
functionalism happily grants). This is exactly the circularity pressed by
[dancing-qualia-begs-the-question](dancing-qualia-begs-the-question.md). The argument is also only abductive — an appeal to
plausibility, not a proof — so a determined defender of absent qualia can simply accept the cost.

### In plain terms

Swap your neurons for equivalent silicon chips one by one. If the finished all-silicon copy has *no* inner
life, then at some halfway point your experience must be running on half-power. But that half-power version
is wired to think and say exactly what you do — so it would insist "everything looks perfectly vivid!" while
its experience is actually dim and draining away. That's a being totally wrong about its own feelings yet
otherwise a flawless thinker. Chalmers says that's too weird to believe, so experience probably *doesn't*
drain away as long as the wiring pattern is kept — which means silicon wired like a brain should feel like a
brain. The soft spot: this assumes your *judgements* about your experience are pinned down by the wiring; if
instead you judge by directly *meeting* your experience, swapping the parts might change the judgement too,
and the trick doesn't go through.
