The **appearance-is-reality argument** defends the datum view of introspection on principled
grounds: the very distinction that makes representation *fallible* is missing in the case of
occurrent experience.

**Inference form:** deductive (from the structure of the appearance/reality distinction).

1. A representation can be wrong only where how things *seem* can differ from how they *are* — the
   appearance/reality gap is what error lives in.
2. For ordinary objects the gap is real: the stick seems bent but is straight. But a conscious
   experience just is a seeming: its reality consists in how it appears to its subject. Where
   consciousness is concerned, Searle argues, we cannot make the appearance/reality distinction,
   because *the appearance is the reality* ([1992](../sources/searle-1992.md)). Kripke's modal version
   makes the same point: nothing could be exactly like pain without being pain — the felt
   appearance exhausts the essence ([1980](../sources/kripke-1980.md)).
3. So there is no room for a subject's current experience to seem some way while being, felt-wise,
   another: a "misrepresented seeming" would simply be a different seeming.
4. ∴ Introspective contact with occurrent experience is not a fallible report about an independent
   target but direct possession of the fact itself
   ([introspective-acquaintance-is-datum](../claims/introspective-acquaintance-is-datum.md)) — and the representation view
   ([introspection-is-fallible-representation](../claims/introspection-is-fallible-representation.md)), which models that contact on outward
   report channels, mislocates the gap.

**Weakest premise:** step 2's identification of experience with its seeming. The illusionist
replies that this is exactly the question at issue: if introspection is a constructed model
([Frankish 2016](../sources/frankish-2016.md); [Graziano 2013](../sources/graziano-2013.md)), then "how the
experience seems" is the model's *output*, and the model can misdescribe the neural-functional
state it tracks — the felt "seeming" is then precisely the kind of appearance that can diverge from
reality. The argument, they say, secures only the trivial point that a seeming seems how it seems.
Note also the scope concession built into the argument: it protects the *occurrent* feel only, not
introspective judgments about causes, comparisons, or categories, which all parties agree can err
(see [introspective-confabulation](introspective-confabulation.md)).

### In plain terms

You can be fooled about a stick in water because the stick has two sides: how it looks and how it
is. But a feeling seems to have only one side — the way it feels *is* what it is. A pain that
merely "seemed" to hurt would just... hurt. If that's right, your awareness of a present feeling
can't be wrong the way a report can, because there's no hidden reality behind the feeling for the
report to miss — so inner awareness is direct contact
([introspective-acquaintance-is-datum](../claims/introspective-acquaintance-is-datum.md)), not testimony to be discounted
([introspection-is-fallible-representation](../claims/introspection-is-fallible-representation.md)). The skeptic's reply: maybe "how it feels" is
itself the brain's little summary — and summaries can be wrong about the machinery underneath. Then
the one-sided coin is an illusion too, and the argument has assumed what it set out to prove.
