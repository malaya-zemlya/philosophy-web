Raises analogy CQ3 (base) — see the [analogy pattern](../../patterns/analogy.md) — against the
[combining-subjects argument](subjects-can-combine.md) by challenging its base verdict. That argument
transfers a verdict from macroscopic cases of subjects merging and dividing to micro-composition,
and its flagship case is the split brain. The objection denies that the split brain is a case of a
subject *dividing* at all. On the evidence of Pinto and colleagues, callosotomy patients respond
accurately to stimuli in either visual half-field, and with either hand across the midline — a
profile better explained by one subject whose *perceptual access* is split than by two subjects each
locked to a hemisphere ([pinto-2017](../sources/pinto-2017.md); [split-brain-single-subject](../claims/split-brain-single-subject.md)).

### What it does and does not show

The attack lands on premise (1) of the combining argument,
[subjects-actually-merge-and-divide](../claims/subjects-actually-merge-and-divide.md), which cites
three case families — split-brain patients, craniopagus twins, and groups in deep synchrony. The
objection engages only the first, and only to *remove* it as secure support: if the split brain is
one subject with divided access, the argument's most-cited and most-studied instance of subject
division is no longer available, and the base verdict rests on the weaker remaining cases. This does
not refute the premise outright — the twin and group cases stand untouched, and the older Nagel
reading of bisection as genuine disunity ([nagel-1971](../sources/nagel-1971.md)) remains a live
interpretation — but per CQ3 an analogy is only as strong as its base, and the strongest leg of this
base is kicked away.

### The weakest premise

The soft point is the interpretive one: whether "accurate response across both fields with either
hand" really favours one subject over two. A defender of division can read the integrated responding
as an experimental artefact of testing conditions that permit cross-cueing, restoring the
two-subjects picture. The dispute is thus over what the behavioural data underdetermine — which is
precisely why the objection claims only that the single-subject reading is *at least as good*, enough
to unseat the split brain as a secure base case.

### In plain terms

The bigger argument says: we can watch subjects merge and split, so tiny minds merging into a big one
isn't so strange — and its headline example is the split-brain patient, whose severed hemispheres look
like two centres of experience. This objection says the headline example may be misread. Newer testing
shows these patients can still answer about things shown to either eye, using either hand — which looks
less like two people sharing a skull and more like one person whose *incoming information* is split
while the *subject* stays single. If the best case of a mind dividing turns out to be one mind with
divided input, the argument's strongest evidence goes soft.
