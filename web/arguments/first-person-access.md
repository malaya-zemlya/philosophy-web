This argument takes the everyday asymmetry of [first-person-access](../concepts/first-person-access.md) — that you are
directly acquainted with your own suffering but reach any other mind only by inference — and turns it
into a claim about *warrant*: you are entitled to count yourself as a bearer of moral status in a way
you are not yet entitled to count a machine. The inference is abductive and epistemic, concerning the
*grounds* of justified moral regard, not a deductive proof of what the machine is or lacks.

The reasoning runs in three steps:

1. ([privileged first-person access](../claims/privileged-first-person-access.md)) I have direct,
   non-inferential acquaintance with my own suffering.
2. ([acquaintance as moral-status condition](../claims/acquaintance-necessary-for-moral-status-warrant.md))
   Such acquaintance is necessary for being justified that there is "someone there to be wronged."
3. ([no acquaintance with machines](../claims/no-acquaintance-with-machine.md)) I have no such acquaintance
   with the machine — only inference.

Therefore ([access-based moral asymmetry](../claims/access-grounds-moral-status-asymmetry.md)) I am
warranted in regarding myself as a bearer of moral status in a way I am not warranted in regarding
the machine.

### The weakest premise

Premise 2 — [acquaintance as a moral-status
condition](../claims/acquaintance-necessary-for-moral-status-warrant.md) — is where the argument is most exposed, and why it stands as contested. Two worries
recur:

- *Over-generation (it proves too much).* If first-person acquaintance were genuinely necessary for
  warranted moral regard, it would equally deny me warrant about *other human beings*, since I have
  no acquaintance with their suffering either — collapsing the view into solipsism about moral
  status. This symmetric objection is lodged as [access-symmetry-rebuttal](access-symmetry-rebuttal.md).
- *The epistemic-to-metaphysical gap.* The premises concern my *justification*; the conclusion is
  sometimes overread as a claim about the machine's *actual* status. As written, the conclusion is
  carefully epistemic and makes no such metaphysical announcement.

### Provenance

The move is a problem-of-other-minds appeal applied to artificial minds. In the source dialogue
([lem](../sources/lem.md)'s pastiche lineage — *О правах непротеиновых существ*, "On the Rights of Non-Protein
Beings") it is the speaker Cleant's appeal, adopted on the proteocentrist side; it is imported here
as a node, not as a character. Through its conclusion it lends support to [proteocentrism](../positions/proteocentrism.md).

### In plain terms

You know your own suffering from the inside, directly — for you there's no doubt that *someone* is in
there hurting. With a machine, all you ever get is an outside guess: it behaves as if it suffers, but
you're never personally acquainted with any inner hurt. The argument: since that direct, inside
acquaintance is what really backs up the belief "there's a someone here who can be wronged," you're
entitled to count *yourself* as mattering morally in a way you're not equally entitled to count the
machine. Notice it's a claim about what you're *justified* in believing, not a flat announcement that
the machine is empty. Its main weakness — and why it's marked contested — is that the very same
reasoning seems to lock you out of other *people* too, since you have no inside access to their pain
either, threatening to leave you alone as the only being you can be sure matters.
