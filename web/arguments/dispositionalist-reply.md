The **dispositionalist reply** — the *straight solution* to Kripke's paradox — rejects the sceptical
conclusion at its second step. Where [kripke-rule-following](kripke-rule-following.md) eliminates *dispositions* as a
candidate meaning-constituting fact (they are finite, they include mistakes, and they are merely
descriptive), the dispositionalist answers that a *suitably refined* dispositional account survives all
three charges. If it does, there is after all a fact that constitutes meaning, contrary to
[no-constitutive-fact-of-meaning](../claims/no-constitutive-fact-of-meaning.md).

**Inference form:** rebuttal — an attack on one premise of the sceptical elimination.

1. **Finitude.** Kripke's objection assumes the constituting dispositions are the speaker's *actual,
   bounded* ones. Index meaning instead to dispositions under *optimal conditions* — unbounded memory
   and working time — and the finite ceiling that let "quus" in disappears.
2. **Error.** Mistakes are exactly the deviations optimal conditions screen off, so the idealized
   dispositions define the *correct* answer, not merely the likely one.
3. **Normativity.** What one is disposed to do under optimal conditions is what one *would* do; the
   dispositionalist argues this recovers the required "ought" — to mean plus is to be such that,
   corrected and idealized, one *would* answer 125.

∴ Meaning is constituted by (idealized) dispositions ([meaning-constituted-by-dispositions](../claims/meaning-constituted-by-dispositions.md)),
and the sceptical [no-constitutive-fact-of-meaning](../claims/no-constitutive-fact-of-meaning.md) fails. The account is endorsed for logical
vocabulary by Fodor ([fodor-1990](../sources/fodor-1990.md)).

**Weakest premise:** step 3, the recovery of *normativity*. Kripke's deepest objection is that no
*descriptive* fact about what one would do — however idealized — yields what one *ought* to do. Worse,
selecting the "optimal" conditions that screen off exactly the errors seems to presuppose the very
correctness facts the account was meant to constitute, so the idealization threatens to be either
circular or to smuggle the norm in from outside. Critics add that the open-ended ceteris paribus
clauses and idealizations (unbounded memory) depart from any respectable scientific notion of a
disposition, leaving the constituting fact unexplained rather than exhibited.

### In plain terms

Kripke says your habits can't fix what you mean, because real habits run out and are full of slips.
The dispositionalist answers: don't look at your sloppy habits, look at what you'd do *if* you had
endless memory and never goofed — under those clean conditions you would add, not "quadd," and that
settles that you mean plus. The catch: to say which conditions count as "clean," you seem to need to
already know the right answers — the very thing in dispute. So the reply works only if it can pin down
the ideal conditions without secretly assuming what you mean.
