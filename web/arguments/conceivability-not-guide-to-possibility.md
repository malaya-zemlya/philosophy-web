Conceivability is not, in general, a reliable guide to metaphysical possibility — so the bridge the
zombie argument leans on cannot simply be assumed. The objection turns a celebrated result in the
metaphysics of modality against the [zombie-conceivability](zombie-conceivability.md): since Saul Kripke's work on
naming, philosophers have widely accepted that some truths are *necessary* yet knowable only *a
posteriori*, and in every such case a coherent-seeming scenario is conceivable even though it is
metaphysically impossible.

The move is a counterexample to a general principle:

1. ([some-necessities-knowable-only-a-posteriori](../claims/some-necessities-knowable-only-a-posteriori.md), from Kripke's *Naming and Necessity*
   [kripke-1980](../sources/kripke-1980.md)) There are necessary truths knowable only a posteriori — water is H₂O, heat is
   molecular motion, Hesperus is Phosphorus — whose negations are conceivable yet metaphysically
   impossible.

∴ Conceivability does not in general entail possibility: [conceivability-entails-possibility](../claims/conceivability-entails-possibility.md)
fails as a universal bridge.

Applied to consciousness, this is the **type-B physicalist** strategy ([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md)): a
phenomenal–physical identity ("pain *is* such-and-such neural state") could itself be a *strong* a
posteriori necessity. If so, a [philosophical-zombie](../concepts/philosophical-zombie.md) is conceivable but metaphysically
impossible, and the [zombie-conceivability](zombie-conceivability.md) is cut at its bridge — without conceding any
non-physical fact.

### The crux: is consciousness like water?

The **weakest premise** is the tacit one: that the *phenomenal* case is relevantly like the *water*
case. The two-dimensional rejoinder ([modal-rationalism](modal-rationalism.md), built on
[aposteriori-necessities-are-misdescribed-possibilities](../claims/aposteriori-necessities-are-misdescribed-possibilities.md)) replies that every Kripkean necessity
is a *misdescribed possibility*, and that for phenomenal terms the primary and secondary intensions
*coincide* — there is no appearance/reality wedge to exploit — so the water-style escape is unavailable
for zombies. Whether the phenomenal is genuinely disanalogous to water is where the dispute lives.

This should not be confused with [gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts](gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts.md): that explains away the
*appearance* of an explanatory gap through the architecture of phenomenal concepts; the present move
makes the purely *modal* point that a-posteriori necessities already break conceivability→possibility,
independently of any account of phenomenal concepts.

### In plain terms

The whole zombie argument needs the rule "if you can coherently imagine it, it's really possible." This
objection says that rule is just false. Take "water is H₂O": for centuries people could coherently
imagine water turning out to be something other than H₂O — yet it is impossible, because water *just is*
H₂O, a fact we had to discover rather than deduce. So imaginability isn't proof of possibility. Maybe
consciousness is the same: a zombie feels imaginable only because we haven't yet seen that experience
*just is* some physical process. The standard comeback, due to David Chalmers ([chalmers-1996](../sources/chalmers-1996.md)),
is that the water trick works only because "water" hides a surface/essence gap — and feelings have no
such gap, so the trick cannot be repeated for zombies.
