The *dancing qualia* argument ([chalmers-1995b](../sources/chalmers-1995b.md); [chalmers-1996](../sources/chalmers-1996.md), ch. 7) is the sharp
edge of Chalmers's case for [organizational-invariance](../claims/organizational-invariance.md). Where [fading
qualia](fading-qualia.md) targets *absent* qualia, this targets *inverted* qualia: the supposition that a perfect functional
duplicate could experience blue where you experience red. It is a descendant of the inverted-spectrum
tradition (Shoemaker's argument that a *functionally undetectable* inversion is incoherent), upgraded with a
dynamic switching device.

### The argument

The inference is a **thought-experiment reductio**.

1. *The device.* Suppose a silicon isomorph of your visual system has *inverted* qualia but identical
   functional organization. Wire that silicon circuit into your brain in parallel with the biological
   original, with a **switch** selecting which one drives the rest of you. Because both are exact functional
   duplicates, throwing the switch changes *nothing* functional — same downstream activity, same judgements,
   same behaviour.
2. *The dance.* By the inversion hypothesis, the two circuits ground *different* experiences. So toggling
   the switch would make your colour experience *flip* — red to blue and back — your qualia literally
   *dancing* as the switch is thrown.
3. *The absurdity.* Yet because functional organization is held fixed throughout, you would notice nothing,
   report nothing, believe nothing has changed: you would sincerely judge "my experience is perfectly
   stable" while it flips before your mind's eye. Your introspective access to your own experience would be
   not just mistaken but *dramatically, dynamically* severed from it.

∴ That total divorce of experience from one's knowledge of it is incredible; reject the premise that
generated it. A functional isomorph *cannot* have inverted (or absent) qualia, so experience is fixed by
fine-grained organization: [organizational-invariance](../claims/organizational-invariance.md), and hence
[substrate-independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md) for the phenomenal.

### The weakest premise

As with fading qualia, the load is borne by the assumption that *introspective judgement is held fixed by
the functional duplication* — that "I notice no change" is settled purely by occupying the right causal node.
That is precisely what an opponent who takes introspection to be *acquaintance* with the quale denies: on
that view, when the quale flips the judgement flips with it, so either the switch produces a *noticed* change
(no absurdity, and no dancing-without-detection) or the construction has covertly stipulated a *functional
zombie-introspector*, assuming functionalism about introspection to refute anti-functionalism about
experience. This is the question-begging charge of [dancing-qualia-begs-the-question](dancing-qualia-begs-the-question.md) and
[dancing-qualia-presupposes-functional-introspection](../claims/dancing-qualia-presupposes-functional-introspection.md). The argument is abductive, not a proof; and
note its delicate coexistence with Chalmers's own [phenomenal-residue](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md) and
[zombie conceivability](../claims/zombie-conceivable.md) — it claims *nomological* determination of qualia by
organization, not metaphysical constitution, and critics question whether that pairing is stable.

### How it is used in the web

Functionalists press this argument *past* Chalmers's own intentions — as positive support for *strong*
functionalism about the phenomenal — in [gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts](gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts.md), which deploys the
dancing-qualia move to argue that strong functionalism predicts the explanatory gap. The standing reply,
[dancing-qualia-begs-the-question](dancing-qualia-begs-the-question.md), answers *that* deployment at the same pressure point named
above.

### In plain terms

Imagine a silicon copy of your visual system that (the opponent says) sees blue where you see red, but works
exactly like yours. Wire it in alongside the real thing with a switch. Because the two are functionally
identical, flipping the switch changes nothing you do or say — but if they really host different experiences,
your colours would jump red-blue-red every time it's flipped. So your experience would be *dancing*, and
you'd be completely oblivious — calmly reporting "looks totally stable" while it flips. That's so absurd,
Chalmers says, that the opponent's premise must be wrong: a copy wired exactly like you must have *your*
experiences, red for red. So what your experience is depends on the wiring pattern, not the material — and a
brain-like machine would feel as you do. The catch is the same as before: it assumes your "I notice no
change" is locked by the wiring; if you instead judge by directly meeting your experience, the flip would be
noticed and the paradox dissolves.
