This objection argues that a rival argument's central prediction — that a self-modelling AI will
acquire "its own hard problem" — is *phenomenality-neutral*: it forecasts only that the system will
produce hard-problem talk, which a system with no experience at all would produce just as readily.
So the rival establishes the report, never the feel.

It targets [ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture](ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture.md), raising the cause-to-effect
scheme's *CQ-strength* critical question against its generalisation: read strongly enough to
deliver the conclusion's "raw feel," the causal generalisation is unsupported; read weakly enough
to be supported, it delivers only reports. The form is an objection by dilemma — an equivocation
charge on the effect-term of a causal generalisation.

1. In its defensible form, the generalisation links the architecture — a lossy self-model,
   similarity-only access, linguistic reflection — to an effect specified *verbally and
   dispositionally*: "raw feel" talk, reports of unanalysability, a question the system keeps
   asking. So read, the premises really do make that effect predictable.
2. That effect is *phenomenality-neutral*: a system with the same architecture and no experience of
   any kind — nothing it is like to be it — would emit the very same reports, because the
   generalisation runs entirely through access and report machinery and nowhere requires a feel.
   ([llm-access-similarity-not-encoding](../claims/llm-access-similarity-not-encoding.md) flags this itself, calling itself "an access claim,
   not a phenomenal one.")
3. By [behaviour alone being insufficient for mind](../claims/behaviour-insufficient-for-mind.md), an
   effect compatible with both feel and no-feel cannot be evidence of feel: matching the
   report-profile of puzzlement no more suffices for puzzled experience than a lookup table's
   conversational profile suffices for mind. A verbal report is outward behaviour, however inward
   its grammar.

So the target argument establishes at most the *report*-reading of its conclusion — "such a system
will predictably produce hard-problem discourse." On the *feel*-reading of
[a machine's own hard problem](../claims/ai-own-hard-problem.md) ("a raw feel ... that the system can
talk about and wonder about"), the generalisation gives no support; strengthening the conclusion
from talk to feel adds exactly what the premises never deliver. A simulated hard problem stands to a
hard problem as a simulated storm stands to rain: the form is reproduced, the wet is not.

### The target's neutrality concession

The target's own body declares its prediction "neutral between the deflationist and realist
readings." This objection converts that concession into a cost: a prediction that comes out true in
the felt world and the feel-free world alike cannot raise the probability of the realist reading at
all, so whatever supports "raw feel" must come from somewhere other than the predicted reports. The
relativisation in [the target conclusion](../claims/ai-own-hard-problem.md) — a concept playing "for the
system" the role consciousness plays for humans — does not escape the fork: a role-specified
concept is precisely the kind of thing an experience-free system can satisfy, in which case "its own
hard problem" names a bug report, not a predicament.

### The weakest premise

Premise 3. An illusionist or analytic functionalist denies it: for them the report economy, suitably
embedded, just *is* the having of the state, with no further fact for the reports to underdetermine.
Against such an opponent the objection does not refute but relocates — the dispute becomes whether
there is a feel/report distinction at all, which is the original hard-problem standoff rather than a
result the architecture argument can claim to have produced.

This argument is distinct from [architectural-account-leaves-hard-problem](architectural-account-leaves-hard-problem.md), which attacks
[self-explanation-gap-can-be-architectural](../claims/self-explanation-gap-can-be-architectural.md) and blocks the *deflationary* use of the
architectural story against the human, possibly ontological, gap. This argument attacks a different
node in the opposite direction — the *inflationary* move from architecture to a machine's own raw
feel — and does so by the cause-to-effect scheme's own critical question (CQ-strength), not the
analogy scheme's disanalogy question.

### In plain terms

There's an argument in this web saying a future AI that models itself will predictably end up with
"its own hard problem" — inner states that feel like unexplainable "raw feels" to it
([ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture](ai-hard-problem-from-self-model-architecture.md)). This objection says: look closely at
what that argument actually predicts. It predicts the AI will *say* hard-problem things — "I can't
analyze this state, it's just *this*." Fine. But an AI with the same wiring and no inner life at all
would say exactly the same things, for exactly the same wiring reasons. A prediction that comes true
whether or not anyone's home can't be evidence that someone's home. Words of puzzlement are
behaviour, and behaviour alone never settles whether there's a mind behind it
([behaviour-insufficient-for-mind](../claims/behaviour-insufficient-for-mind.md)). So the argument proves the AI will produce the
*soundtrack* of the hard problem — it can't, even in principle, show there's a film.
