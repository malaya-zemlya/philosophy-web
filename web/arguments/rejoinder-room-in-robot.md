Searle's rejoinder to the [Robot Reply](robot-reply.md). Grant the robot its cameras, limbs, and
causal traffic with the world — then put the man inside its "head," receiving the strings of symbols that
come in from the sensors and producing the strings that drive the motors, all by the same shape-matching
rulebook. The man knows nothing of where the symbols come from or what they do; to him they are still
meaningless squiggles. Adding perceptual and motor causal links changes what the symbols *do*, but not what
they *mean to the man* — so the grounding, Searle concludes, does not inject understanding after all.

The move `supports` A3 ([syntax-insufficient-for-semantics](../claims/syntax-insufficient-for-semantics.md)): causal embedding supplies more
syntax-driven behaviour, not [original intentionality](../concepts/intrinsic-derived-intentionality.md).

### The live crux

The rejoinder is widely thought to *miss* the Robot Reply rather than meet it, because the reply never
claimed the *man* understands — it claimed the *robot* does, in virtue of the system-level causal commerce
the man is merely a cog in. So the rejoinder appears to smuggle the [Systems
Reply](systems-reply.md)'s level confusion back in: of course the homunculus doesn't understand; the question was whether the
grounded system does. Whether causal grounding *constitutes* meaning or merely *accompanies* it is left
genuinely open.

### In plain terms

The Robot Reply said: wire the program to a body and its symbols finally mean something. Searle climbs
inside the robot: he sits in its head shuffling the very symbols that flow from its eyes to its hands, and
he *still* has no idea any of it means "cup" or "wall." So, he says, bolting on a body didn't create
understanding. The standard comeback: but nobody said *the man in the head* understands — the claim was
that the *robot as a whole* does, and Searle keeps insisting on interrogating the little man instead of the
robot.
