A Dennettian defence of [llm-workspace-from-functional-signature](llm-workspace-from-functional-signature.md) against its own
recorded weakest point. The forgeability worry there runs: the Jacobian lens is *defined* by
verbalizability, so a verbalizability-shaped probe finding reportable representations proves little
— the [J-space](../concepts/j-space.md) might be a signature of language-bound computation rather than of
genuine workspace organization. This rejoinder observes that the worry has a suppressed premise:
that verbal poise is merely a *sign* of some further workspace fact, which the sign might miss or
counterfeit.

1. Conscious contents are those that achieve widespread cerebral influence, especially over verbal
   report ([consciousness-is-cerebral-celebrity](../claims/consciousness-is-cerebral-celebrity.md)) — being poised to drive report and
   flexible behaviour is not evidence of conscious access but what conscious access *consists in*.
∴ An instrument that identifies representations by their poise for verbal report is not begging the
question against a hidden workspace fact; it is measuring the constitutive property directly, so
the forgeability objection lapses ([llm-workspace-from-functional-signature](llm-workspace-from-functional-signature.md) is
supported).

The inference form is deductive given the premise: if celebrity-with-report-influence is
constitutive rather than symptomatic, there is no gap between the sign and the state for a forgery
to slip through. The weakest premise is plainly premise 1 itself — the whole weight rests on
Dennett's deflationary identification, and anyone who rejects cerebral celebrity as an account of
conscious access (a phenomenal realist, a higher-order theorist, or a global-workspace theorist who
takes broadcast rather than report-influence as constitutive) will regard the rejoinder as merely
relocating the dispute. It also defends only against the circularity reading of forgeability; the
architectural objection (feedforward broadcast is not ignition) is untouched.

### In plain terms

One objection to the claim that AI language models have a "mental workspace" is that the detector
was rigged: it was built to find "sayable" representations, so of course it found reportable ones.
This reply, in the spirit of the philosopher Daniel Dennett's view that consciousness is just
[fame in the brain](../claims/consciousness-is-cerebral-celebrity.md), says the objection assumes there
is some deeper hidden fact the detector might have missed. If being poised to be talked about *is*
the phenomenon — rather than a mere symptom of it — then building the detector around sayability
was not a rigging but a bullseye. The catch: you have to buy Dennett's view first.
