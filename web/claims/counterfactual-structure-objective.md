Facts about what a system *would* do under interventions are objective features of the world, not
artifacts of an observer's chosen description. Whether intervening on X would change Y is settled by
the world, not chosen — this is the *interventionist* account of causation, on which a cause is
something that, were it manipulated, would shift its effect ([woodward-2003](../sources/woodward-2003.md)).

### Scope

The claim concerns the objectivity of counterfactual-causal facts *once a set of variables is
fixed*. It does not claim that the *choice of variables* is itself observer-independent — that gap is
exactly what [the underdetermination
claim](counterfactual-structure-underdetermines-decomposition.md) exploits.

### Who would deny it

A thoroughgoing observer-relativist about causation and computation — Searle's "syntax is not
intrinsic to physics" ([searle-1990b](../sources/searle-1990b.md)), read strongly.

It is distinct from [the claim that
implementation requires counterfactual structure](implementation-requires-counterfactual-structure.md): that says implementation *needs* counterfactual
structure, whereas this says such structure, once the variables are fixed, is *objective*.

### In plain terms

Whether poking a system *here* would change what happens *there* is a fact about the world, not
something an observer just decides. These "what would happen if…" facts are real features of how the
system is wired, the same for everyone who looks. (Fine print: this holds *once you've agreed what
counts as the system's parts* — which way to carve it up in the first place is a separate, thornier
question.)
