On *semantic dispositionalism*, the fact that a speaker means addition by "+" — or *cat* by "cat" —
just is a fact about their *dispositions*: they are disposed to answer with the sum, to apply "cat"
to cats, and so on. Meaning is not a hidden occurrent state but a pattern of use the speaker is set
up to produce. To meet the objection that raw dispositions are finite and shot through with error,
the sophisticated version indexes the constituting dispositions to *idealized* or *optimal*
conditions — unbounded memory, no performance slips — so that what one means is what one *would* do
were those conditions met.

The thesis is that dispositions, so idealized, *constitute* meaning; it does not claim we could list
them, only that they fix the fact of the matter. It is defended for logical vocabulary by Fodor
([fodor-1990](../sources/fodor-1990.md)), and is the "straight solution" the rule-following sceptic ([kripke-1982](../sources/kripke-1982.md))
sets up and rejects. It is denied by Kripke's Wittgenstein — the [kripke-rule-following](../arguments/kripke-rule-following.md)
and its conclusion [no-constitutive-fact-of-meaning](no-constitutive-fact-of-meaning.md) — on the ground that even idealized
dispositions are descriptive and so miss the *normativity* of meaning.

### In plain terms

What you mean by a word is just what you're set up to do with it: you mean *plus* because you're
disposed to give sums. Since your real habits run out and include mistakes, the careful version says:
look at what you'd do with perfect memory and no slips. That idealized habit, the dispositionalist
says, is what fixes your meaning.
