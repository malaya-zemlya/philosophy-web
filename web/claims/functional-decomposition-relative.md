A physical system has no unique functional decomposition. Which physical states count as inputs and
which as outputs, where the system's boundary falls, and how the underlying microstates are grouped
into "the same" functional state — all of these can be fixed in many equally admissible ways, each
yielding a *different* functional organisation. Functional or computational individuation is
therefore description- or observer-relative ([searle-1990b](../sources/searle-1990b.md)); in the limit, Putnam's result
that an ordinary open system implements many automata under suitable mappings ([putnam-1988](../sources/putnam-1988.md)).

### Scope

The claim is about the *individuation* of functional states, prior to any verdict about minds. It
does not say every carving is equally *useful or natural*, only that the physics alone singles out
none as the system's *real* organisation. It is distinct from grain: [grain](../concepts/grain.md)
concerns the *resolution* of a role along one scale, whereas this concerns the *non-uniqueness of
the carving itself*, even at a fixed resolution.

### Who would deny it

A functionalist who holds that objective counterfactual/causal structure fixes a privileged
decomposition — so that only carvings respecting genuine causal dependencies count
([chalmers-1996b](../sources/chalmers-1996b.md)). That is the live reply to the
[decomposition-indeterminacy objection](../arguments/decomposition-indeterminacy.md).

It is distinct from [the claim that role leaves qualia out](phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md): this says
role-talk does not even fix a determinate system — a prior, more general worry that applies to
intentional states too.

### In plain terms

There's no single fact about which bits of a physical system are its "inputs," which are "outputs,"
where it stops, or how to lump its tiny states into "the same" working part — you can do it in many
equally legitimate ways, each giving a different "organization." So describing something by its
function is partly a matter of how you choose to look at it, not something read straight off the
physics. (This is the strong version of the worry; a common reply is that real cause-and-effect does
pin down one correct carving after all.)
