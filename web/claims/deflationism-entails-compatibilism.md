If the consciousness and free-will debates are mirror images
([consciousness-freewill-mirror](consciousness-freewill-mirror.md)), then positions travel across the mirror as packages: the
deflationist who says the zombie's functional duplicate lacks nothing
([illusionism](../positions/illusionism.md), [type-a-materialism](../positions/type-a-materialism.md)) is saying, in translation, exactly what
the [compatibilist](../positions/compatibilism.md) says about the volitional zombie
([zombie-dualizes-to-volitional-zombie](zombie-dualizes-to-volitional-zombie.md)). Holding deflationism about phenomenality while
rejecting compatibilism is therefore an unstable combination — its holder owes an account of which
dictionary entry fails, some relevant disanalogy that breaks the parity at just that point.

The commitment is conditional on the mirror being real, and that conditionality is essential: the
parity argument cannot *establish* the mirror it presupposes without circularity. Its force is
diagnostic — it converts the duality from a curiosity into a constraint on position-space, linking
commitments across two literatures that mostly ignore each other. Who would deny it: anyone who
locates a principled disanalogy between phenomenality and origination (for instance, that
phenomenality is a datum while origination is a theory), thereby breaking parity exactly where the
dictionary needs it.

### In plain terms

Some philosophers dismiss the "inner glow" of consciousness as a kind of user illusion — if a robot
copy of you acts exactly like you, nothing important is missing. This claim says such philosophers
can't consistently *also* insist that free will requires some extra spark beyond ordinary
deliberation. The two dismissals are the same move in two debates
([zombie-dualizes-to-volitional-zombie](zombie-dualizes-to-volitional-zombie.md)), so making one while refusing the other requires
explaining what breaks the symmetry.
