Why is there *one* subject rather than a committee of streams? Arrow-reversed, the question becomes:
why *one* will rather than a parliament of competing sub-agents? The unity of consciousness — the
co-presence of many contents in a single experiential perspective (Nagel,
[1971](../sources/nagel-1971.md)) — dualizes to the unity of agency, the convergence of many motives on a
single course of action. And the *failures* pair as neatly as the successes: breakdowns of input-side
binding ([binding-problem](../concepts/binding-problem.md)) mirror akrasia, Plato's tripartite soul
([Republic IV](../sources/plato-republic.md)), and the Augustinian divided will
([Confessions VIII](../sources/augustine-confessions.md)) on the output side — cases where the streams
are present but the *one* fails to form.

In arrow terms — the arrows being directed dependences among states, the diagram convention fixed
in [the duality entry](../concepts/consciousness-freewill-duality.md) — the unity of consciousness
concerns the *in-fan*: many channel-arrows converging on a single experiential apex, and the puzzle
of why there is one apex rather than several. Reversing the arrows yields the *out-fan*: a single
volitional apex issuing command-arrows, and the puzzle of why one issuing point rather than a
committee. The pathologies are the fans fragmenting — features that fail to bind on the input
side, sub-agents issuing conflicting commands on the output side.

The claim asserts the structural correspondence of the two unity problems, not any particular
solution to either. It is a dictionary entry of the
[consciousness–free-will duality](../concepts/consciousness-freewill-duality.md), among those where
arrow-reversal lands on a problem the other literature already owns under its own name — evidence
counted by [consciousness-freewill-duality](../arguments/consciousness-freewill-duality.md).

### In plain terms

Your senses deliver many streams, yet you experience one world as one self — and when that
integration fails, philosophy calls it a problem about the *unity of consciousness*. Your motives
pull in many directions, yet normally one decision emerges — and when *that* fails, we call it weak
will, or being "of two minds," a puzzle as old as Plato's image of the soul as reason, spirit, and
appetite. This claim says the two puzzles are the same puzzle, one about inputs, the other about
outputs.
