Behavioural evidence from callosotomy patients can be read as showing not two subjects but one
subject whose *perceptual access* is divided. In Pinto and colleagues' experiments, patients
could respond accurately to a stimulus presented in either visual half-field, and could do so
with either hand, across the midline — a pattern hard to reconcile with two independent centres
of awareness each confined to one hemisphere, and naturally read as a single subject with
split perception ([pinto-2017](../sources/pinto-2017.md)).

The claim is deliberately narrow. It concerns the split-brain case specifically, and asserts
that this case is at least as well explained by "one subject, divided access" as by "two
subjects" — not that the two-subjects reading is refuted outright, and not anything about
craniopagus twins or group synchrony. Its role is to remove the split brain as a secure instance
of a subject dividing, which is how the flagship example is used elsewhere in the web
([subjects-actually-merge-and-divide](subjects-actually-merge-and-divide.md)). The older
reading, on which bisection yields genuinely disunified consciousness, traces to Nagel
([nagel-1971](../sources/nagel-1971.md)).
