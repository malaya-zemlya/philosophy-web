Unborrowed aboutness comes from *experience*: there is a kind of intentionality, pervasive in
human mental life, that is constitutively determined by phenomenal character — by what the state is
like for its subject — and all other intentionality is derivative from it (Horgan & Tienson,
[2002](../sources/horgan-tienson-2002.md)). A conscious thought about coffee is intrinsically,
determinately about coffee because of how thinking it *feels*; states of systems with no
phenomenology have at best the derived, reader-conferred aboutness of ink on a page
([intrinsic-derived-intentionality](../concepts/intrinsic-derived-intentionality.md)). The order of explanation is thus the reverse of
tracking and teleological theories: consciousness grounds content, not external relations. A
kindred thought is Searle's connection principle — intentionality is only ever the intentionality
of an actual or potential conscious state ([1992](../sources/searle-1992.md)).

**Scope.** The claim concerns *original* intentionality and its ground; it does not deny that
causal and biological relations help fix the *reference* of thoughts about the external world, nor
does it say which theory of consciousness is true. It is denied by
[teleosemanticists](../positions/teleosemantics.md) and other externalist naturalizers, for whom content
needs no experiential ground, and by [interpretivists](../positions/intentional-stance.md), who deflate
the original intentionality it sets out to explain. It bears directly on AI: if it is true, then a
system with no phenomenal consciousness — however fluent — has only derived intentionality, so the
[problem of intentionality](../questions/intentionality.md) and the question of machine experience stand
or fall together.

### In plain terms

Your thought about coffee is *about* coffee in a way ink and pixels never are on their own. What
makes the difference? This claim's answer: consciousness. Because there is something it is like to
think the thought — a felt graspiness to meaning it — the thought points at coffee all by itself,
no outside reader needed. Meaning starts with experience, and everything else (words, maps,
databases) borrows from conscious minds. The flip side: a machine with no inner experience wouldn't
truly mean anything by its sentences, no matter how good they sound.
