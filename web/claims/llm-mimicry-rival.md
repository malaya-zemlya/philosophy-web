A large language model is trained to predict the next token over a vast corpus of human text.
That objective rewards reproducing the *statistical form* of how people talk about minds —
their reports of belief, desire, and feeling — regardless of whether the system occupies the
functional roles those words normally track. So there is a standing rival explanation of an
LLM's mind-like behaviour: high-fidelity distributional imitation of mind-talk, rather than
realisation of the mental states the talk expresses ([bender-2021](../sources/bender-2021.md)).
The rival turns on the form/meaning distinction — mastery of linguistic form underdetermines
the grasp of meaning that relates form to world and intent ([bender-koller-2020](../sources/bender-koller-2020.md)).

The claim asserts that this rival is *available and antecedently likely given the training
objective*, not that it is established as the correct explanation in any given case. Its force is
comparative: because the objective actively selects for form-matching, an argument that infers
mental states as the *best* explanation of the behaviour must discharge this rival rather than
pass it over. It is distinct from the lookup-table objection, which says the behaviour *could in
principle* be produced mindlessly by brute storage; here the point is that a specific mechanism
known to be operating in actual systems already predicts the behaviour.
