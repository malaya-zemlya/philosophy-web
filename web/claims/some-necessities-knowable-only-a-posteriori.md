Some truths are *metaphysically necessary* — they could not have been otherwise, in any possible
world — yet they can be known only by empirical investigation, never by reasoning from the
armchair. The standard examples are theoretical identities: "water is H₂O," "heat is molecular
motion," "Hesperus is Phosphorus" (the evening star is the morning star). Each was a discovery,
established by looking at the world; yet, Saul Kripke argued, once such an identity is true it is
*necessarily* true ([kripke-1980](../sources/kripke-1980.md)).

The striking consequence is that for such a truth the *negation is conceivable* while being
metaphysically *impossible*. One can coherently seem to imagine water that is not H₂O — some
watery liquid drawn from rivers and rain that turns out to have a different chemistry — even
though, water being H₂O, no such liquid is genuinely possible. So conceivability and possibility
come apart, at least here: an *a posteriori necessity* is a truth that is necessary but knowable
only after experience.

This is a claim in modal metaphysics and epistemology, widely accepted after Kripke. It
establishes that conceivability is *not in general* an infallible guide to possibility. It does
not by itself say *how wide* the gap is, nor whether the phenomenal case — the conceivability of
zombies and the like — falls on the water side of the line.

Who would deny it: a descriptivist who denies that there are any genuine a posteriori
necessities; or anyone who reduces metaphysical possibility to epistemic or conceptual
possibility outright.

It stands as the standard counterexample to the bridge from conceivability to possibility
([conceivability-entails-possibility](conceivability-entails-possibility.md)), and it is the very class of case that the
two-dimensionalist *reinterprets* rather than accepts at face value
([aposteriori-necessities-are-misdescribed-possibilities](aposteriori-necessities-are-misdescribed-possibilities.md)).

### In plain terms

Some facts had to be true no matter what — there's no possible way the world could have made them
false — and yet the only way to *find them out* is to do science, not to think hard in an armchair.
"Water is H₂O" is the classic case: water simply *is* H₂O, so nothing that wasn't H₂O could really be
water; but no amount of pure reasoning tells you that — chemists had to discover it. The lesson: being
able to *imagine* something (water that isn't H₂O) doesn't prove it's truly *possible*. So imagination
is not a reliable map of what could really be.
