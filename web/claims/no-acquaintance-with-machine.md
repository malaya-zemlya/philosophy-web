Whatever it is (if anything) like to be a machine, one has no direct acquaintance with it. At most
one can infer it from the machine's behaviour or construction; the inside view — the lived access a
subject has to its own states ([first-person-access](../concepts/first-person-access.md)) — is unavailable from the outside.

This is a claim about *my* epistemic position toward the machine, stated from the human side. It does
not assert that the machine has no experience, nor that the machine lacks first-person access to its
own states; it asserts only that I do not share in whatever access there may be. As stated, virtually
no one denies it — the disputes are all about what *follows* from it.

What follows is exactly where it joins the larger debate. The [access-symmetry
rebuttal](../arguments/access-symmetry-rebuttal.md) turns on the fact that this non-acquaintance is reciprocal and fully general rather than a
special deficit of machines. And the claim is distinct from
[acquaintance-necessary-for-moral-status-warrant](acquaintance-necessary-for-moral-status-warrant.md): that one supplies the necessity principle
(acquaintance is required for warranted moral status), whereas this supplies the factual premise to
which such a principle would be applied.

### In plain terms

Whatever a machine may or may not feel inside, you have no direct line to it — you can only guess from
how it behaves or how it's built. That much is almost undeniable; the real fights are all about what
*follows* from it. (Note what it does *not* say: it doesn't claim the machine feels nothing, only that
*you* can't reach that feeling from the inside.)
