To *state* the hard problem from the inside — to point at one's phenomenal-seeming states on one
side, the physical or functional story on the other, and judge the two irreconcilable — is itself a
cognitive act. Like any cognitive act, it can only operate on contents the system has access to. So
merely *posing* the gap requires *access consciousness* of at least a representation of the
phenomenal relatum: you cannot point at, compare, or puzzle over what you have no cognitive access
to.

This is a claim about the preconditions for *formulating* the [hard problem](../concepts/hard-problem.md),
not the far stronger thesis that phenomenal consciousness itself requires access consciousness. On
Block's view ([block-2007](../sources/block-2007.md)) the two come apart:
[phenomenal states may overflow access](../concepts/access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md), so a system
could *have* phenomenal character it cannot fully bring before the mind. The point here is only that
the act of *raising* the gap — as opposed to merely undergoing experience — is access-dependent.

It would be denied by someone who holds that the explanatory gap is registered non-cognitively, as a
felt shortfall requiring no representation of the phenomenal relatum; or by someone who collapses the
access/phenomenal distinction, so that "pointing at the phenomenal" is not a further,
access-dependent step.

Distinct from [phenomenal-concept-isolation-tradeoff](phenomenal-concept-isolation-tradeoff.md) and
[the phenomenal-concept overgeneration objection](../arguments/phenomenal-concept-overgeneration.md), which
concern whether a *phenomenal concept* can be at once informative enough to ground self-knowledge and
isolated enough to deflate the gap; this is the prior, weaker point that *some* access to the
phenomenal relatum is needed merely to pose the gap at all. (Flagged for `/reconcile`: the two are
kin, and a human should check they are not collapsible.)

### In plain terms

The "hard problem" is the puzzle of why physical brain activity is accompanied by inner experience
at all. This claim makes a modest observation: just to *raise* that puzzle about yourself — to notice
your own feelings, hold them up against the brain story, and find them mismatched — your mind has to
get hold of those feelings in the first place. You cannot wonder about something you have no mental
access to. It does not say you need that access in order to *have* experiences; only in order to
think or talk about the puzzle they pose.
