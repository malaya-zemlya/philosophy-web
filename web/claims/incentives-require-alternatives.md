An incentive works by moving an agent from the path she would otherwise have taken onto a different
one: the threat of punishment deters *because* refraining was available, the promise of reward
attracts *because* pursuing it was one option among others. So the very machinery of influence
presupposes that more than one course of action is genuinely open to the agent
([alternatives-genuinely-open](alternatives-genuinely-open.md)). Where a single continuation is fixed — where the agent
*could not* have taken any other path — reward and punishment are idle: no matter how much we praise,
blame, fine, or revenge, a course already settled cannot be redirected, and the incentive changes
nothing but the ledger.

The observation matters most for [consequentialist-responsibility](../positions/consequentialist-responsibility.md), the forward-looking
theory of moral responsibility on which holding someone responsible is not a verdict about the
metaphysics of a past act but a **tool** others use to shape future conduct — the practice of
blaming, praising, punishing, rewarding, shunning, and the rest is justified by its power to
influence how the actor will act (Schlick, [1939](../sources/schlick-1939.md)). That justification is only as good as the influence
it claims, and this claim locates the influence's precondition: the practices can steer behaviour
only over an agent for whom alternatives are really available to be steered among. Leeway thus earns
a *practical* keep, not merely the metaphysical one it has in the free-will question
([free-will](../questions/free-will.md)) — it is a condition on the efficacy of the responsibility system, not only
on desert.

A sharp corollary falls on [hard-determinism](../positions/hard-determinism.md). Having denied that anyone ever has open
alternatives, the hard determinist typically rescues the practices with exactly this forward-looking
justification — punishment for deterrence and reform rather than desert. If this claim holds, that
escape is in tension with its own premise: an agent with no genuine alternatives is, by the same
token, an agent whose behaviour the incentive cannot redirect.

### Scope

The claim is contested at the seam between two readings of "open alternatives." A **compatibilist**
will grant that incentives require the agent's conduct to be *counterfactually sensitive* to them —
that had the incentive differed, the behaviour would have differed — while denying that this needs
the metaphysical openness of [alternatives-genuinely-open](alternatives-genuinely-open.md), on which the past and the laws
leave the future genuinely unfixed. Indeed a fully deterministic agent whose actions are a reliable
function of the incentives she faces is the *ideal* target of deterrence, not a counter-example to
it: determinism, on this reply, underwrites the efficacy of influence rather than undermining it. So
the claim as stated — that *genuine leeway* is the precondition — is denied by the compatibilist,
who substitutes reasons-responsiveness; it is affirmed by those who hold that a course the world has
already fixed is, in the sense that matters for steering, not really available at all.

### In plain terms

Rewards and punishments only do anything if the person could actually go more than one way. Threaten
a fine to stop someone parking on the kerb and it works only because *not* parking there was a real
option for them; if they truly had no choice, the fine punishes but changes nothing. This is why it
matters for a certain view of blame and praise — the view that says the whole point of holding
people responsible isn't to settle some cosmic scorecard about the past, but to *nudge* how they and
others behave next time ([Schlick's](../sources/schlick-1939.md) idea). That nudging needs room to nudge:
if the person's path was fixed all along, there was nothing to redirect. (Not everyone agrees the
room has to be the deep metaphysical kind — some say it's enough that the person *responds* to
carrots and sticks, which even a fully predictable person can do.)
