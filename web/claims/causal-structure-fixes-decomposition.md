Objective causal structure, this claim says, does not merely constrain how a physical system may be
carved into functional parts — it singles out one privileged carving as the system's real functional
organisation. The observer-independent invariant that a [decomposition-indeterminacy worry](../arguments/decomposition-indeterminacy.md) demands therefore exists, and the worry is met.

The claim assembles two prior commitments: that [implementation requires counterfactual structure](implementation-requires-counterfactual-structure.md) and that this
[counterfactual structure is objective](counterfactual-structure-objective.md). Put together,
the functionalist concludes that objective counterfactual-causal structure fixes a system's
privileged functional decomposition.

### Scope

This is the strong claim the causal-structure reply needs, and it goes beyond what the
anti-triviality results establish. Chalmers' "Does a Rock Implement Every Finite-State Automaton?"
([chalmers-1996b](../sources/chalmers-1996b.md)) secures non-*triviality* — not every system implements every
computation — whereas this claim asserts *uniqueness*, that the structure picks out a single
decomposition. That extra step is contested.

### Who would deny it

Anyone running the two standing attacks:
[counterfactual constraints leave a
whole family of carvings](../arguments/counterfactuals-yield-non-triviality-not-uniqueness.md), and [causal structure alone does not
select the mind-relevant relations](../arguments/relevance-filter-regress.md). More broadly, a residual-indeterminacy theorist denies it.

It is distinct from [the claim that the correct grain is
organisational](role-grain-is-organisational.md): that names *which* grain is correct, whereas this asserts that causal structure
fixes *a* decomposition at all.

### In plain terms

You might worry there's no single "right" way to carve a system into parts and processes — that it's
all in the eye of the beholder. This claim answers: there is a right way, because real cause-and-
effect (what genuinely would have happened under other conditions) is a fact about the world, not a
matter of how an observer chooses to look — and it picks out one true organization. (It's a contested
step: critics reply that cause-and-effect narrows the options without nailing down exactly one.)
