That something is coherently conceivable — even rigorously, mathematically coherent — does not
entail that it can be physically built or realized. The inference from conceivable to
*physically* possible fails.

The claim concerns the conceivability → **physical** possibility bridge specifically. It is
deliberately silent on the conceivability → **metaphysical** possibility bridge
([conceivability-entails-possibility](conceivability-entails-possibility.md)): the witnessing case, the halting oracle, is
*metaphysically* possible (a consistent structure) and merely *physically* unrealizable, so it is
no counterexample to the metaphysical bridge. To threaten *that* bridge one would need a
*Turing-machine* halting-decider, which is metaphysically impossible — but on ideal reflection
that is not even *ideally* conceivable, since Turing's contradiction is a priori. So the
metaphysical bridge keeps its *prima facie / ideal* refinement, parallel to the primary/secondary
move on the water case.

The cleanest witnessing instance is the halting oracle
([halting-oracle-coherent-but-unrealizable](halting-oracle-coherent-but-unrealizable.md)) — coherent, theorized, provably not
Turing-computable, and on standard physics unbuildable, with *no* "you mislabelled it" escape.
The behaviour-matching lookup table ([lookup-table-physically-unrealisable](lookup-table-physically-unrealisable.md)) is a second
instance, failing instead on resource bounds ([observable-universe-finite-storage](observable-universe-finite-storage.md)).

Who would deny it: someone who collapses physical possibility into logical or mathematical
possibility (a strong digital-physics or modal-collapse view); or a hypercomputation theorist who
holds that the witnessing cases are realizable after all.

It is distinct from the metaphysical bridge ([conceivability-entails-possibility](conceivability-entails-possibility.md)): same
antecedent, different consequent — *physical* realizability, not *metaphysical* possibility.

### In plain terms

Being able to coherently imagine — or even rigorously define and theorize — something doesn't mean
nature can build it. The halting oracle is the showcase: airtight as an idea, impossible as a machine.
This is a *better* version of the old "water that isn't H₂O" point, because there's no wriggle room
about whether you've really conceived it — there's a whole branch of mathematics about it. One careful
boundary: this is about what can be *built*, not about what's *possible in the broadest sense* — the
oracle is still a consistent abstract object; it just can't be made real.
