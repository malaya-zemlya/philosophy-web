The everyday categories of mind — beliefs, desires, and qualia — are the theoretical posits of *folk
psychology*, the tacit theory ordinary people use to explain and predict behaviour. On this claim
folk psychology is an empirical theory that a mature neuroscience will show to be *false* and will
*replace* rather than vindicate. So strictly there are no beliefs, desires, or qualia — only neural
processes the folk framework has been mislabelling (Paul M. Churchland, [churchland-1981](../sources/churchland-1981.md)).
In the A/B/C taxonomy of physicalisms ([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md)) it is a *type-A* materialism:
it denies there is any phenomenal explanandum to begin with.

This is an *eliminativist* claim about the **non-existence** of the folk posits, not merely a claim
that they reduce to neural states. It is broader than illusionism: it eliminates the *propositional
attitudes* (belief, desire) as well, not only phenomenal feel. It is denied by realists about folk
psychology — most functionalists and intentional-stance theorists, who hold the folk categories earn
their keep — and by phenomenal realists asserting [a phenomenal residue](phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md).
Distinct from [the illusionist claim](phenomenality-is-illusion.md): that thesis targets
*phenomenal* properties specifically and grants the introspective appearance (the states seem to feel
some way), whereas this eliminativist claim is wider and more radical, discarding the propositional
attitudes too.

### In plain terms

The idea that our familiar way of talking about minds — "she believes it's raining," "he wants
coffee," "this hurts" — is a folk *theory* that could turn out to be wrong, the way "phlogiston" or
"the four humours" turned out to be wrong. If brain science eventually explains us without ever
needing beliefs, desires, or feelings, then, on this claim, those things simply don't exist; we were
mislabelling brain processes all along. It goes further than merely saying "there's no special inner
feel": it also throws out everyday beliefs and desires.
