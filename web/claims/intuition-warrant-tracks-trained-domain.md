Thought-experiment intuitions are calibrated on actual, encountered cases. A verdict about a
hypothetical therefore carries evidential weight only in proportion to how closely that hypothetical
resembles the exemplars the intuition was formed on — in scale, in structure, in kind. Cases far
outside the trained range yield verdicts of degraded warrant: the faculty is being run on inputs it was
never tuned for. The point is a familiar theme in the epistemology of thought experiments
([williamson-2007](../sources/williamson-2007.md)), in the critique of "intuition pumps" that smuggle a verdict by tweaking
irrelevant details ([dennett-2013](../sources/dennett-2013.md)), and in experimental-philosophy findings that intuitions
grow unstable for exotic cases.

This is a methodological and epistemic principle about the *warrant* of intuitive verdicts, neutral on
any first-order theory of mind. It does not say far-fetched cases are impossible, nor that distant
intuitions are always false — only that their evidential weight decays with dissimilarity to paradigm
cases, so a distant verdict cannot bear heavy argumentative load unsupported. It would be denied by a
rationalist about modal or conceptual intuition, who holds that clear conceivability delivers reliable
verdicts regardless of a case's distance from experience.

It is distinct from [the blockhead claim](imaginary-blockhead-no-secure-verdict.md), which
*applies* this principle to a particular case (the giant look-up-table mind); this is the general
principle that case instances.

### In plain terms

Your gut feelings about made-up cases are trained on the real cases you've actually met. So a hunch is
trustworthy roughly to the degree the imagined case resembles those familiar ones — in size, in
structure, in kind. Run your intuition on something wildly outside anything you've ever encountered and
its verdicts get shaky; you're using a tool far outside the range it was built for. (It doesn't say
far-fetched cases are impossible, or that distant hunches are always wrong — only that they can't carry
heavy argumentative weight on their own.)
