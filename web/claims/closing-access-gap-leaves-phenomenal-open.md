A system could be handed complete, transparent access to its own inner workings — its calibrated
uncertainty, its raw activations, every counterfactual it might run on itself — and still face the
question, *why is any of this accompanied by experience at all?* Supplying that self-knowledge
closes an *access* deficit (a shortfall in what the system can get at, use, and report about its own
states); it does not thereby close the *phenomenal* deficit (the puzzle of why those states are felt
at all). The two are different gaps, and a fix for one need not be a fix for the other.

The distinction is Block's: [access consciousness](../concepts/access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md) is
information poised for use in reasoning, speech, and control, whereas phenomenal consciousness is
what a state is like to undergo. The [hard problem](../concepts/hard-problem.md) is posed about the
phenomenal side, so an account that improves a system's access to itself — however complete —
under-reaches the phenomenal question rather than answering it.

The claim is deliberately narrow. It asserts only the *one-directional independence* of the two
gaps: that an access remedy need not be a phenomenal remedy. It does not assert that there is a
phenomenal residue left over, nor that the gap is ontological rather than merely epistemic; it says
only that an architectural story stops short of the phenomenal question and so cannot, by itself,
settle it.

It would be denied by an illusionist or strong functionalist who holds there is no phenomenal
explanandum over and above the access facts — on that view, once the access gap is closed there is
nothing further left to explain.

Distinct from [phenomenal-residue](phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md), which makes the positive claim that role under-determines
the phenomenal (a residue thesis); this makes the weaker, dialectical point that an access-level
explanation does not reach the phenomenal question either way.

### In plain terms

Suppose you build a machine that can inspect and honestly report everything going on inside it — how
confident it is, what each part is doing, what it would do in other circumstances. You would have
solved the problem of *access*: nothing inside is hidden from it. But you would not, just by doing
that, have explained why any of it should *feel* like anything from the inside. Those are two
separate problems, and solving the first leaves the second wide open.
