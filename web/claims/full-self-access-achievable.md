A sufficiently reflective finite system can, in principle, be engineered to have complete, transparent
access to *all* of its own current state from within. On this optimistic view the familiar limits on
introspection are contingent and removable, not structural — the position denied by
[the limited-inside-vantage claim](inside-vantage-access-limited.md).

### Scope

The substantive reading is *internal* and *complete*. It is trivially true if "access" is allowed to
be *external* instrumentation — but an external instrument is not part of the system whose state is
represented, so that reading abandons the self-access claim.

### Why contested

The claim is attacked on capacity grounds by [finite-self-model-incomplete](../arguments/finite-self-model-incomplete.md): a proper part
of a finite state cannot losslessly encode the whole. Anyone holding that bound denies it.

### Distinct from neighbours

Distinct from [llm-functional-introspection](llm-functional-introspection.md): that asserts *limited, unreliable* self-access
exists; this asserts that *complete* self-access is achievable — the strong thesis the impossibility
argument targets.

### In plain terms

The optimistic bet: with clever enough engineering, a system could be built to see everything about
its own current inner state, perfectly and all at once — so the reason we (and today's AIs) can't
fully introspect would be just a fixable shortcoming, not a hard wall. The opposing camp says there is
a hard wall, provable by a counting argument ([finite-self-model-incomplete](../arguments/finite-self-model-incomplete.md)); that dispute
is why this claim is marked contested.
