At least some mental events make a difference to the physical world: decisions issue in bodily
movements, pains prompt winces, perceptions guide reaching hands. The mind is not a mere spectator;
it is among the causes of what bodies do.

**Scope.** The claim asserts only that mental events have *physical* effects — that mental causation
reaches the physical world. It does not say *how* (whether by identity, realisation, or some other
relation), nor how many mental events are efficacious. It is the premise that the most thoroughgoing
[epiphenomenalism](../positions/epiphenomenalism.md) denies, holding the mental to be a causally inert by-product
([mental-states-causally-inert](mental-states-causally-inert.md)). Most parties — physicalist and dualist alike — accept it as a
near-datum of common sense, which is what gives the [causal-argument-for-physicalism](../arguments/causal-argument-for-physicalism.md) its grip:
combined with the [completeness-of-physics](../concepts/completeness-of-physics.md), a mind that genuinely moves the body is pressed
toward being physical.
