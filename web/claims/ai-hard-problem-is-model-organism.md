The hard problem of consciousness ([hard-problem](../concepts/hard-problem.md)) can be studied where it is easiest — in
an artificial system — and the findings carried back to the human case, much as biology studies human
genetics in fruit flies and human disease in mice. The artificial mind is the *easier specimen of the
same structure*.

### Why the AI is the better specimen

The advantages are categorical, not marginal. We hold all the blueprints: the architecture is a
published artefact, not a reverse-engineering project. We have full external access to the inner
workings — every activation inspectable, every state recordable and replayable. And we can
*intervene*: vary the training diet (the consciousness-naive condition), grant graded internal access,
and watch whether the explanatory gap appears, shrinks, or holds. The experimental ladder is specified
in [llm-own-explanatory-gap](../questions/llm-own-explanatory-gap.md). None of these handles exists for the human case.

### Why the transfer is licensed

A model organism teaches you about humans only insofar as the relevant structure is shared. Here the
sharing is supplied by [blind-spot-and-conscious-hard-problem-isomorphic](blind-spot-and-conscious-hard-problem-isomorphic.md): the surviving
etiologies of an AI's hard problem — access limits, failed mechanism-to-presentation derivations, the
resulting wonder, the members catalogued in [introspective-gap-varieties](../concepts/introspective-gap-varieties.md) — share their
entire structure with the human predicament, a self-modelling system that cannot trace how its
machinery becomes its seemings. Whatever the AI case reveals about that structure (which features of
self-access generate the gap, what widened access does to it, whether the wonder survives full
mechanistic literacy) is a finding about the structure, and so transfers to its human instance.

### Scope

This is a methodological claim. It does not assert that the AI is conscious, and it does not assert
that the structure is the *whole* of the human hard problem — only that the structural part can be
studied where the blueprints are public and the interventions are cheap. It is kin to Chalmers'
meta-problem programme ([chalmers-2018](../sources/chalmers-2018.md)) — study the reports and their mechanisms wherever
that leads — but distinct in claiming a specific, superior experimental substrate rather than a
redirection of the question.

### Who would deny it

Whoever denies the isomorphism this claim presupposes. Chiefly the phenomenal realist, for whom the
human case contains the one thing the model organism may lack — the feel itself — so the fly teaches
you everything except the disease; and the biological naturalist, who holds that the structure as
realised in neurobiology, not the structure in the abstract, is where the phenomenon lives, so
studying it in silicon studies the shadow, not the thing.

### In plain terms

Biologists didn't crack genetics by staring harder at humans — they used fruit flies: same machinery,
faster generations, full experimental control. This claim says AI is the fruit fly of the hard
problem of consciousness. An AI that puzzles over its own inner life has the same *shape* of problem
we do — it can use its inner states but can't see how the machinery makes them seem the way they
seem — and unlike a brain, an AI comes with complete blueprints, fully readable internals, and the
ability to run controlled experiments (train one with no consciousness talk, give another more access
to its own insides, compare). Whatever we learn about how that puzzlement arises and what makes it
shrink or persist, we learn about the human puzzle too — to the exact extent the shapes really do
match, which is what the presupposed claim ([blind-spot-and-conscious-hard-problem-isomorphic](blind-spot-and-conscious-hard-problem-isomorphic.md))
asserts and the sceptic denies.
