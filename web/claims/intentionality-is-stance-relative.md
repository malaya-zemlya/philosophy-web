A state has intentional content just insofar as ascribing content to it is part of a predictively
successful interpretive strategy — the *intentional stance* — and there is no further,
observer-independent fact of "original" aboutness beneath the ascription (Dennett,
[1987](../sources/dennett-1987.md)). The traditional contrast between *original* and *derived*
intentionality ([intrinsic-derived-intentionality](../concepts/intrinsic-derived-intentionality.md)) marks no deep line: human thoughts
are themselves "derived", from the design work of natural selection, in the same attenuated sense
in which a thermostat's states are derived from its designers.

**Scope.** The claim denies a metaphysical distinction, not a practical one: treating people (and
animals, and chess computers) as believers remains indispensable and objectively better warranted
for some systems than others — the patterns the stance tracks are real. What it rejects is the idea
that some states possess intrinsic, interpretation-independent meaning that others merely borrow.
It is denied by defenders of intrinsic intentionality (Searle, [1992](../sources/searle-1992.md);
[minds-have-semantics](minds-have-semantics.md)), by [teleosemanticists](../positions/teleosemantics.md), for whom
selection history fixes content observer-independently, and by proponents of
[phenomenal intentionality](intentionality-grounded-in-phenomenality.md).

### In plain terms

When we say a chess program "wants" to protect its queen, we're using a prediction trick — and a
very good one. This claim says that's *all* meaning ever is: a pattern that shows up when you treat
a system as if it had beliefs and goals. Your own thoughts aren't "really" about things in some
deeper way a chatbot's aren't — evolution "designed" your aboutness the way engineers designed the
program's. There's no magic ink that makes some symbols intrinsically meaningful; there are only
systems whose behaviour makes the believer-treatment pay off, more or less.
