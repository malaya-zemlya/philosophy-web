One is justified in holding that there is "someone there to be wronged" only where one has direct
first-person acquaintance with the relevant experience. Absent such acquaintance — the lived, inside
access a subject has to its own states ([first-person-access](../concepts/first-person-access.md)) — attributing moral status
is at best an undischarged inference, never a warranted judgment.

The claim concerns the *warrant* for moral-status attributions, not what moral status metaphysically
consists in. It is the load-bearing and weakest premise of [first-person-access](../arguments/first-person-access.md): it is
what converts an asymmetry of *access* into an asymmetry of *justified moral regard*. Without it, the
fact that I cannot get inside a machine would carry no weight on whether I may justifiably count it as
a being that matters.

It is contested from two directions. Some hold that behavioural, functional, or theoretical evidence
can justify moral-status attributions with no first-person acquaintance at all — for instance the
[behavioural-parity argument](../arguments/behavioral-parity.md). Others press the problem of other minds:
taken strictly, the principle seems to leave me equally unwarranted about other human beings, since I
have no inside line to their experience either, and that is the lever of the
[access-symmetry rebuttal](../arguments/access-symmetry-rebuttal.md).

This is distinct from [privileged-first-person-access](privileged-first-person-access.md), which merely asserts that
self-acquaintance exists; the present claim asserts the far stronger, contestable thesis that such
acquaintance is *necessary* for warranted moral-status attribution.

### In plain terms

This claim says you're only really entitled to believe "there's someone in there who can be wronged"
when you have direct, first-hand acquaintance with their experience. Short of that inside contact,
calling something a being-that-matters is just an unproven guess. It's a strong and much-disputed
claim — taken strictly, it seems to leave you unable to vouch even for *other people*, since you have
no inside line to their feelings either.
