What we have reason to care about, when we care about "surviving," is not numerical identity but
*Relation R*: psychological connectedness and continuity, with any reliable cause. Where identity
and Relation R come apart — as in fission, where one person's psychology continues in two
successors, neither of whom can be identical to the original — the rational concern goes with R:
dividing is not death, though it fails identity's one-one logic (Parfit,
[1984](../sources/parfit-1984.md); [fission](../arguments/fission.md)).

**Scope.** This is the *normative* thesis in Parfit's package, distinct from the metaphysical
degree thesis [personal-identity-admits-of-degree](personal-identity-admits-of-degree.md) — one could accept that identity admits
of degree while still holding identity to be what matters, and vice versa. As an answer to the
[personal-identity question](../questions/personal-identity.md) it is deflationary: the question is
real but its practical importance is inherited entirely from R, so once R is tracked, nothing of
prudential significance turns on identity itself. It is denied by anyone for whom strict identity
carries independent weight — notably
[further-fact theorists](identity-is-further-fact.md), for whom anticipating *my* future is
rationally unlike anticipating a duplicate's, and by
[animalists](../positions/animalism.md) insofar as they keep identity prudentially central (though some
borrow this claim to defuse the transplant intuition).

### In plain terms

Suppose a machine could copy your mind into two new bodies as your old one dies. Neither copy can
be *you* in the strict one-and-only sense — there are two of them and one of you. Disaster? Parfit
says no: everything you actually care about in surviving — someone waking up with your memories,
loving the people you love, finishing your projects — is there twice over. What we call "survival"
was never really about the strict logical relation of being one-and-the-same thing; it was about
the psychological thread continuing. So the right response to the puzzle cases isn't to find the
hidden answer to "which one is me?" — it's to see that the question matters less than we thought.
