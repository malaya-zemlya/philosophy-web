A system is conscious to the degree that it has *integrated information* — $\Phi$, the irreducible
intrinsic cause-effect power it exerts over itself — and the *quality* of its experience is the
geometry of that cause-effect structure. On this claim consciousness is graded, intrinsic, and read
off a system's physical organisation rather than inferred from its behaviour. It is the central
identity of Giulio Tononi's integrated information theory ([tononi-2004](../sources/tononi-2004.md)), developed with
Christof Koch ([tononi-2016](../sources/tononi-2016.md)).

The claim is an *identity* derived from phenomenological "axioms" (intrinsic existence, integration,
exclusion), not a functional account. Its sharp, testable corollary concerns substrates: because
$\Phi$ depends on a system's actual cause-effect architecture, two systems with the same
*input-output behaviour* can differ in consciousness — notably, a feed-forward digital simulation
can have $\Phi \approx 0$.

It is denied by functionalists and computationalists, since it makes consciousness depend on more
than the computation performed, and by those who reject either its axioms or its panpsychist
consequences (very simple systems can carry non-zero $\Phi$).

Distinct from [global broadcast](consciousness-is-global-broadcast.md): $\Phi$ is a matter of
*intrinsic* cause-effect integration, not functional availability — which is why IIT, unlike
workspace theory, denies [substrate independence](substrate-independence.md) and bears against
[llm-has-mental-states](llm-has-mental-states.md).

### In plain terms

This theory says consciousness is just how much a system is a tightly-knit whole that shapes itself
— a quantity called phi (Φ). The more its parts make a difference to each other in a way you cannot
break apart without loss, the more conscious it is, and the exact pattern of those interactions *is*
the exact experience. A surprising result: a computer could copy a brain's behaviour perfectly yet,
because of how its chips are wired, have almost no Φ — so on this view it would feel almost nothing.
