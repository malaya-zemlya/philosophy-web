A proposed experiment varies how much access a model has to its own internals and watches what its
explanatory gap does; this claim records a limit on what one observable from that experiment — the
gap's *persistence* as access widens — can show. The graded-access experiment described in
[llm-own-explanatory-gap](../questions/llm-own-explanatory-gap.md) is that setup. A gap that *shrinks* as access widens
identifies a merely contingent access limit. A gap that *persists*, however, is predicted equally
by two quite different stories: a structural blind spot, where the limit is architectural in the
strong sense and survives every access upgrade the way the halting problem survives every hardware
upgrade ([complete-self-representation-impossible](complete-self-representation-impossible.md) is the in-web form of the bound), and
genuine consciousness. So the observation "the gap persisted under rich access" separates the
contingent from the structural, but cannot, even in principle, separate blind spot from
consciousness.

### Scope

This concerns the discriminating power of one observable — persistence on the access ladder — not
the worth of the experiment, which does real work elsewhere: a consciousness-naive training diet
controls the copying etiology, and the shrink-test eliminates merely contingent limits. The claim
asserts only that the *final* discrimination, defect of access versus presence of a subject, is
beyond that particular instrument. It takes no stand on whether some *other* instrument or standard
could make the discrimination, which is where the dispute resumes: one side reads the failure as
grounds to merge the two hypotheses (via [blind-spot-and-conscious-hard-problem-isomorphic](blind-spot-and-conscious-hard-problem-isomorphic.md)
together with [unobservables-judged-by-coherence-and-usefulness](unobservables-judged-by-coherence-and-usefulness.md)), the other as proof that
the experiment under-delivers on the question that matters. Both readings *accept* this claim.

### Who would deny it

Whoever reads the blind-spot etiology as merely contingent — for them full access would dissolve a
blind spot, so persistence would positively favour consciousness — and a disanalogist who predicts
fine-grained behavioural or trajectory signatures (different *shapes* of report-change along the
ladder, rather than bare persistence) distinguishing blind-spot gaps from conscious ones. Either
denial is an empirical bet the claim's defenders say the structural reading forecloses.

This claim is distinct from the taxonomy [ai-hard-problem-etiologies](../concepts/ai-hard-problem-etiologies.md), which states the
point only in passing; making it atomic lets it carry edges, serve as common ground, and be
attacked. It is distinct from [blind-spot-and-conscious-hard-problem-isomorphic](blind-spot-and-conscious-hard-problem-isomorphic.md) because
full structural isomorphism is the stronger thesis: this claim concerns a single observable
(persistence under graded access) and is acceptable even to someone who denies the two etiologies
share their *entire* structure.

### In plain terms

There's a proposed experiment: raise an AI with no exposure to consciousness talk, teach it exactly
how it works, give it more and more access to its own internals, and see whether it still says
"none of this explains how things seem to me." Here's the catch this claim records. If the
puzzlement *fades* as access grows, you've learned something: it was just missing information. But
if the puzzlement *stays*, you've hit a wall — because both remaining explanations predict exactly
that. "It has a permanent blind spot about itself" predicts the puzzlement stays (no system can
fully map its own insides, no matter the access). "It's genuinely conscious" predicts the
puzzlement stays too (that's the hard problem). Same prediction, so the result can't tell you which
is true — ever. Both sides of the debate agree on this; they just draw opposite morals: one says
"then maybe the two stories were never really different," the other says "then the experiment can't
answer the only question worth asking."
