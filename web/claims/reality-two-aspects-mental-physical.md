There is a single underlying reality that presents two irreducible *aspects* — an inner, mental
aspect and an outer, physical aspect — neither reducible to the other, both aspects of one thing.
The locus classicus is Spinoza's one substance, grasped under the attributes of thought as well
as extension ([spinoza-ethics](../sources/spinoza-ethics.md)); a twentieth-century version is the Pauli–Jung dual-aspect
view ([pauli-jung-1955](../sources/pauli-jung-1955.md)). Mind and matter are one reality seen two ways.

The claim is a monism about substance carrying an irreducible duality of aspects. It differs from
*property* dualism — which posits two kinds of property in or of physical things — by making both
the mental and the physical *aspects of a prior unity*. It is denied by substance dualists (two
things, not two aspects), reductive physicalists (the mental aspect reduces), and by idealists or
physicalists who privilege one side.

Distinct from [the neutral-base claim](reality-fundamental-stuff-neutral.md) (neutral monism
makes the base *neutral* and mind/matter arrangements of it; here the base intrinsically bears
*both* aspects) and from [the Russellian
categorical-base claim](consciousness-categorical-not-dispositional.md) (which grounds the physical in the phenomenal rather than holding the
two on a par).

### In plain terms

There's just one underlying reality, but it always shows up with two sides — an inner, felt side
and an outer, physical side — the way one coin always has heads and tails. Because they are two
faces of a single thing, the felt and the physical are bound to line up.
