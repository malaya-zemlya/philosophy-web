The natural property that grounds consciousness — the brain property in virtue of which experience
arises ([consciousness has a natural explanation](consciousness-has-natural-explanation.md)) —
lies outside the range of concepts the human mind can form. The mind–body problem is, on this
thesis, *soluble in itself but insoluble by us*: there is an answer, but we are constitutively
unable to grasp it. This is the central mysterian claim, due to Colin McGinn (1989;
[mcginn-1989](../sources/mcginn-1989.md)).

The claim is about *our* cognitive limits, not about metaphysics. It does not say consciousness is
non-physical, or that the explanatory gap reflects a real ontological divide; on the contrary, it
presupposes that the link is a perfectly natural fact. It is the conclusion of the
[cognitive-closure argument](../arguments/cognitive-closure.md).

It would be denied from three directions: optimistic naturalists (the problem is hard but
tractable); reductive functionalists and illusionists (there is no residual hard problem for us to
be closed to); and dualists, who read the very same gap as ontological rather than cognitive.

Distinct from [the claim that felt character outruns functional role](phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md):
that locates the shortfall in the *world* — role does not exhaust the phenomenal; this locates it in
*us* — we cannot grasp the natural property that does the work.

### In plain terms

This is the pessimistic core of mysterianism: the brain really does produce consciousness by some
ordinary physical means, but our minds may simply lack the concepts to ever understand how — the way
a dog can't grasp arithmetic. The point isn't that consciousness is spooky or non-physical; it's
that the explanation, though real, sits permanently out of our reach.
