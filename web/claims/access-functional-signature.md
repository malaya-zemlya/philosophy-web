Five functional properties are commonly held to distinguish consciously accessible information from
unconscious processing: it is *reportable* (it can be put into words on request — the classic
empirical signature of conscious access); it is subject to *top-down modulation* (a content can be
deliberately summoned, held in mind, and dismissed); it is the *medium of deliberate reasoning*
(the effortful chaining of one thought to the next); it permits *flexible generalization* (the same
content can be routed to whatever operation the task demands and recombined in novel
configurations); and it is *selective* (only a small fraction of ongoing processing is accessible
at any moment, the rest proceeding automatically). Taken together, these constitute the functional
signature by which [access consciousness](../concepts/access-consciousness.md) is identified in the
cognitive-science literature descending from global workspace theory ([baars-1988](../sources/baars-1988.md),
[dehaene-naccache-2001](../sources/dehaene-naccache-2001.md)), and they supply indicator properties by which artificial systems
have been proposed to be assessed for consciousness-related processing ([butlin-2023](../sources/butlin-2023.md)).

The claim concerns the *access* side of the
[access/phenomenal distinction](../concepts/access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md) only: it says what
functional profile accessible information exhibits, and is silent on whether that profile has
anything to do with felt experience. It would be denied from two directions: by those who hold that
the signature is not *sufficient* to identify a genuine workspace — a recurrent-processing theorist
requires a specific architecture, not just the functional profile — and by those who deny it
captures consciousness proper, as phenomenal-overflow theorists do when they argue that experience
outruns anything reportable ([block-2007](../sources/block-2007.md)).
