Freedom, on this *leeway* conception, requires real alternatives: when an agent chooses freely, the
world up to that moment leaves more than one continuation available, and which one occurs is up to
the agent. The openness asserted is metaphysical, not merely epistemic — it is not just that the
agent doesn't *know* what she will do, but that the past and the laws do not fix it.

The claim is the defining commitment of leeway forms of [libertarianism](../positions/libertarianism.md),
and the Consequence Argument (van Inwagen, [1983](../sources/van-inwagen-1983.md)) defends its
incompatibility with determinism: if determinism is true, our acts are consequences of the laws and
the remote past, neither of which is up to us. It is denied outright by
[hard determinists](../positions/hard-determinism.md), for whom the appearance of open alternatives is a
fact about deliberation rather than about the world, and it is denied *as a condition on freedom*
by actual-sequence compatibilists, for whom [Frankfurt cases](../concepts/frankfurt-cases.md) show that
responsibility survives the deletion of alternatives. In the
[consciousness–free-will duality](../concepts/consciousness-freewill-duality.md) this claim is the thesis
that the action space is a genuine coproduct of really-available options, not an epistemic one with
a single inhabited summand.
