Whether some later person is "the same person" as an earlier one can hold to a greater or lesser
degree; in the middle of a gradual psychological transformation there need be no determinate
yes-or-no answer ([Parfit](../sources/parfit-1984.md)).

This is a metaphysical thesis about the identity relation itself, not yet the normative corollary
that "identity is not what matters." It selects the *non-discrete-$Y$* (degree) horn of the
[continuity-argument-schema](../concepts/continuity-argument-schema.md) for the personal-identity case.

It is denied by defenders of a determinate, all-or-nothing identity criterion, who must then take
either the discontinuity horn (a sharp boundary) or the indeterminacy horn (gaps without degrees).

Distinct from a claim about *what matters in survival*: this is the degree thesis about identity
itself; the practical upshot is a separate proposition.

### In plain terms

Being "the same person" over time needn't be a flat yes or no — it can hold more or less. Across a
slow, sweeping change of mind and memory there may simply be no sharp answer to "is that still the
same person?"; they're partly continuous, a matter of degree. (This is the metaphysical claim; the
famous follow-up — that identity therefore "isn't what matters" — is a separate step.)
