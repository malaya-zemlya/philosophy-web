Each type of mental state is identical to a type of brain state: mental-state *types* just are
physical (neural) state *types*. Pain, for instance, is held to be identical to a specific neural
event such as C-fibre firing — an identity discovered *a posteriori*, the way "water is H₂O" was,
rather than fixed by the meanings of words. This is the classical mind-brain identity theory of
U. T. Place ([place-1956](../sources/place-1956.md)), J. J. C. Smart ([smart-1959](../sources/smart-1959.md)), and Herbert Feigl
([feigl-1958](../sources/feigl-1958.md)). In the A/B/C taxonomy of physicalisms ([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md)) it is
a *type-B* view: it grants the epistemic gap between physical and phenomenal descriptions but denies
that any ontological gap corresponds to it.

The identity claimed is *type-type*, stronger than mere token-identity (which says only that each
particular mental event is *some* physical event). Because the identity is with a specific physical
type, it implies that mental states are *not* multiply realisable in arbitrary substrates — the
contrary of [substrate independence](substrate-independence.md).

Who would deny it: functionalists, via the multiple-realizability argument — Putnam's point that the
same mental state can be realised in silicon, in other animals, and so on
([putnam-1967](../sources/putnam-1967.md)), which historically pushed the field from identity theory to
[functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md); and non-physicalists, who deny the identity is with
anything physical. Distinct from [substrate independence](substrate-independence.md), which it
directly contradicts, and from [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md), which individuates mental
states by realiser-neutral role rather than physical type.

### In plain terms

This is the original "the mind is the brain" idea, stated precisely: every kind of mental state is
one and the same thing as some kind of brain activity — pain, say, being a particular pattern of
nerve cells firing. It's meant as a scientific discovery (like finding out water is H₂O), not a
definition. The catch: if pain just *is* one specific brain pattern, then a creature built very
differently — a computer, an alien — couldn't be in pain in that sense, since it lacks that exact
brain state. That objection is what led many philosophers to [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md)
instead.
