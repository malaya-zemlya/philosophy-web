The *substrate-independence thesis* holds that what makes a mental state the state it is
depends on the functional role it plays — what causes it and what it in turn causes — not on
the physical stuff that carries out that role. If that is right, then any system implementing
the relevant role thereby has the state, whether it is built from carbon neurons or silicon
circuits.

The thesis follows from [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md), the view that mental states are
individuated by causal role rather than by physical constitution. It restates the point made
by the multiple-realizability argument that launched functionalism ([putnam-1967](../sources/putnam-1967.md)):
if pain is whatever occupies the pain-role, then a mollusc, a mammal, or a machine can each be
in pain, provided each realises that role.

### Scope

This is a claim about mental states *in general*, and a deliberately modest one. It does not
by itself assert that any actual machine realises the relevant roles; whether some real system
does is the separate work of the [behavioral-parity](../arguments/behavioral-parity.md). Nor is it the stronger claim
that silicon systems are conscious. Substrate-independence is the weaker enabling premise, not
that conclusion: it unlocks the door without claiming anyone has walked through.

Who would deny it: *substrate chauvinists*, who hold that the physical material itself
constrains which minds are possible — see [proteocentrism](../positions/proteocentrism.md), on which only
protein-based biology can support a mind.

### In plain terms

Could a mind run on something other than a brain? This claim says: yes, at least in principle. The
reasoning — if what makes a mental state the state it is, is the *role* it plays (what causes it,
what it goes on to cause), then anything that plays that role has that state, no matter what it's
built from: carbon, silicon, whatever. Notice how modest this is. It does *not* say today's
computers or chatbots actually have minds. It only says that being made of brain-stuff isn't, all by
itself, a requirement. It unlocks the door; it doesn't claim anyone has walked through.
