When we hold someone responsible — feel the pull of resentment, judge that she is to blame, want her
punished — much of that response is not the conclusion of an argument but the firing of an evolved
social instinct. On this view the human mind carries an innate *moral faculty*, a "universal moral
grammar" that delivers rapid verdicts of right and wrong before any reasoning takes place (Hauser,
[2006](../sources/hauser-2006.md)); the reasons we later give are, more often than not, *post-hoc*
rationalisations of a judgment already made — the emotional dog wagging its rational tail (Haidt,
[2001](../sources/haidt-2001.md)). Because the source of these judgments is an instinct shaped by evolution
for the coordination of social life, and not a faculty of consistent reason, their outputs carry no
guarantee of coherence or rational defensibility.

The evidence is that the judgments misbehave in exactly the ways a reasoned faculty should not.
*Moral dumbfounding*: people cling to a condemnation while admitting they can supply no reason for it.
*Framing and order effects*: structurally identical dilemmas draw opposite verdicts depending on
wording or on which case is considered first — most famously the trolley pair, where diverting a
runaway trolley to kill one instead of five is widely approved, yet pushing one person to the same
arithmetic end is widely condemned. And the instinct is *parochial*: it weights kin, in-group, and
the vivid and near far above the statistical and distant. None of this looks like the profile of a
consistent rational principle; it looks like the profile of a heuristic that was selected because it
worked, not because it was valid.

The claim reframes the surrounding debate rather than settling it. It gives an evolutionary,
empirical underpinning to the observation that the reactive attitudes are natural facts of human life
rather than conclusions drawn from a theory ([strawson-1962](../sources/strawson-1962.md)) — but presses further than that
tradition usually does: being *natural* is not being *authoritative*, and a natural response can be
parochial and incoherent. It complicates the rationalist reconstructions on the other side too:
[consequentialist-responsibility](../positions/consequentialist-responsibility.md) treats holding-responsible as a rational instrument for
shaping conduct, yet if the practice is largely pre-rational instinct, it will persist and misfire in
ways no purely instrumental design would predict. And it bears on the [free-will](../questions/free-will.md): if the
felt conviction that an agent "could have done otherwise" and "deserves" blame is itself partly an
evolved intuition, that is a reason to hold our desert-intuitions at arm's length — though, by
itself, no proof that they track nothing real.

### Scope

The claim asserts a *lack of guarantee*, not falsity. That a judgment springs from instinct does not
by itself show it is mistaken — to infer "instinctive, therefore wrong" would be the genetic fallacy.
What the instinctual origin removes is any *automatic entitlement* to treat the judgments as
consistent or rationally authoritative. **Moral rationalists** deny even this much: they hold that
genuine moral judgment is, or ought to be, the work of reason, and that intuitive reactions are
merely raw material to be disciplined by principle — so the reactions' evolutionary pedigree impugns
neither the principles nor the judgments reason ultimately endorses. The claim is also *partial* by
design ("in part"): it does not say responsibility judgments are *nothing but* instinct, only that
instinct is a large and distorting component reasoning does not fully govern.

### In plain terms

When you feel that someone *deserves* to be blamed or punished, that feeling mostly arrives before
any reasoning — it's a gut reaction built into us by evolution because reacting that way helped our
ancestors live together. We then invent reasons for it afterwards. The catch is that a gut reaction
isn't built to be logical: people will insist something is wrong while admitting they can't say why,
and two situations that are really the same can trigger opposite verdicts just because of how they're
described. So our sense of who's to blame is real and powerful, but it comes with no promise of being
consistent or fair — which is worth remembering when a whole theory of blame and praise
([consequentialist-responsibility](../positions/consequentialist-responsibility.md)) treats it as if it were a cool, rational tool. (Saying
the reaction is an instinct doesn't automatically make it *wrong* — just not automatically to be
trusted.)
