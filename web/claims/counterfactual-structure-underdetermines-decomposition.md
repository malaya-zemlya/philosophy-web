Counterfactual-causal constraints exclude gerrymandered carvings, and so secure *non-triviality* —
but they still leave a whole *family* of admissible decompositions. The survivors differ in where
the system's boundary is drawn, in the grain or level of the state variables, and in the choice of
variable set on which the (interventionist) causal facts are defined. Insisting on real cause and
effect narrows the field; it does not crown a single winner. Chalmers' own result claims only
non-triviality, not uniqueness ([chalmers-1996b](../sources/chalmers-1996b.md)).

### Scope

This is an underdetermination claim. It grants that the causal facts are objective *given* a variable
set; it denies that the world fixes the variable set, boundary, or grain uniquely.

### Who would deny it

A functionalist who holds that one privileged level or boundary is marked out by nature's joints —
for instance, the [grain](../concepts/grain.md) at which the system forms a closed causal or informational
unit.

It is the attenuated survivor of [the radical, Putnam-style
relativity](functional-decomposition-relative.md): constrained multiplicity, not "anything goes."

### In plain terms

Insisting on real cause-and-effect throws out the silly, cooked-up ways of slicing a system into
parts — but it doesn't leave just one. Several perfectly sensible carvings survive, differing in where
you draw the system's edges and how coarse or fine you make the pieces. So objective cause-and-effect
narrows the field without crowning a single winner. It's the milder cousin of "anything goes":
constrained, yet still more than one option.
