A first-order mental state is *conscious* in virtue of being represented by a suitable
*higher-order* state, and not otherwise. What it is for a state to be conscious is thus a
relational, representational fact about it, not an intrinsic glow it carries on its own. (The
first-/higher-order contrast is set out in [order-of-representation](../concepts/order-of-representation.md).) The higher-order
state may be an occurrent *higher-order thought* that one is in the lower state
([rosenthal-2005](../sources/rosenthal-2005.md)) or a quasi-perceptual *higher-order perception* of it
([lycan-1996](../sources/lycan-1996.md)); on a dispositional variant the higher-order content need only be poised to
occur, not actually entertained ([carruthers-2000](../sources/carruthers-2000.md)).

The claim is a reductive account of *state* consciousness — what makes a state conscious — most
naturally read as a theory of the access side of [the
access/phenomenal distinction](../concepts/access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md). It does not by itself settle the hard problem: critics say it
explains a state's availability, not its felt character.

It is denied by first-order representationalists, who hold that consciousness needs no higher-order
state, and by phenomenal realists, who hold that felt character is intrinsic rather than
constituted by being represented.

Distinct from [the global-broadcast claim](consciousness-is-global-broadcast.md), on which
consciousness is global availability rather than a dedicated higher-order representation, and from
[the first-order representational claim](phenomenal-character-is-representational.md), which
locates phenomenal character in first-order, world-directed content.

### In plain terms

A mental state — a pain, say — counts as *conscious* only when your mind also represents itself as
having it: a thought, or an inner perception, about the state. On this view there is no special
inner shine that makes a state conscious; it is conscious because it is being monitored from a level
above. The much-debated worry is that this explains your being *aware* of the pain without
explaining the *hurt* itself.
