What is fundamental is mind or experience: the physical world is constituted by, or derivative
of, consciousness rather than the reverse. The claim states an *idealist monism* — one
fundamental kind of thing, the mental — and is the inverse of physicalism on the direction of
grounding.

Historically it takes several forms: Berkeley's subjective idealism, on which to be is to be
perceived ([berkeley-1710](../sources/berkeley-1710.md)); Kastrup's *analytic idealism*, one universal mind whose
dissociations are individual subjects ([kastrup-2019](../sources/kastrup-2019.md)); and Hoffman's conscious realism,
on which spacetime is a perceptual interface rather than something fundamental
([hoffman-2008](../sources/hoffman-2008.md)). In each, the physical is an appearance *within* mind.

The claim dissolves the hard problem from the far side — there is no puzzle of getting mind from
matter, because matter is an appearance within mind — but it inherits the mirror-image burden, a
*decomposition* or *structure* problem of deriving the law-governed, shared physical world from
mind. It is denied by physicalists (matter is fundamental), dualists (two fundamental kinds),
and neutral monists (the base is neither mental nor physical); most contemporary philosophy
treats idealism as a minority view, though it is resurgent.

Distinct from [the claim that the base is neutral](reality-fundamental-stuff-neutral.md)
(which makes the fundamental stuff neither mental nor physical) and from
[panpsychism's claim](consciousness-fundamental-ubiquitous.md) (on which mind is fundamental
*in* matter, not the ground *of* it).

### In plain terms

The idea is that mind comes first and the physical world is something that appears *inside*
experience, not a mind-free stuff out of which minds are later made. If that is right, the puzzle
of how lifeless matter could ever produce feeling never gets started — but you then owe an
account of why this "appearance" is so orderly, lawful, and the same for everyone.
