The skeptical conclusion of Kripke's Wittgenstein ([kripke-1982](../sources/kripke-1982.md)): nothing about a person —
nothing in their past behaviour, their dispositions, their mental images, or any introspectible state —
*constitutes* the fact that they meant addition rather than some deviant function by "+". Generalised, no
fact settles what anyone means by any expression, so there is no fact of the matter, in the
truth-conditional sense, about meaning and content.

### Scope

The claim is about the *constitution* of meaning — what a meaning-fact could consist in — not about
whether people communicate or use words consistently (plainly they do). It does not say words are
meaningless; Kripke's Wittgenstein pairs it with a *skeptical solution* that relocates meaning from
truth-conditional facts to communal *assertibility conditions* (the practice, sustained by agreement, of
counting some uses as correct). It is a thesis about *semantic* content; it is not the same as any thesis
about the determinacy of *phenomenal* character.

### Who would deny it

Semantic realists who hold there is a genuine fact of meaning — whether grounded in suitably idealised
*dispositions* (what one would do under optimal conditions), in causal-informational or teleological
relations between states and the world, or in a primitive, irreducible *grasping* of a rule. Each is a
candidate "constituting fact" the skeptical argument ([kripke-rule-following](../arguments/kripke-rule-following.md)) tries to
eliminate; denying the claim means defending one of them against that elimination.

Distinct from [syntax-insufficient-for-semantics](syntax-insufficient-for-semantics.md) because that says formal symbol-manipulation does
not by itself *yield* meaning (a sufficiency claim about syntax); this says no fact of *any* kind — syntactic
or otherwise — constitutes determinate meaning in the first place. Distinct, too, from a claim about
phenomenal determinacy: the indeterminacy here is of *content*, not of *feel*.

### In plain terms

You've added numbers your whole life, but only finitely many times. Now someone asks: when you said you
meant *plus*, what made it *plus* rather than a sneaky rule ("plus, except give 5 for big numbers you never
actually tried") that fits everything you've ever done equally well? Look for the fact that pins it down:
your past sums are finite and fit both; your *habits* are finite too and you sometimes slip up; picturing
or feeling "I mean addition" doesn't spell out the answer for every new case. Kripke's unsettling claim is
that there's *no* such fact — nothing inside you nailed down which rule you meant. (His own way out isn't
"words are empty" but "meaning lives in the shared practice of a community," not in a private fact.)
