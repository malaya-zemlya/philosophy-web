The basic stuff of the world is *neutral* — in itself neither mental nor physical — and the
mental and the physical are two different *arrangements* or groupings of these same neutral
elements. Mind and matter are derivative patterns, not fundamental kinds. The idea traces to
Mach's neutral *elements* ([mach-1914](../sources/mach-1914.md)), James's *pure experience* ([james-1904](../sources/james-1904.md)),
and Russell's neutral monism ([russell-1921](../sources/russell-1921.md)).

The claim is a monism about the fundamental base together with a deflation of the mental/physical
divide to a difference in arrangement. In its classic form it does *not* say the neutral elements
are proto-experiential; that extra step is what yields Russellian monism. It is denied by
dualists (two fundamental kinds), physicalists (the base is physical), idealists (the base is
mental), and Russellian monists, who hold the intrinsic natures are (proto)phenomenal rather than
genuinely neutral.

Distinct from the base of [Russellian monism](../positions/russellian-monism.md)
([its categorical base](consciousness-categorical-not-dispositional.md)): neutral monism
keeps the base *neutral*, whereas Russellian monism makes that intrinsic base the very thing
consciousness is. Neutral monism is the historical parent of the Russellian view.

### In plain terms

Mind and matter aren't two ultimate kinds of thing, and neither is built from the other. Both are
patterns in a single, more basic stuff that is in itself neither mental nor physical — like the
same dots arranged once into a "mental" picture and once into a "physical" one.
