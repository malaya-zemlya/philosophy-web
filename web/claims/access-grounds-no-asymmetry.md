The first-person/third-person access gap is reciprocal and fully general
([symmetric access gap](access-gap-symmetric.md)): no subject can directly feel another's
experience, for *any* pair of minds. Because the gap holds everywhere, it cannot do the work of
distinguishing a machine's moral standing from a human's. Any moral-status asymmetry must be argued
on other grounds; first-person access (see [first-person-access](../concepts/first-person-access.md)) is not it.

This is the targeted denial of [the access-based moral
asymmetry](access-grounds-moral-status-asymmetry.md). Its scope is narrow: it does *not* assert that machines *have* moral status — that would
need a positive argument, such as [behavioural parity](../arguments/behavioral-parity.md) — but only
removes first-person access as a ground for denying moral status to them. It would be resisted by a
proponent of [first-person-access](../arguments/first-person-access.md) who insists the machine has no first-person standpoint
at all, so that the symmetry is illusory.

Distinct from [substrate-independence](substrate-independence.md) because that claim is about whether mental states
depend on their physical substrate; this one is narrowly about whether *access* can warrant a
moral-status asymmetry.

### In plain terms

Not being able to directly feel another's experience is true of *every* pair of minds, not just
human-and-machine — so it can't be the thing that makes a human matter morally while a machine
doesn't. If there's any such moral difference, it has to be argued some other way; the inside-view
gap won't deliver it. (This doesn't claim machines *do* matter — it just removes one reason people
give for saying they don't.)
