There is some natural property *P* of the brain in virtue of which the brain is the basis of
consciousness. The link between neural process and felt experience is, on this claim, a *natural*
fact — not a miracle, not a non-physical addition, and not a brute, inexplicable surd. It is the
naturalist commitment at the heart of McGinn's *transcendental naturalism*: the view that
consciousness has a perfectly natural explanation, even one we may be constitutionally unable to
grasp ([mcginn-1989](../sources/mcginn-1989.md)).

The claim is realist and naturalist about the *existence* of an explanation, not about its
accessibility. It explicitly does not say the explanation is dualistic, nor that we can comprehend
it — the latter is denied by [the cognitive-closure
claim](humans-closed-to-psychophysical-link.md). It is the premise that keeps mysterianism on the *naturalist* side of the ledger rather
than letting it collapse into dualism.

It is denied by the property dualist or panpsychist, who holds that no ordinary natural property
could close the explanatory gap, and by the eliminativist or illusionist, who denies there is any
phenomenal explanandum to be explained in the first place.

Distinct from [the substrate-independence thesis](substrate-independence.md): that concerns
which *substrates* can bear a mind, whereas this asserts only that *some* natural brain property
grounds consciousness, taking no stand on whether silicon could share it.

### In plain terms

This says that whatever it is about the brain that produces conscious experience, it's an ordinary
part of nature — some physical feature of the brain, not magic and not a soul added from outside. It
deliberately stops short of two further claims: it doesn't say we humans could ever understand that
feature (a separate, gloomier thesis, [that we're
cognitively closed to it](humans-closed-to-psychophysical-link.md)), and it doesn't say the explanation is non-physical. It's the "still
natural" half of the mysterian position: the mystery is in our reach, not in nature.
