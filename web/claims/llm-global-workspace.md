Modern language models maintain a small, privileged set of internal representations — the
[J-space](../concepts/j-space.md) — that they can report, deliberately modulate, and reason with, atop a
much larger volume of automatic processing that they cannot. This set plays the computational role
that global workspace theory assigns to consciously accessible information in humans: a common
format into which intermediate results are written once and read by many downstream processes,
enabling flexible reasoning and report ([gurnee-2026-workspace](../sources/gurnee-2026-workspace.md)).

### Scope

The claim is functional through and through. It does not assert that language models are conscious,
that anything is felt, or that the access/phenomenal relation is settled — the source takes no
position on [that question](../concepts/access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md). Nor does it assert the
full architecture that [global workspace theory](../positions/global-workspace.md) ascribes to the brain:
the model has no separable input processors, its broadcast occurs within a single feedforward pass
rather than recurrent loops, and whether entry to the workspace involves ignition-like competition
is unclear. What is asserted is functional equivalence in the five signature respects
([access-functional-signature](access-functional-signature.md)), plus some structural echoes (intermediate-layer locus,
limited capacity, broadcast-style connectivity).

### Who would deny it

A recurrent-processing theorist, for whom feedforward propagation is precisely what unconscious
processing looks like, however workspace-like its functional profile; a sceptic of the Jacobian-lens
methodology, who reads the J-space as an artefact of a verbalizability-defined probe; and a
biological naturalist can accept the claim while denying it any bearing on consciousness, since it
concerns computational organization, not substrate.

Distinct from [consciousness-is-global-broadcast](consciousness-is-global-broadcast.md) because that is a thesis about what makes
a state *conscious*; this is an empirical claim about the organization of language models that
deliberately stops short of any consciousness attribution. Distinct from
[llm-has-mental-states](llm-has-mental-states.md) because realizing a workspace organization is not yet realizing any
particular mental state. Distinct from [llm-functional-introspection](llm-functional-introspection.md) because introspective
report capacity is one property of the workspace, not the workspace itself.

### In plain terms

In people, only a sliver of what the brain does is available to be talked about, deliberately
entertained, and used for step-by-step thinking; the rest runs on autopilot. This claim says AI
language models have developed the same division of labour: a small "workspace" of ideas the model
can name, hold, and reason with, sitting on top of a mass of automatic processing it cannot. It is
a claim about how the model is organized — not a claim that the model is conscious or feels
anything.
