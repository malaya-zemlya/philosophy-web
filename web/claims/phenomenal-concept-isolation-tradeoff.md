A phenomenal concept cannot be at once *informative* enough to ground our knowledge that we are
conscious and *isolated* enough to deflate the explanatory gap. The two jobs pull in opposite
directions, and no single concept can do both.

### What the claim asserts

The *phenomenal-concept strategy* ([loar-1990](../sources/loar-1990.md)) needs the first-person, demonstrative
concept of a state to be *cognitively isolated* — sharing no inferential corridor with the
third-person role-concept — so that the cross-guise identity question stays residually intelligible
and the explanatory-gap datum E is predicted by strong functionalism (see
[phenomenal-concept deflation](../arguments/gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts.md)). But that same
isolation drains the concept of informativeness about the property it purports to track: a concept
inferentially sealed from its referent's nature grounds no knowledge of that nature. Hence the
trade-off. To the degree the phenomenal concept is isolated enough to flatten $P(E\mid \text{H1})$,
it is too sealed to ground our knowledge that we are phenomenally conscious; to the degree it is
informative enough to ground that self-knowledge, it is not isolated enough to guarantee E's
residual intelligibility by architecture alone.

### Scope

This is a claim about the strategy's internal *cost structure*, not a denial that phenomenal
concepts exist or are special. It targets only the conjunction the strategy needs. It does not
claim that phenomenal concepts ground *no* self-knowledge; it claims that the strategy's
*deflationary use* of them purchases that result only by undercutting self-knowledge.

### Who would deny it

Phenomenal-concept-strategy functionalists who hold that a concept can be referentially isolated
yet epistemically grounding, and who advance the deflation in
[phenomenal-concept deflation](../arguments/gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts.md); and *acquaintance
theorists* who think both informativeness and isolation can be had at once — and who then owe a
specification of the corridor.

The claim is distinct from the [phenomenal residue](phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md), which asserts that
role under-determines phenomenal character (a metaphysical thesis): this asserts an epistemic
trade-off internal to a particular *deflationary move*, and would have force even granting that the
residue is metaphysically open. It is also distinct from
[role-grain-is-organisational](role-grain-is-organisational.md), which concerns the grain of role-individuation rather than
the epistemology of phenomenal concepts.

### In plain terms

One side tries to explain away the stubborn feeling that consciousness is "something extra" like
this: we grasp our experiences through a special inside concept that's *sealed off* from our
scientific concepts, so the "are these the same thing?" question is bound to always feel open
([gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts](../arguments/gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts.md)). This claim catches that move in a bind. The very
sealing-off that keeps the question feeling open *also* cuts the inside concept off from telling you
anything real about your experience. So either the concept is sealed (and then it can't ground your
knowledge that you're even conscious) or it's informative (and then it isn't sealed enough to do the
explaining-away). It can't be both at once.
