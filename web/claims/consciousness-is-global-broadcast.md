A state is conscious only if its content is *globally broadcast* — made widely available to the
brain's many specialist subsystems (memory, language, motor control) for report and flexible use.
On this claim consciousness is a matter of a content entering a "global workspace" and being shared
widely, rather than remaining locked inside a single module. It is the core of global workspace
theory, introduced by Bernard Baars ([baars-1988](../sources/baars-1988.md)); Stanislas Dehaene's *global neuronal
workspace* supplies the neural implementation ([dehaene-naccache-2001](../sources/dehaene-naccache-2001.md)).

This is most naturally a thesis about the *access* side of the
[access/phenomenal distinction](../concepts/access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md): it explains what makes
a content widely usable and reportable. Whether global broadcast also constitutes *phenomenal*
character — the felt quality of experience — is exactly what critics say it leaves open.

It is denied by those who hold that phenomenal consciousness can *overflow* access (Ned Block
[block-2007](../sources/block-2007.md)), so that broadcast is not necessary for felt experience, and by phenomenal
realists who deny that wide availability could ever suffice for felt character.

Distinct from [higher-order representation](consciousness-is-higher-order-representation.md),
which requires a dedicated state *about* another mental state rather than mere broadcast, and from
[integrated information](consciousness-is-integrated-information.md), which turns on intrinsic
cause-effect structure rather than functional broadcast.

### In plain terms

The idea: a thought or perception becomes conscious when it is shared across the whole brain — put
on a kind of central noticeboard that the speech, memory, and action systems can all read — rather
than staying trapped in one specialist circuit. The doubt some raise: this explains how information
becomes usable and reportable, but maybe not why it *feels* like anything to have it.
