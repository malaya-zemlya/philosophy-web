A candidate theory of consciousness meets the [minimal
requirements](../concepts/minimal-theory-of-consciousness.md) only if it does two things: give an account of [access-consciousness](../concepts/access-consciousness.md), and
solve the meta-problem ([paradox-of-phenomenal-judgment](../concepts/paradox-of-phenomenal-judgment.md)) — explaining, in terms its own
metaphysics can support, why we are compelled to talk of a hard problem. A theory silent on either
explanandum has not cleared the entry bar, however rich its further apparatus.

The requirements are a floor, not a full account of what makes a theory good: they do not say that a
theory clearing the bar is *true*, only that one missing either item is disqualified. They would be
rejected by a strong phenomenal realist who holds the meta-problem optional — that explaining
experience need not include explaining our talk and judgments about it — and by a theorist who treats
access as beside the point (some [IIT](../positions/integrated-information-theory.md) readings, where a theory
of report is not what is wanted). Distinct from the requirements it invokes:
[minimal-theory-of-consciousness](../concepts/minimal-theory-of-consciousness.md) *defines* the two explananda, while this claim *asserts*
that meeting them is a necessary condition on any theory of consciousness, and so is the premise an
argument wields when it charges a rival with leaving one of the two boxes empty.
