Conscious understanding is not algorithmic: no Turing-computable process reproduces it. The route to
this conclusion is *Gödelian* — for any consistent formal system adequate for arithmetic there are
truths unprovable within it, yet a human mathematician can see that they are true, so human insight
is held to outrun any fixed algorithm. Consciousness must therefore exploit *non-computable* physics,
proposed to be objective quantum-state reduction (orchestrated objective reduction, Orch-OR, with
Stuart Hameroff). The Gödelian argument is J. R. Lucas's (1961; [lucas-1961](../sources/lucas-1961.md)); the
non-computability thesis is Roger Penrose's, in *The Emperor's New Mind* (1989;
[penrose-1989](../sources/penrose-1989.md)) and *Shadows of the Mind* (1994; [penrose-1994](../sources/penrose-1994.md)).

This is a claim about the *computational character* of consciousness — that it is non-algorithmic —
paired with a specific physical hypothesis, Orch-OR, as the proposed mechanism. Its most contested
step is the Gödelian premise that human insight is non-algorithmic, disputed by Hilary Putnam,
Solomon Feferman, and many others.

It is denied by computationalists and functionalists, for whom cognition just is computation, and by
the critics of the Gödelian anti-mechanism argument.

Distinct from [substrate independence](substrate-independence.md): this does not demand a
*biological* substrate but one exploiting *non-computable physics*, so it denies specifically the
*computational/algorithmic* realisation of mind — and hence bears against
[LLMs have mental states](llm-has-mental-states.md).

### In plain terms

The idea that being conscious involves something a computer program can't do. The argument leans on
a result from logic (Gödel's theorem): any fixed system of rules for proving mathematical truths will
leave some true statements it can't prove, yet a human can often see that those statements are true.
Penrose takes this to show that human understanding isn't just rule-following — it isn't an
algorithm — so the brain must use a kind of physics ordinary computers can't imitate. Many logicians
and philosophers dispute the key step from "mathematicians can do this" to "no algorithm could."
