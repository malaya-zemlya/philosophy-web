The halting oracle ([halting-oracle](../concepts/halting-oracle.md)) is a consistent, precisely specified object of a
developed mathematical theory — relative computability — and yet no physical device can be one.
Given that no Turing machine decides the halting problem ([halting-problem-undecidable](halting-problem-undecidable.md))
and that every physically realizable computation is Turing-computable
([the physical Church–Turing thesis](physical-church-turing-thesis.md)), the oracle is
coherent and theorizable but unbuildable.

The claim deliberately pairs *mathematical coherence* with *physical unrealizability*, and two
caveats keep the pairing honest. First, the unrealizability inherits the *contingency* of the
physical Church–Turing thesis: if hypercomputation turns out to be physically possible, the
oracle may be realizable after all. Second, the oracle is *metaphysically* possible — it is a
consistent abstract structure, and there is a possible world containing one. What fails is
*physical* realizability, not coherence or metaphysical possibility.

Someone might deny the claim from either flank: a hypercomputation theorist who rejects the
physical Church–Turing thesis, and so holds the oracle *is* physically realizable; or a strict
finitist or formalist who denies that the oracle is a legitimately coherent object at all.

This is distinct from [the unbuildable lookup
table](lookup-table-physically-unrealisable.md), another coherent-but-physically-impossible object — a behaviour-matching table too large
for the universe to store. That case fails on *resource bounds*; the oracle fails on
*uncomputability*. They are two different routes to "specifiable but unbuildable."

### In plain terms

You can write down exactly what a halting oracle would be, prove theorems about what it could do, and
teach a whole course on it — it's a completely coherent idea. And yet, on standard physics, you could
never actually construct one. So it sits in a striking spot: fully thinkable and mathematically real,
but physically out of reach. (Two honest footnotes: this assumes nature has no exotic "super-computers"
hiding in it; and the oracle is still *possible in the abstract* — it's only the *building* of one
that's blocked.)
