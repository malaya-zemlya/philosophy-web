Suppose a decision is undetermined, and everything that leads up to it consists of events involving
the agent — her desires, her reasons, her deliberative states — probabilistically causing the
outcome. Then, holding the complete prior state fixed, either option can still occur; and since the
agent's contribution is exhausted by those prior states, no *further* fact about the agent
determines which one does. Whatever tips the balance is not her. The agent, considered as the
source who resolves the choice, has disappeared from the story (Pereboom,
[2014](../sources/pereboom-2014.md)).

The claim is the conclusion of the [disappearing-agent objection](../arguments/disappearing-agent.md)
and the sharpest form of the *luck objection*: adding indeterminism to an event-causal picture adds
openness ([alternatives-genuinely-open](alternatives-genuinely-open.md)) without adding authorship. It concerns
*event-causal* libertarianism specifically — its standard escape is
[agent causation](../concepts/agent-causation.md), which posits the agent-as-substance as exactly the
settling item the event-causal story lacks. Who denies it: event-causal libertarians such as Kane,
for whom being probabilistically caused by the agent's own reasons-states *is* all the settling
that agency requires.
