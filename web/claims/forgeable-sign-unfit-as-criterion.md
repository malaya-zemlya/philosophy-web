Because a cheaply forgeable sign can be produced without the very condition it is supposed to
signal, it cannot be allowed to *carry the weight* of conferring moral or legal standing: resting
standing on it would flood the category with false positives. A test that anyone can counterfeit
on demand admits the counterfeits along with the genuine cases.

This is a *normative-epistemic bridge premise*. It moves from the descriptive claim that the sign
is gameable ([rights-criterion-will-be-gamed](rights-criterion-will-be-gamed.md)) to the normative conclusion that the sign
must not serve as the criterion — the criterion-gaming dynamic of [criterion-gaming](../concepts/criterion-gaming.md)
turned into a disqualification. It is the load-bearing and weakest step of the
[rights-sign-forgeable](../arguments/rights-sign-forgeable.md).

It would be denied by anyone invoking the *false-positive / base-rate* distinction: a sign that
*can* be forged is not thereby worthless evidence. Fingerprints can be forged yet still count; on
this view forgeability bounds the rate of false positives without nullifying evidential value, so a
gameable sign may remain a defeasible criterion rather than no criterion at all.

Distinct from [rights-criterion-will-be-gamed](rights-criterion-will-be-gamed.md), which asserts that the gaming *will* occur;
this asserts the normative consequence that such a sign is *therefore* disqualified as a criterion.

### In plain terms

This is the step from "the sign can be cheaply faked" to "so it can't be what we hang rights on."
If you can produce the sign without the suffering it's meant to indicate, then making it the
official test floods the category with false positives — undeserving cases waved through. So a
cheap-to-fake sign shouldn't carry that weight. It's the load-bearing (and most disputed) link in
the caution argument: critics note that forgeable signs — fingerprints again — can still be
genuinely useful evidence, just not foolproof.
