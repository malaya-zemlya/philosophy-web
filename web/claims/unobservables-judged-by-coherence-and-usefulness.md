When a question concerns a difference unobservable *in principle* — not hard to measure, but such that
no possible observation comes out differently either way ("is the light *really* on inside?") — any
dispute over it reduces to one party's word against another's. The most that can be asked of such a
concept is whether it is *coherent* and whether it is *useful*: whether it earns its keep by doing work
somewhere — predicting, organising, distinguishing. Quantum amplitude and incomputable numbers are
coherent and useful; an object that changes colour only when nobody looks at it is coherent but useless.

The examples bear weight. A quantum amplitude is never observed as such — only its squared modulus shows
up, in the frequencies of outcomes — yet the concept pays its way many times over: it predicts, it
unifies, and refusing it costs you physics. An incomputable number can never be written out or generated
by any effective procedure, yet such numbers are indispensable to the theory of computation. So
unobservability in principle is, by itself, no disqualification; this standard is deliberately more
hospitable than crude verificationism. What disqualifies is *idleness*: a posit that makes no observable
difference *and* does no organising work — the colour that changes only while unwatched — retains
coherence alone, and coherence alone is cheap.

The standard has a pedigree. It is kin to the Peirce–James pragmatic maxim, that a difference must make
a difference ([peirce-1878](../sources/peirce-1878.md); [james-1907](../sources/james-1907.md)), and to Carnap's treatment of external
questions, to be judged by fruitfulness rather than correspondence ([carnap-1950](../sources/carnap-1950.md)). It is kin
too to Smart's "nomological danglers" ([smart-1959](../sources/smart-1959.md)): psychic posits that dangle outside the
nomological net, lawfully connected to nothing, are to be shaved off by Occam's razor — which is this
standard's usefulness axis turned into an argument. The standing complaint against
[epiphenomenalism](../positions/epiphenomenalism.md) is the same verdict in the particular: a feel that makes
no causal difference ([that mental states are causally inert](mental-states-causally-inert.md)) is
the unwatched colour-change wearing a serious expression. The standard is nonetheless distinct from
these ancestors in what it *keeps*: it does not rule in-principle unobservables meaningless (the
amplitude and the incomputable number are honest citizens), and it does not relativize truth to
linguistic frameworks. It grades such posits on exactly two axes — coherence and earned usefulness —
and is silent about everything observable.

Its scope is narrow. It applies only where no possible evidence discriminates, and says nothing about
matters that are merely *practically* unobservable. And "coherent, not useful" is a verdict of idleness,
not of falsehood: the claim licenses retiring an idle posit from argument, not declaring it false. It
would be denied by a metaphysical realist about the relevant domain — for instance a phenomenal realist
holding that "is there something it is like to be that system?" has a determinate answer whose
importance is independent of any work the concept does ([the phenomenal-residue
thesis](phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md) is the natural home of such a denial) — and by anyone who thinks coherence plus a priori
reasoning alone can settle substantive questions about in-principle unobservables.

### In plain terms

Some questions are set up so that no test could ever tell the answers apart — "does this thing *really*
have an inner light on, or does it just act exactly as if it did?" For questions like that, it's
permanently your word against mine. So this claim proposes the only scorecard left: is the idea
*coherent* (does it make sense), and is it *useful* (does it help you predict, organise, or distinguish
anything)? Physics keeps "quantum amplitude" — you can never see one directly, but it runs the most
accurate predictions ever made. Mathematics keeps numbers no computer could ever print out, because
theory needs them. But "objects change colour only when nobody looks" — perfectly consistent, and
perfectly worthless: it predicts nothing, helps nowhere. Keep the first kind, retire the second.
