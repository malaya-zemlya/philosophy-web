title: The physical effects of mental causes are not systematically overdetermined

When a mental event causes a physical effect, that effect is not *also* brought about by a distinct,
independently sufficient non-physical cause running alongside the physical one. Bodily movements are not
routinely double-caused, the way a death might be overdetermined by two simultaneous fatal wounds. Such
coincidence might happen once by fluke; it does not happen *systematically*, for every voluntary action
of every agent.

**Scope.** The claim rules out only *systematic* overdetermination, not the odd genuine case. Its work is
to close the gap the [completeness-of-physics](../concepts/completeness-of-physics.md) leaves open: completeness guarantees a sufficient
physical cause, and this premise denies a second, non-physical one standing beside it, so that together
they yield the [causal-closure](../concepts/causal-closure.md) of the physical. It is the premise an interactionist dualist is
most tempted to challenge — by embracing overdetermination, or by denying that the physical cause was
sufficient after all. Kim ([kim-2005](../sources/kim-2005.md)) builds it into the exclusion principle at the heart of his
[causal argument](../arguments/causal-argument-for-physicalism.md).
