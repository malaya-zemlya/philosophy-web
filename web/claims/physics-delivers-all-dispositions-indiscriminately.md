The complete physical, or counterfactual, fact about a system includes *every* causal disposition it
has — its mass, its thermal conductivity, the squishy noise it makes when struck with a blunt object —
not only those one judges cognitively relevant. Physics does not pre-sort dispositions into
"organisational" and "mere side-effect."

This is an observation about what "causal structure" delivers when it is taken raw. If *all*
dispositions count, then the brain's structure is shared by no silicon system (which makes no squishy
noise), and multiple realizability fails immediately — so [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md)
must be reading "causal structure" as a *restricted*, organisational subset.

Almost no one denies the claim as stated; the live dispute is whether the restriction to the relevant
subset can be made on a principled basis — see [the
demand for a decomposition invariant](functionalism-owes-decomposition-invariant.md) and the [relevance-filter
regress](../arguments/relevance-filter-regress.md).

This is distinct from [the relativity of functional
decomposition](functional-decomposition-relative.md): that concerns the non-uniqueness of the *carving*, while this concerns the
non-selective *inclusion* of dispositions even within a fixed carving.

### In plain terms

Physics doesn't tidily separate a thing's "thinking-relevant" powers from its other powers — the
complete physical story of a brain bundles its weight, its warmth, and the squelch it makes when
poked right in with the signal-passing. Nothing in the raw physics flags which bits are the
mind-relevant ones. (Why it matters: if you had to count *all* of them, a silicon chip could never
match a brain — chips don't squelch — so anyone who says "a mind just is its cause-and-effect
structure" must be quietly keeping only *some* of that structure, and then owes you a rule for which.)
