The functional role by which a mental state is individuated is specified neither at the coarse grain
of gross input–output behaviour nor at the fine grain of the physical realiser's microchemistry,
but at the *intermediate* grain of the system's internal counterfactual-causal organisation — the
structure of state-to-state transitions among its components. Physical details such as spike
timing, neuromodulator diffusion, or transistor physics matter to the role only insofar as they
make a counterfactual difference to that transition structure.

This is a claim about the *correct grain of role-individuation* for
[functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md), not about whether any particular system meets it. It
asserts that the grain is both substrate-neutral, hence multiply realisable in silicon, and
demanding of internal organisation, hence not satisfied by mere input–output mimicry. It does not
by itself say that any given machine — a language model, or the blockhead — meets or fails the
grain; those are downstream applications.

It would be denied by defenders of pure input–output (machine-table or behaviourist)
functionalism; by fine-grained chauvinists who hold that biologically specific microstructure is
individuative; and by anyone who holds that [the grain dilemma](../arguments/role-grain-dilemma.md) is
exhaustive between coarse and biological-fine, with no principled middle.

Distinct from [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md) proper, which leaves the grain of "role" open
(listing machine-state, role, and commonsense senses): this claim *fixes* the grain at one level
and motivates the choice. Distinct from [substrate independence](substrate-independence.md),
which asserts that substrate does no individuative work: this asserts *which level of organisation*
does the work instead, and is the specification that, if true, secures substrate independence
against the grain dilemma.

### In plain terms

This picks the "just right" level of detail for defining a mind. Not too coarse (plain
input-and-output behavior — that would let a dumb cheat-sheet count) and not too fine (exact brain
chemistry — that would shut out every computer). The sweet spot in between: a mind *is* its inner
pattern of cause-and-effect — which inner states trigger which others. Physical details matter only
insofar as they change that pattern. The payoff it's aiming for: the pattern can run on silicon (so
machines aren't ruled out) yet a mere answer-fetcher lacks it (so behavior-fakers are ruled out).
