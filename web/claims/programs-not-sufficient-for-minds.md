Running the right computer program is neither *constitutive of* a mind nor *sufficient for* one: no system
has mental states *in virtue of* implementing a program. This is conclusion C1 of Searle's formal
[Chinese Room](../concepts/chinese-room.md) derivation ([searle-1990](../sources/searle-1990.md)), following from
[A1](programs-are-syntactic.md) (programs are syntactic), [A2](minds-have-semantics.md) (minds have
semantics), and [A3](syntax-insufficient-for-semantics.md) (syntax is not sufficient for semantics). It
is the direct denial of **strong AI** ([strong-weak-ai](../concepts/strong-weak-ai.md)) — the thesis that the right program
*is* a mind — while leaving *weak* AI (the computer as a model of mind) untouched.

**Scope.** C1 concerns *programs as such* — pure formal implementation. It does *not* say machines can
never think, nor that artificial minds are impossible: a non-program artificial system with the right
causal powers might (the [Many Mansions Reply](../arguments/many-mansions-reply.md) presses exactly this gap).
What C1 denies is that *computation alone* delivers a mind.

**Who would deny it.** Computational [functionalists](../concepts/functionalism.md) and defenders of
[substrate independence](substrate-independence.md) deny C1: for them implementing the right
program-role just *is* having the corresponding mental states. Their objections — the
[Systems](../arguments/systems-reply.md), [Robot](../arguments/robot-reply.md), and
[Brain Simulator](../arguments/brain-simulator-reply.md) replies — all aim, in the end, to block the move to C1
by denying A3.

Distinct from [syntax-insufficient-for-semantics](syntax-insufficient-for-semantics.md) (A3): A3 is the *general* principle about syntax
and semantics; C1 is its *application to minds and programs* — the conclusion A3 is wielded to reach.

### In plain terms

The payoff of Searle's argument: no amount of running-the-right-software is, by itself, enough to *be* a
mind or to *have* one. A program is just shape-shuffling (step one), real minds actually mean things (step
two), and shuffling shapes can't manufacture meaning (step three) — so software alone can't add up to a
mind. Note the careful limit: this says *programs* aren't enough, not that machines can never think. A
suitably built machine with the right causal powers isn't ruled out — only the idea that the *code* is the
mind.
