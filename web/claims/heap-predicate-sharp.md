For the predicate "heap" there is a precise boundary — some exact grain-count $n$ such that a
collection of $n$ grains is a heap and one of $n-1$ grains is not. Bivalence follows: given a cutoff,
every case falls determinately on one side or the other.

This is the naive realist picture of a vague predicate, isolated to its load-bearing commitment: the
*existence* of a cutoff. It says nothing about *where* the boundary lies; locating it is the
epistemicist's separate, and unmet, burden.

The claim is denied by most theories of vagueness — the degree theorist (the verdict comes in
degrees, so the verdict-space is not discrete), the supervaluationist or other indeterminist (some
cases lack any fact of the matter), and the deflationist (there is no real heap/non-heap distinction
at all). These are three of the horns of the [continuity-argument-schema](../concepts/continuity-argument-schema.md). Only the
epistemicist *keeps* the claim, paying for it with a sharp boundary that is in principle unknowable.

Distinct from [sorites tolerance](sorites-tolerance.md), the step-insensitivity premise: this
claim is instead the *target* the paradox refutes. The [sorites paradox](../arguments/sorites-paradox.md)
attacks it precisely by leaning on tolerance.

### In plain terms

This is the stubborn common-sense idea that for "heap" there's some exact number — say, 1,847 grains
— where you cross from heap to non-heap, even if nobody could ever tell you which number it is. Every
pile then counts definitely as one or the other. It's the picture the heap paradox sets out to
attack, and most people conclude *something* about it has to give.
