From the first-person perspective, what one is now experiencing — and that one is experiencing at all
— is a definite matter. It does not vary with how some outside observer chooses to describe or
decompose the underlying system. Phenomenal consciousness presents itself as *determinate* and
*intrinsic*: a fact in its own right, not a fact relative to a chosen carving of the brain into
parts.

The intuition has prominent homes on both sides of the consciousness debate. Searle presses an
*intrinsic existence* point for consciousness — that it is a real, first-personal feature of the
world, not an observer-relative description projected onto a system ([searle-1992](../sources/searle-1992.md)).
Integrated information theory builds the same idea in as a founding axiom, treating the intrinsic
existence of experience (its being there for the subject, independent of any external observer) as a
datum any theory must honour ([tononi-2016](../sources/tononi-2016.md)).

The claim is about *determinacy*, and it is neutral on what consciousness ultimately is. It does not
deny that the *edges* of experience — peripheral or inattentive content — can be genuinely vague. It
asserts only that there is a fact of the matter about one's phenomenal state, rather than merely a
fact-relative-to-a-description.

It would be denied by a theorist willing to accept phenomenal *indeterminacy*: that for some of one's
experience there is simply no fact of the matter about what, or whether, one is experiencing. That is
the harder bullet a functionalist may bite to resist
[the decomposition-indeterminacy objection](../arguments/decomposition-indeterminacy.md).

Distinct from [privileged-first-person-access](privileged-first-person-access.md), which concerns the *epistemic* authority of
introspection; this concerns the *determinacy of the phenomenal facts themselves*, however they are
known.

### In plain terms

There's a definite fact about what you're feeling right now — and that you're feeling anything at all
— not something that shifts depending on how an outsider chooses to describe your brain. Your
experience presents itself as just *there*, settled. (It allows that the fuzzy *edges* of experience
— stuff at the corner of attention — can be vague; it only insists there's a genuine fact of the
matter, not a make-it-up-as-you-carve-it situation.)
