Because one has direct acquaintance with one's own suffering but only an undischarged inference to a
machine's, one is justified in regarding oneself as a bearer of moral status in a way one is not
justified in regarding the machine. The asymmetry of regard is grounded in an asymmetry of access
(see [first-person-access](../concepts/first-person-access.md)).

The claim is about *justified regard*, offered as a candidate — negative-for-machines — answer to the
[moral status of LLMs](../questions/llm-moral-status.md). It is epistemic, not metaphysical: it does *not*
assert that the machine in fact lacks moral status, only that warrant for attributing it is absent in
the machine case while present in one's own. It lends support to [proteocentrism](../positions/proteocentrism.md) without
entailing it.

It would be denied by those who hold that third-person evidence can itself warrant a moral-status
attribution ([behavioural parity](../arguments/behavioral-parity.md)), and by the symmetry objection
([access-symmetry-rebuttal](../arguments/access-symmetry-rebuttal.md)), which holds that no *human/machine* asymmetry is available
here at all.

Distinct from [proteocentrism](../positions/proteocentrism.md) because that is a metaphysical thesis — only carbon life
can bear consciousness — whereas this is the weaker epistemic claim that *warrant* tracks
first-person access.

### In plain terms

You feel your own suffering directly; with a machine you can only infer, from the outside, that it
*might* be suffering. So this claim says you're entitled to count yourself as morally important in a
way you're not yet entitled to count the machine. It's a point about what you're *justified* in
believing — not a flat verdict that the machine is empty inside.
