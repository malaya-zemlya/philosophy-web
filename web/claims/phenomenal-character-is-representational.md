What it is like to undergo an experience just is the experience's *representational content* of a
certain kind. Qualia are not intrinsic *mental paint* but ways the world is represented to be; to
introspect "how it feels" is to be aware of *what* is represented. On one influential development
the relevant content is *PANIC* — poised, abstract, non-conceptual, intentional content
([tye-2000](../sources/tye-2000.md)) — and related naturalising versions are due to Dretske ([dretske-1995](../sources/dretske-1995.md))
and Harman ([harman-1990](../sources/harman-1990.md)).

This is a *first-order* representationalism ([order-of-representation](../concepts/order-of-representation.md)): the content need
not itself be represented by a higher-order state in order to be conscious. Because it naturalises
phenomenal character through an independent (teleosemantic) theory of representation, it sits among
the type-A/type-B physicalist options surveyed in [materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md).

It is denied by those who treat qualia as intrinsic and non-representational (appealing to
inverted-spectrum and inverted-earth cases), by higher-order theorists, and by phenomenal realists
who assert [a phenomenal residue](phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md) left over once representational content
is fixed.

Distinct from [the higher-order claim](consciousness-is-higher-order-representation.md), which
requires a *higher-order* state: this one locates phenomenal character in *first-order*,
world-directed content.

### In plain terms

The "feel" of an experience is nothing over and above what the experience tells you about the world.
Seeing red is your visual system representing "red surface there"; the redness that seems to be in
your mind is just the red it assigns to the world. So feelings aren't inner stuff that science can't
reach — they're the world as represented to you. The test cases ask whether two people could
represent all the same things yet still *feel* differently.
