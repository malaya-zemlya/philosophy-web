For any physical effect that is caused at all, there is a wholly physical cause that suffices to bring
it about. One never needs to reach outside the physical domain to find a sufficient cause of a physical
happening. This is the [completeness-of-physics](../concepts/completeness-of-physics.md) stated as a single premise.

**Scope.** "Sufficient" is the operative word: the claim does not say the physical cause is the *only*
cause, only that it is *enough*. It therefore leaves open (until [no-systematic-overdetermination](no-systematic-overdetermination.md)
is added) that a physical effect might also have a non-physical cause. To accommodate indeterminism it is
read as a claim about *chances*: in so far as a physical effect's probability is fixed at all, it is fixed
by physical antecedents.

**Status.** Defenders treat it as an empirical result, not an a priori axiom — Papineau ([papineau-2002](../sources/papineau-2002.md))
argues it was established by the historical closing of the inventory of fundamental forces and is
underwritten by the conservation laws. Interactionist [dualism](../positions/dualism.md) is the principal view that
rejects it.
