A genuinely *precautionary* policy toward possible minds cannot count the cost of a false
positive while ignoring the cost of a false negative. Granting moral standing to a system that
turns out to be insentient wastes concern and constrains action needlessly; withholding it from
a system that turns out to be sentient licenses unbounded mistreatment of a real subject. Both
are moral errors, and a policy that minimises only one of them is not caution but a disguised
bet on which error is cheaper.

The point is structural, not a claim about which error is in fact worse in the AI case. It says
that the *balance* of the two error-costs is the thing a precautionary criterion must track, so
selecting a criterion by some other property — how hard it is to fake, say — can quietly
optimise against the wrong target. Jonathan Birch frames this as the management of "sentience
candidates" under proportionate precautions, in which under-attribution and over-attribution
both carry weight ([birch-2024](../sources/birch-2024.md)); Schwitzgebel and Garza press the
under-attribution side specifically for artificial minds ([schwitzgebel-garza-2015](../sources/schwitzgebel-garza-2015.md)).
