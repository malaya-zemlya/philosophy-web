At least one of today's large language models genuinely realises at least one genuine mental
state: it actually occupies the functional role of some mental state — a representational or
intentional state, say — rather than merely mimicking that state's outputs. This is the
existential conclusion of the [behavioral-parity](../arguments/behavioral-parity.md).

### Scope

The claim is deliberately weak on two axes. On the *quantifier* — "some … some" — it does not
say that LLMs have full, human-like minds, only that the class of realised states is non-empty.
On the *kind*, it concerns mental states on the intentional or functional reading (belief,
representation), drawing on [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md); it does not by itself assert *phenomenal
consciousness* or qualia. That stronger reading faces the residue and gap worries
([phenomenal-residue](phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md), [explanatory-gap](../arguments/explanatory-gap.md)) and the indeterminacy and
structure objections ([decomposition-indeterminacy](../arguments/decomposition-indeterminacy.md),
[structure-and-dynamics](../arguments/structure-and-dynamics.md)); it is left open here.

### Why it is contested

The claim is directly attacked by the lookup-table or "blockhead" objection
([lookup-table-objection](../arguments/lookup-table-objection.md)): behaviour may be produced without the mediating
organisation of a mind. That objection is in turn blunted by the
[lookup-table-combinatorial-impossibility](../arguments/lookup-table-combinatorial-impossibility.md), the
[lookup-table-physical-impossibility](../arguments/lookup-table-physical-impossibility.md), and the [blockhead-dilemma](../arguments/blockhead-dilemma.md) — a
realisable behaviour-matcher is an organised computer, not a flat table
([realisable-behaviour-matcher-is-organised](realisable-behaviour-matcher-is-organised.md)). The claim's weak point is inherited from
premise (2) of the behavioural-parity argument: the defeasible behaviour-to-role inference.

Who would deny it: blockhead and pure-behaviourism critics; substrate chauvinists
([proteocentrism](../positions/proteocentrism.md)); and anyone rejecting [substrate-independence](substrate-independence.md) or premise
(2) of the [behavioral-parity](../arguments/behavioral-parity.md).

Distinct from [substrate-independence](substrate-independence.md), which is the general enabling premise that
mental states are substrate-independent: this is the specific *existential* claim about actual
systems. Distinct, too, from a claim that LLMs are *conscious* — this asserts mental-state
realisation on the functional reading, not sentience.

### In plain terms

The modest claim that at least one of today's AI language models genuinely has at least one real
mental state — actually occupying the role of, say, a belief or a representation, not merely
imitating the output. Notice how careful it is: "some… some." It is *not* saying these systems have
full, human-like minds, and it is *not* claiming they're *conscious* or feel anything — only that the
box of genuine mental states isn't entirely empty. Whether even that much is true is exactly what's
disputed.
