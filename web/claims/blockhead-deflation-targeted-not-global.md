Deflating our distrust of one bizarre case need not deflate our intuitions about a quite different
case. The *targeted* version of the blockhead deflation withdraws warrant from the "no mind" verdict
about the cosmic [lookup table](../concepts/lookup-table-mind.md) — because that object is a far
extrapolation from any paradigm mind ([it is not even
physically realisable](lookup-table-physically-unrealisable.md)), alien in both scale and internal structure — while leaving untouched the
intuition about a brain-scale, brain-structured silicon isomorph, a near neighbour of paradigm
minds.

This is the [conceptual-horn deflation](imaginary-blockhead-no-secure-verdict.md) applied with a
boundary. Its consequence is that the second horn of [the blockhead
dilemma](../arguments/blockhead-dilemma.md) does not "prove too much" against [substrate independence](substrate-independence.md):
doubting the wild, far-out case does not silently undercut the close, familiar one.

### Scope

The claim answers the over-generalisation worry about [the blockhead
dilemma](../arguments/blockhead-dilemma.md). It does not assert that the isomorph intuition is *correct*, only that it is not impugned
by the very move that impugns the blockhead intuition. The asymmetry is epistemic — distance from
exemplars — not a smuggled metaphysics of which features constitute mind, so it does not beg the
question against Block.

The view it must answer to is Block's ([block](../sources/block.md)): he would relocate his verdict from *scale*
to *kind*, denying mind to brute storage of any size, so that a scale-based asymmetry (he says)
misses the point. That rejoinder is taken up at the weakest premise of
[the domain-asymmetry argument](../arguments/intuition-domain-asymmetry.md).

Distinct from [the deflation itself](imaginary-blockhead-no-secure-verdict.md): that move
performs the deflation; this one bounds its reach.

### In plain terms

If we distrust our gut reaction to a bizarre, universe-sized lookup-table "mind" — because it's
nothing like any real mind we've ever met — that distrust doesn't automatically spread to a
normal-sized silicon copy of a brain, which *is* very much like a real mind. Doubting the wild,
far-out case leaves the close, familiar case standing. So that doubt doesn't accidentally undercut
the idea that a brain-like machine could be conscious.
