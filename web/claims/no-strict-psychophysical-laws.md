No exceptionless, predictive law links kinds picked out in mental vocabulary ("believes that it is
raining", "decides to leave") with kinds picked out in physical vocabulary. Whatever regularities
connect mind and brain are hedged, *ceteris paribus* generalizations, not the strict laws of a
finished physics. Donald Davidson's ground for the thesis ([1970](../sources/davidson-1970.md)) is that
mental ascription answers to holistic norms of rationality — beliefs and desires are attributed so
as to make the agent broadly coherent — and no such normative constraint can be captured in the
closed, exceptionless net of physical law.

**Scope.** The claim rules out strict *psychophysical* laws only. It does not deny that mental
events are physical events (Davidson affirms that), nor that there are rough, useful psychological
generalizations, nor that physical law is strict in its own domain. It is denied by
[type-identity theorists](../positions/type-identity.md), for whom the identity of mental and neural
kinds would supply exactly such bridge laws, and by any reductionist who expects a completed
neuroscience to deliver them.

### In plain terms

Physics has laws with no exceptions: given the setup, the outcome follows, full stop. This claim
says nothing like that will ever link thoughts to brain states — no rule of the form "whenever
someone believes X, neurons do Y, without exception." Psychology's rules always carry a silent
"other things being equal." Why? Because calling something a *belief* is partly about whether it
makes the person rational, and rationality can't be written into physics-style equations.
