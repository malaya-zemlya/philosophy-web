What it takes for you to persist is what it takes for a *human animal* to persist: the continuation
of a single living organism — one self-maintaining biological life — regardless of what happens to
its psychology (Olson, [1997](../sources/olson-1997.md)). You once persisted as an embryo with no
psychology at all, and you could persist into a persistent vegetative state with none again; memory
and character are things you *have*, not what you *are*.

**Scope.** The claim is a criterion of numerical identity over time for beings like us, answering
the [personal-identity question](../questions/personal-identity.md) biologically. It does not deny that
psychology is what we *care about*, nor that "person" may name a phase (like "adolescent") rather
than a substance; it locates persistence in the life, not the mind. It is denied by the
[psychological continuity theory](../positions/psychological-continuity-theory.md)
([identity-is-psychological-continuity](identity-is-psychological-continuity.md)) — most sharply in transplant cases, where the
intuition that the person goes with the transplanted brain
([transplant-intuition](../arguments/transplant-intuition.md)) contradicts this criterion's verdict that the person stays
with the living, empty-headed organism — and by
[further-fact theorists](identity-is-further-fact.md).

### In plain terms

You are an animal — a particular living human organism — and you last exactly as long as that
animal lives. You were once a fetus with no thoughts; you might one day be a patient with no
thoughts; both are still *you*, because the same life is running. Memories and personality matter
enormously, but they're features you carry, like a language you speak — losing them wouldn't
destroy you, just impoverish you. The hard case for this view is the brain transplant: if your
brain (with all your mental life) went into another body, this view has to say you'd stay behind
as the brainless body on the table — and most people find that verdict hard to accept.
