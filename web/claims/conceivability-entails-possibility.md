If a scenario is *ideally* conceivable — coherent under idealized rational reflection, with no concealed
contradiction — then it is metaphysically possible. This is the modal-epistemology bridge of the zombie
argument, and its most contested step. It is emphatically *not* the trivial claim that whatever we can
loosely picture is possible; it concerns *ideal* conceivability, what survives unlimited rational
scrutiny. On the two-dimensional version (David Chalmers, [chalmers-1996](../sources/chalmers-1996.md);
[chalmers-2002](../sources/chalmers-2002.md)), the principle reads more precisely: ideal *primary* conceivability entails
*primary* possibility, and for the zombie case the primary and secondary intensions are taken to
coincide.

The bridge is what converts [zombie-conceivable](zombie-conceivable.md) into a conclusion against physicalism, so the
whole dispute concentrates here.

### Argued both ways

It is not a brute premise. It is **defended** by modal rationalism ([modal-rationalism](../arguments/modal-rationalism.md)): once
the [primary-intension](../concepts/primary-intension.md) and [secondary-intension](../concepts/secondary-intension.md) are distinguished, the apparent
counterexamples are misdescribed possibilities
([aposteriori-necessities-are-misdescribed-possibilities](aposteriori-necessities-are-misdescribed-possibilities.md)), so ideal primary conceivability
tracks primary possibility. It is **attacked** by the a-posteriori-necessity objection
([conceivability-not-guide-to-possibility](../arguments/conceivability-not-guide-to-possibility.md)): Kripkean necessities
([some-necessities-knowable-only-a-posteriori](some-necessities-knowable-only-a-posteriori.md), [kripke-1980](../sources/kripke-1980.md)) already break the link,
so a zombie may be conceivable yet impossible — the type-B physicalist reading
([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md)). A deeper, allied **phenomenal-concept strategy**
([gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts](../arguments/gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts.md), [phenomenal-concept-isolation-tradeoff](phenomenal-concept-isolation-tradeoff.md))
explains the conceivability as an artefact of how phenomenal concepts work. Throughout, the crux is the
same: whether the *phenomenal* case has the appearance/reality gap that lets the water case escape.

The claim is distinct from [zombie-conceivable](zombie-conceivable.md) (the antecedent it operates on) and from
[intuition-warrant-tracks-trained-domain](intuition-warrant-tracks-trained-domain.md) (an epistemology of *intuitions about cases*); this
one is specifically about the conceivability→possibility inference.

### In plain terms

This is the hinge the whole zombie argument swings on: that *if you can coherently imagine something —
really coherently, with no hidden contradiction — then it is genuinely possible*. Grant it, and "I can
imagine a zombie" turns into "a zombie is possible," which would mean experience is something extra
beyond the physical. The classic pushback borrows from science: "water is H₂O" is something you *can*
imagine being false, yet it is necessarily true — so being able to imagine otherwise does not always
prove real possibility. Maybe zombies are like that: imaginable but impossible.
