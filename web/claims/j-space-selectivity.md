Only a small, non-routine fraction of a language model's processing depends on its
[J-space](../concepts/j-space.md). With the J-space suppressed, the model still speaks fluently, parses
its input, and performs a great deal of automatic inference; what fails under the same intervention
is explicit report and the more complex forms of flexible, internal reasoning
([gurnee-2026-workspace](../sources/gurnee-2026-workspace.md)). The dissociation mirrors the selectivity of conscious access in
humans, where the bulk of perceptual, motor, and linguistic computation proceeds automatically,
outside the small set of contents accessible at any moment.

The claim matters because it blocks a deflationary reading of the other workspace properties: the
J-space is not simply "the model's activations under another name" but a functionally distinguished
minority of them, bypassed by well-practised computation. It would be denied by a sceptic who
attributes the reasoning failures under suppression to generic damage from the intervention rather
than to the loss of a specific workspace; the matched-perturbation controls in the source are the
direct answer to that reading.
