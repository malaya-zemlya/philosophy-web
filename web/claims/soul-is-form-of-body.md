The soul or mind is the *form* of a living organism — its organising principle or actuality — and
not a separate substance lodged within it nor merely its matter. The relation is like that of a
statue's shape to the bronze: the shape is neither a second thing standing beside the bronze nor
identical with the bronze itself. Mind and body, on this view, are the form and matter of a single
substance, the living animal. The position descends from Aristotle's *De Anima*
([aristotle-de-anima](../sources/aristotle-de-anima.md)) and Aquinas ([aquinas-summa](../sources/aquinas-summa.md)), and is defended by modern
hylomorphists such as William Jaworski ([jaworski-2016](../sources/jaworski-2016.md)) and Anna Marmodoro
([marmodoro-2013](../sources/marmodoro-2013.md)).

This is a *hylomorphic* claim, set against both Cartesian substance dualism (which makes the mind
a distinct substance) and reductive materialism (which makes form nothing over and above the
material facts). It treats mental capacities as form-level features of a suitably organised living
thing.

It is denied by substance dualists, for whom the mind is a separate substance; by reductive
physicalists, for whom form adds nothing to the material facts; and by anyone who rejects the
Aristotelian form/matter framework altogether.

Distinct from [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md): although "form as organisation" can sound functionalist,
the hylomorphist ties form to a *living* substance with intrinsic teleology rather than to a
substrate-neutral causal role, so the claim does not by itself underwrite
[substrate-independence](substrate-independence.md).
