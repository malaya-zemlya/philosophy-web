Consciousness is a real, higher-level biological feature *caused by* lower-level neurobiological
processes and *realised in* the brain — much as digestion is caused by and realised in the stomach.
On this claim it is *ontologically irreducible*, having an essentially first-person character that
no third-person description captures, yet it is causally explained by, and nothing "over and above,"
the brain's specific causal powers. This is the core thesis of the *biological naturalism* John
Searle set out in *The Rediscovery of the Mind* ([searle-1992](../sources/searle-1992.md)).

The claim is physicalist but both anti-reductionist and anti-computationalist. Because it is the
brain's concrete *causal powers* that produce consciousness — and not the abstract program it might
be said to run — merely implementing the right syntax is insufficient for mind
([syntax-insufficient-for-semantics](syntax-insufficient-for-semantics.md), the moral of the [chinese-room](../concepts/chinese-room.md)).

It is denied by strong-AI functionalists ([functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md),
[substrate independence](substrate-independence.md)), who hold that the right causal
*organisation* suffices regardless of substrate. It is also distinct from
[type-identity](mental-states-are-brain-state-types.md) — a reductive identification of mental
states with neural state-types — since biological naturalism is non-reductive; and from
proteocentrism, since what it demands is the right *causal powers*, which need not be
carbon-specific, even if Searle holds that only suitably brain-like systems are so far known to
have them.

### In plain terms

Brains make consciousness the way stomachs make digestion: it is a perfectly physical product of a
living organ, not a ghostly extra and not a piece of software. So you cannot get a mind just by
running the right program on any machine — you would need something with the brain's actual physical
powers to cause it.
