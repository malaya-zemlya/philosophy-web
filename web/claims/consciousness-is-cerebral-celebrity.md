A mental content is conscious just to the degree that it achieves "cerebral celebrity": it
perseveres, monopolizes resources, and thereby exerts widespread influence on memory, on the
control of behavior, and — the effect Daniel Dennett singles out as most telling — on what the
subject can verbally report ([dennett-1993](../sources/dennett-1993.md)). Consciousness is fame in the brain, and
nothing more: there is no further property a famous content acquires, no theater it enters and no
extra glow it takes on, over and above winning that competition for influence. The underlying
picture is the Multiple Drafts Model of [dennett-1991](../sources/dennett-1991.md): many contents circulate in
parallel, and which of them "were conscious" is settled only by which ones win uptake — canonically,
availability to verbal probing.

Two features distinguish the claim from its nearest neighbour,
[consciousness-is-global-broadcast](consciousness-is-global-broadcast.md). First, it is *gradable and deflationary*: fame comes in
degrees with no sharp threshold, whereas the broadcast thesis posits entry into a definite
workspace, a functional difference in kind. Second, it *weights verbalization*: where global
workspace theory treats report as one downstream consumer among many, Dennett makes connection to
verbal report the most symptomatic mark of the conscious. The claim is also offered as an account
of consciousness *tout court* — Dennett denies there is a further
[phenomenal](../concepts/access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md) fact left over — not merely of the access
side.

It would be denied by phenomenal realists, for whom influence and reportability at best correlate
with consciousness ([block-2007](../sources/block-2007.md)); by higher-order theorists, who require a
meta-representation rather than mere influence ([consciousness-is-higher-order-representation](consciousness-is-higher-order-representation.md));
and by anyone who holds that consciousness must have a sharp on/off boundary.

### In plain terms

Think of the brain as a crowd of processes all shouting at once. On this view, a thought is
"conscious" simply when it becomes famous in that crowd — when it hangs around long enough to shape
memory and behaviour and, above all, to be the thing you end up able to talk about. There is no
inner stage a thought steps onto; being widely influential, especially influential on speech, is
all being conscious ever was.
