This is the scenario premise of the [Chinese room](../concepts/chinese-room.md): purely formal
symbol-manipulation — matching input Chinese strings to output Chinese strings by a rulebook keyed
to shape, not meaning — can in principle reproduce the full conversational behaviour of a Chinese
speaker. The executor runs the program but need not, and by stipulation does not, understand the
symbols.

It is a possibility-or-setup claim about what *formal computation* can reproduce *behaviourally*.
It asserts nothing yet about understanding; it grants the program its behavioural success precisely
so that the next verdict — that there is [no understanding in
the room](chinese-room-no-understanding.md) — can isolate the question of *semantics*.

One could deny it by holding that no purely syntactic rulebook could actually sustain open-ended
Chinese conversation — a tractability or combinatorial worry, parallel to the lookup-table size
objections ([lookup-table-combinatorially-inadequate](lookup-table-combinatorially-inadequate.md)). On that reading the room shares
the coherence-of-scenario vulnerability that the
[thought-experiment](../../patterns/thought-experiment.md) pattern flags as its *coherence*
critical question (CQ-coherence).

Distinct from [lookup-table-possible](lookup-table-possible.md) because that system is flat storage with no
processing, whereas the room *runs a program* (mediating, rule-governed steps); it therefore
instantiates computation, not mere lookup, and so bears on computational functionalism rather than
on behaviourism alone.

### In plain terms

This just sets the stage: a person who knows no Chinese, following a thick enough rulebook, could
in principle hold up his end of a Chinese conversation so well that no one could tell. It says
nothing yet about whether he *understands* — that comes next. The only point here is that getting
all the right outputs by following rules is possible.
