To ask whether someone is morally responsible, on this view, is not to ask the retrospective
question of what she *deserves* for a deed already done, but the practical question of whether
responding to her — with blame or praise, punishment or reward — will do any good going forward.
Responsibility attaches to whoever can be *influenced*: the practice is a means of forming motives
and steering conduct, and it is justified, where it is justified at all, by that steering effect
rather than by any fact about desert (Schlick, [1939](../sources/schlick-1939.md)).

The claim inverts the ordinary order of explanation. Retributive and desert-based views hold that we
punish *because* the agent is responsible; the forward-looking claim holds that we call the agent
responsible *because* — and to the extent that — punishing (or praising) her works. It is the
defining commitment of [consequentialist-responsibility](../positions/consequentialist-responsibility.md), and it is what lets that view
sidestep the metaphysics of the [free-will](../questions/free-will.md) debate: whether or not anyone has genuinely
open alternatives, the question of whom to influence remains live and answerable.

### Who denies it

Retributivists and desert-theorists deny it outright: for them just deserts are the *ground* of the
practice, not a by-product, and punishing the innocent would remain unjust even where it deterred
effectively. A subtler denial comes from the *reactive-attitudes* tradition after P. F. Strawson
([1962](../sources/strawson-1962.md)), on which blame and gratitude are expressions of attitudes we cannot
and need not justify by their utility — they are constitutive of holding one another to be persons,
not instruments wielded for an external payoff.

### In plain terms

Two ways to answer "is she responsible?" One looks backward — *did she freely do it, so does she
deserve blame?* The other looks forward — *will blaming her make things go better from here?* This
claim takes the forward-looking side: calling someone responsible is really about whether responding
to them (telling them off, rewarding them, locking them up) will shape what they and others do next.
It's what lets a whole theory of blame and praise carry on even if it turns out nobody's choices were
ever truly "up to them."
