Hylomorphism is the view that the mind or soul is the *form* of a living body — its organising
actuality — and neither a separate substance housed in the body nor simply the body's matter
([soul-is-form-of-body](../claims/soul-is-form-of-body.md)). The word combines the Greek *hylē* (matter) and *morphē*
(form). On this picture a human being is one substance, the living animal, and mental capacities
are features of that animal at the level of its form: what the organism can do in virtue of being
organised as it is.

### Lineage

The doctrine originates with Aristotle, whose *De Anima* ([aristotle-de-anima](../sources/aristotle-de-anima.md)) defines
the soul as the form, or *first actuality*, of a natural body that has life potentially within it.
Thomas Aquinas ([aquinas-summa](../sources/aquinas-summa.md)) took up and developed the account, arguing that the
intellectual soul is the substantial form of the human body. It has been revived in recent
analytic philosophy of mind by writers such as William Jaworski ([jaworski-2016](../sources/jaworski-2016.md)) and
Anna Marmodoro ([marmodoro-2013](../sources/marmodoro-2013.md)), who treat structure or form as a basic ontological and
explanatory principle.

### Between dualism and materialism

Hylomorphism is positioned to thread between two opposed views. Against [dualism](dualism.md) it
denies that there is any separate mental substance: there is just the one living thing. Against
reductive materialism it denies that the organism is nothing but its matter, since the form is a
genuine principle and not an optional gloss on the material facts. A standard illustration is a
statue, whose shape is neither a second object sitting beside the bronze nor identical with the
bronze itself.

It answers the [hard problem of consciousness](../questions/hard-problem.md) by locating mind as the
form of a suitably organised living thing, rather than as an extra ingredient or a mere aggregate
of parts. Although "form as organisation" can sound like [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md), the
resemblance is only partial: the hylomorphist ties form to a *living* substance with intrinsic
ends, or *teleology*, not to a substrate-neutral causal role. For that reason the view does not
straightforwardly license [substrate-independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md), the thesis that the same mind could be
realised in any suitable material.

### In plain terms

An old idea (Aristotle) enjoying a revival: the mind isn't a ghost inside the body, nor is it just
the meat — it's the *form* of a living body, the way a statue's shape isn't a second thing beside
the bronze yet isn't the bronze either. You are one living creature, and "mind" names what that
creature can do in virtue of being organised the way it is. It resists both "soul in a machine"
dualism and "nothing but atoms" materialism.
