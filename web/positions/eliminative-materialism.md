Folk psychology — the everyday framework of beliefs, desires, and qualia — is an empirical theory that
a mature neuroscience will *replace* rather than vindicate, so strictly there are no such states
([folk-psychology-is-false-theory](../claims/folk-psychology-is-false-theory.md); Paul & Patricia Churchland, 1981). It answers
[hard-problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) by dissolving its explanandum — a **type-A** materialism in
[materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md) — and therefore `rejects` [phenomenal-residue](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md) and holds zombies
*inconceivable* on reflection, `rejecting` [zombie-conceivable](../claims/zombie-conceivable.md).

what distinguishes it from neighbouring views: it is broader and more radical than
[illusionism](illusionism.md), which targets *phenomenal* properties specifically and grants the
introspective appearance; eliminativism also discards the propositional attitudes (belief, desire). It
contrasts with the realist physicalisms ([type-identity](type-identity.md), type-B) that *keep* the states by
reducing them.

### In plain terms

The boldest physicalist line: our everyday talk of beliefs, desires, and "what it feels like" is a
folk *theory* — and, like "phlogiston" or "the four humours," a mistaken one that brain science will
eventually retire. So the puzzle of consciousness isn't solved so much as *cancelled*: there's no
inner feel to explain because, strictly speaking, there are no such things — only neural processes
we've been mislabelling.
