*Eliminative materialism* is the most radical physicalist view of the mind: it holds that our
everyday categories of the mental — beliefs, desires, and qualia — are not states science will
reduce and keep, but the posits of a *false theory* that a mature neuroscience will discard outright.
The everyday framework is called *folk psychology*: the tacit theory ordinary people use to explain
and predict behaviour in terms of what someone believes, wants, and feels. Eliminativism treats it
as an empirical theory on a par with past failures like phlogiston or the four humours, and, like
them, destined for replacement rather than vindication. So strictly speaking, on this view, there are
no such states at all ([folk-psychology-is-false-theory](../claims/folk-psychology-is-false-theory.md)). The position was developed by
Paul M. Churchland ([churchland-1981](../sources/churchland-1981.md)) and Patricia Churchland.

### How it answers the hard problem

It answers the [hard problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) not by solving it but by dissolving its
*explanandum* — the very thing to be explained. If there are no phenomenal states, there is no felt
residue whose physical basis needs accounting for. In Chalmers' taxonomy it is therefore a *type-A*
materialism ([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md)). Accordingly it rejects
[the claim that some phenomenal character is left over](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md) once function is
explained, and it holds that a [philosophical zombie](../claims/zombie-conceivable.md) is, on reflection,
not genuinely conceivable.

### What sets it apart

Eliminativism is broader and more radical than [illusionism](illusionism.md). Illusionism
targets *phenomenal* properties specifically and grants that introspection presents our states as
feeling some way (it denies only that the feel is real); eliminativism goes further, discarding the
*propositional attitudes* — belief and desire — as well. It also contrasts with the realist
physicalisms such as [type-identity theory](type-identity.md) and type-B materialism, which
*keep* the mental states by reducing or identifying them with physical ones rather than eliminating
them.

### In plain terms

The boldest physicalist line: our everyday talk of beliefs, desires, and "what it feels like" is a
folk *theory* — and, like "phlogiston" or "the four humours," a mistaken one that brain science will
eventually retire. So the puzzle of consciousness isn't solved so much as *cancelled*: there's no
inner feel to explain because, strictly speaking, there are no such things — only neural processes
we've been mislabelling.
