Representationalism (also called *intentionalism*) holds that the *phenomenal character* of an
experience — what it is like to undergo it — just is a kind of *representational content*. What it
is like to see red is the experience's representing the world a certain way; to introspect "the
feel" of the experience is to become aware of *what* it represents, not of any intrinsic *mental
paint* spread across the mind. The seeming redness you find when you look inward is really the red
your visual system attributes to the surface out there. This is the claim that
[phenomenal character is representational](../claims/phenomenal-character-is-representational.md)
([tye-2000](../sources/tye-2000.md), [dretske-1995](../sources/dretske-1995.md), [harman-1990](../sources/harman-1990.md)).

Paired with a naturalistic, *teleosemantic* theory of representation — one that grounds content in
the biological function of representing — the view answers [hard-problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) by reducing
qualia to content, a broadly physicalist option among the [type-A/type-B
materialisms](../concepts/materialism-types.md).

### What distinguishes it from neighbouring views

As a *first-order* view ([order-of-representation](../concepts/order-of-representation.md)), it differs from
[higher-order-theories](higher-order-theories.md): no higher-order state monitoring the experience is required for
it to be conscious. Its classic pressure points are *inverted-spectrum* cases (could two people
represent the same colours yet have systematically swapped experiences?) and *inverted-earth*
cases, together with the residue theorist's charge that [two experiences
could share content yet differ in feel](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md).

### In plain terms

This view says the "feel" of an experience is nothing extra beyond *what it tells you about the world*.
Seeing red is your visual system representing "red surface there"; the redness you seem to find in your
mind is really just the red it's attributing to the world. So feelings aren't inner paint — they're
the world as represented to you, and that's the kind of thing science already studies. The test cases
push on whether two people could represent the same things yet *feel* differently.
