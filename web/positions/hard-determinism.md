*Hard determinism* accepts the incompatibilist's conditional — if determinism is true, no one acts
freely — and affirms the antecedent: the world is deterministic, so free will is an illusion. It
agrees with [libertarianism](libertarianism.md) that freedom would require genuinely open
alternatives and real origination, and agrees with the scientific picture that neither exists;
deliberation feels open only because deliberators cannot know their own outcome in advance. The
felt openness of the future is epistemic, not metaphysical
(rejecting [alternatives-genuinely-open](../claims/alternatives-genuinely-open.md)).

The contemporary descendant is Pereboom's *hard incompatibilism* (Pereboom,
[2014](../sources/pereboom-2014.md)), which no longer leans on determinism being exactly true: whether
our actions are determined or merely the product of chance, neither prior necessitation nor
amplified randomness supplies the authorship freedom requires, so free will loses either way. The
position's distinctive burden is practical — saying what becomes of praise, blame, and punishment
once desert is gone — and its proponents typically answer with forward-looking justifications:
protection, deterrence, reform. Within the
[consciousness–free-will duality](../concepts/consciousness-freewill-duality.md), hard determinism is the
thesis that the action-coproduct is epistemic with one inhabited summand — the eliminativist pole
of the free-will debate, mirroring eliminativism about phenomenal consciousness.

### In plain terms

Hard determinists think free will is like the flat earth: a natural impression that turns out
false. Your choices are events in your brain, your brain follows physics, and physics was set in
motion long before you were born — the feeling that you "could have done otherwise" just reflects
your ignorance of what was coming. The serious question, they say, isn't whether free will exists
(it doesn't) but how to run responsibility, blame, and punishment honestly without it.
