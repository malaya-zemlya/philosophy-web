*Idealism* is the view that what is fundamental is mind or experience, and that the physical
world is constituted by — or derivative of — consciousness rather than the other way around.
Where the physicalist starts with matter and asks how minds arise from it, the idealist reverses
the arrow: matter is an appearance *within* mind, not a mind-free substance out of which minds
are somehow squeezed. The position commits to the claim that
[reality is fundamentally mental](../claims/reality-is-fundamentally-mental.md).

### Variants

Idealism is a family of views rather than a single doctrine. *Subjective idealism*, associated
with George Berkeley ([berkeley-1710](../sources/berkeley-1710.md)), holds that to be is to be perceived (*esse est
percipi*): physical objects exist only as ideas in perceiving minds, with God's perception
underwriting their continuity when no creature is looking. *Analytic idealism*, defended by
Bernardo Kastrup ([kastrup-2019](../sources/kastrup-2019.md)), posits one universal mind whose *dissociations* —
split-off pockets of mental life, on the model of dissociative identity disorder — are individual
subjects like us. *Conscious realism*, due to Donald Hoffman ([hoffman-2008](../sources/hoffman-2008.md)), treats
spacetime and physical objects as a species-specific perceptual *interface*: useful for survival
but no more the ultimate furniture of the world than the icons on a desktop are the circuitry
they stand for.

### How it answers the hard problem

Idealism dissolves the [hard problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) from the far side. There is no
puzzle of extracting mind from matter, because there was never any mind-free matter to begin
with; the physical is an appearance, and appearances are already mental. What looks like an
intractable explanatory gap on the physicalist's books simply does not arise on the idealist's.

### What distinguishes it

Idealism is the mirror image of physicalism: the two agree that reality has a single fundamental
kind and disagree only on its direction of grounding. It thus differs from [dualism](dualism.md), which posits two fundamental kinds, and from [neutral
monism](neutral-monism.md), whose single base is neither mental nor physical. Its characteristic burden is the
inverse of the physicalist's — a *decomposition* or *structure* problem of deriving a
law-governed, intersubjectively shared physical world out of mind: explaining why the
"appearance" is so stable, so lawful, and the same for everyone.

### In plain terms

Idealism turns the usual picture upside down: instead of matter being basic and minds somehow
arising from it, *mind* is basic and the physical world is something that shows up *within*
experience — more like a shared dream with strict rules than a lump of stuff outside us. The
appeal: the hard problem ("how does dead matter produce feeling?") never arises, because there
was never any mind-free matter to start from. The bill it must pay: explaining why this "dream"
is so stable, lawful, and the same for everyone.
