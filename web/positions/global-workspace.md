Global workspace theory holds that a mental content becomes *conscious* when it is broadcast in a
"global workspace" — made widely available to the brain's many specialist systems for memory,
language, report, and flexible control. The metaphor is a theatre: countless unconscious processes
work in the dark, and a piece of information becomes conscious when it is lit on the central stage
and shared with the whole audience at once ([global
broadcast](../claims/consciousness-is-global-broadcast.md)). It is the leading *functional* and empirical theory of consciousness, framed as a
research programme rather than a piece of metaphysics. The framework was introduced by Bernard Baars
([baars-1988](../sources/baars-1988.md)); Stanislas Dehaene and colleagues added a neural mechanism — the *global
neuronal workspace*, in which a stimulus crosses into consciousness through a late, all-or-none
"ignition" of long-range fronto-parietal networks ([dehaene-naccache-2001](../sources/dehaene-naccache-2001.md)).

### What it explains, and what it may not

The theory is most naturally read as an account of the *access* side of the
[access/phenomenal distinction](../concepts/access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md) — what makes a content
reportable, rememberable, and usable in reasoning and action. Whether the same broadcast also
constitutes the *phenomenal* side, the felt quality of experience, is the live question. Offered as
an answer to the [hard problem](../questions/hard-problem.md), it accounts elegantly for how information
reaches the spotlight and gets used, but critics — Ned Block prominently — object that *phenomenal*
consciousness may *overflow* access ([block-2007](../sources/block-2007.md)): we may experience more than ever gets
broadcast and reported, in which case global availability is at best the wrong target for explaining
felt experience.

### How it differs from neighbouring views

Against [higher-order-theories](higher-order-theories.md), the global workspace needs no dedicated
*meta-representation* — no separate state whose job is to represent another mental state. Wide
availability is enough; a content is conscious in virtue of being broadcast, not in virtue of being
monitored by a higher-order thought.

### In plain terms

Picture the mind as a theatre: countless specialist processes work in the dark, and a piece of
information becomes *conscious* when it's lit up on the central stage and broadcast to the whole
audience — so you can talk about it, remember it, and act on it. It's the leading scientific theory
of consciousness and comes with brain evidence. The philosophical catch: this explains how
something gets into the spotlight and gets *used*, but maybe not why being in the spotlight *feels*
like anything.
