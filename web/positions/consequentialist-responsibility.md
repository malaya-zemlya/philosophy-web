*Consequentialism about moral responsibility* holds that our responsibility practices — blaming,
praising, punishing, rewarding, resenting, shunning — are **tools** for shaping conduct, and that
attributions of responsibility are justified by their effects on future behaviour rather than by any
backward-looking fact about desert ([responsibility-is-forward-looking](../claims/responsibility-is-forward-looking.md)). The question *is
she responsible?* is reinterpreted as *is she the kind of agent whom blame and reward can reach?* On
its classic statement the point of punishment is not retribution but education — "the formation of
motives" (Schlick, [1939](../sources/schlick-1939.md)) — and praise and blame are, correspondingly,
dispositions to encourage and discourage.

What most distinguishes the view is its **independence from the metaphysics** of the
[free-will](../questions/free-will.md) debate. Because it grounds the practices in their forward effects rather than
in origination or open alternatives, it can be paired with almost any answer about free will: a
[hard determinist](hard-determinism.md) who thinks no one could ever have done otherwise still
has a use for deterrence and reform, and a [compatibilist](compatibilism.md) can adopt it as
the account of *why* reasons-responsive agents are the proper targets of the reactive practices. Its
natural opponents are **retributivism** and desert-based theories, for which just deserts are the
ground of the practice and not a by-product, and the **reactive-attitudes** tradition after P. F.
Strawson ([1962](../sources/strawson-1962.md)), for which blame and gratitude are constitutive of
interpersonal life rather than instruments justified by their payoff.

A pointed challenge — and the reason this view now connects to the leeway debate — is that the tool
may need the very openness the view hoped to bypass: incentives can redirect an agent only if more
than one course was genuinely available to her ([incentives-require-alternatives](../claims/incentives-require-alternatives.md)). If that
is right, the forward-looking justification does not escape the metaphysics after all, and it sits
least comfortably in the mouth of the hard determinist, who has denied the alternatives the incentive
needs.

### In plain terms

This is the view that holding people responsible is basically a *steering device*. We blame, praise,
punish, and reward not to settle a cosmic scorecard about what someone deserves for what they already
did, but to shape what people do next — yours and everyone watching. Its big selling point is that it
doesn't need to first solve the hard puzzle of whether anyone *really* has free will: whatever the
answer, you can still ask who's worth nudging. Critics push back from two sides — some say it misses
that blame is about *desert*, others that resentment and gratitude are just part of caring about each
other, not tools we picked for their usefulness. And there's a sting in the tail: nudging only works
on someone who could actually go more than one way ([incentives-require-alternatives](../claims/incentives-require-alternatives.md)), so
the steering picture may quietly need the free choice it tried to do without.
