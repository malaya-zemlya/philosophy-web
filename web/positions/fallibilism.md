*Fallibilism*, in its thoroughgoing Popperian form (*critical rationalism*), holds that no belief is ever
conclusively justified, that there are no certain foundations, and that this is no defect to be remedied but
the normal condition of knowledge. Knowledge grows not by securing premises but by *conjecture and
criticism*: we propose bold guesses and try hard to refute them, keeping — always provisionally — those that
survive ([all-knowledge-is-conjectural](../claims/all-knowledge-is-conjectural.md)). Its stance toward
[the Münchhausen trilemma](../concepts/munchhausen-trilemma.md) is distinctive: it does not pick a horn but
*rejects the demand* that generates the trilemma — the justificationist insistence that beliefs be grounded
all the way down. The right question is not "how is this guaranteed?" but "how might it be wrong, and have
we tried to find out?" David Deutsch ([deutsch-2011](../sources/deutsch-2011.md)) develops the view with the criterion that
good explanations are [hard to vary](../claims/good-explanation-is-hard-to-vary.md).

### How it differs from neighbouring views

Where [foundationalism](foundationalism.md), [coherentism](coherentism.md), and [infinitism](infinitism.md) each accept the
demand for justification and answer it differently, fallibilism declines the demand — so it rejects
[basic beliefs](../claims/some-beliefs-are-basic.md) not by offering a rival structure of justification but by
denying that knowledge needs a justifying structure at all. It treats the external-world problem deflation­
arily: the unprovability of [external-world-exists](../claims/external-world-exists.md) is no scandal, since *nothing* is provable in
the foundationalist's sense, and realism is simply the best, hardest-to-vary explanation we have
([deutsch-anti-solipsism](../arguments/deutsch-anti-solipsism.md)). It shares the anti-foundational spirit of
[hinge-epistemology](hinge-epistemology.md) but reaches it through fallibilist methodology rather than through a theory
of framework certainties.

### In plain terms

Fallibilism says: stop looking for certainty — there isn't any, and you don't need it. We don't *prove*
things and build upward; we guess, attack the guesses, and keep whatever survives the beating, knowing it
might still be wrong. So the whole "justify it all the way to bedrock" project was the mistake, not its
failure. And the fact that you can't *prove* there's an external world stops being embarrassing: you can't
conclusively prove anything, and realism is just the best explanation going.
