*Anomalous monism* is Donald Davidson's reconciliation of three theses that look jointly
inconsistent ([1970](../sources/davidson-1970.md)): mental events cause physical events
([mental-events-have-physical-effects](../claims/mental-events-have-physical-effects.md)); causation requires a strict law covering cause and
effect; and there are no strict psychophysical laws ([no-strict-psychophysical-laws](../claims/no-strict-psychophysical-laws.md)). The
reconciliation is an argument *for* monism: if a decision causes an arm to rise, some strict law
covers that causal transaction; strict laws are couched only in physical vocabulary; so the
decision, to fall under the law, must itself *be* a physical event
([mental-is-physical](../claims/mental-is-physical.md)). The result is a token physicalism without reduction — "monism"
because every mental event is a physical event, "anomalous" because mental *kinds* answer to norms
of rationality and never line up law-like with physical kinds, so the type-identity of
[classical identity theory](type-identity.md) ([mental-states-are-brain-state-types](../claims/mental-states-are-brain-state-types.md))
is rejected.

### How it answers the mental-causation question

The [problem of mental causation](../questions/mental-causation.md) asks how a mental state gets a grip
on the physical world. Anomalous monism's answer: straightforwardly, because the mental cause *is*
a physical event, and causation is an extensional relation between events however described. Your
decision moves your hand the way any physical event causes another; "mental" and "physical" are two
vocabularies for describing the one causal order, and only the physical vocabulary carries strict
laws.

### The signature objection

Critics — pressed sharply in the literature around Kim's
[exclusion argument](../arguments/causal-exclusion.md) — reply that this secures causation for mental
*tokens* but not for mental *properties*: if the event causes what it does in virtue of its
physical properties (those figuring in the strict law), its mental properties look idle, and
anomalous monism threatens to collapse into property
[epiphenomenalism](epiphenomenalism.md) — the very inertness
([mental-states-causally-inert](../claims/mental-states-causally-inert.md)) it set out to avoid. Davidson's rejoinder is that
causation relates events, not events-qua-property, so the "in virtue of" question is ill-posed;
whether that rejoinder is available without an account of causal relevance remains the view's
contested point.

### In plain terms

The view says: every particular thought or decision is, in fact, some physical event in your brain
— so of course it can move your body; physical events do that all the time. But there's no
dictionary that translates psychology into physics: no exceptionless law of the form "this type of
thought = that type of brain state." Mind and brain are one reality under two descriptions, one of
which (physics) has strict laws, the other of which (psychology) never will. The standing worry:
if the brain event does its pushing thanks to its *physical* features, does its being a *thought*
do any work at all — or is the mental side just along for the ride, as
[epiphenomenalism](epiphenomenalism.md) says outright?
