*Illusionism* is the view that phenomenal consciousness, as standardly conceived, does not exist:
there is no intrinsic, ineffable, private "felt quality" to experience. What is real is
*introspective machinery* that *misrepresents* our inner states as having such qualities. We are not
deceived about having experiences; we are deceived about their nature
([phenomenality-is-illusion](../claims/phenomenality-is-illusion.md)).

On this view the [hard problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) — why physical processing is accompanied by
felt experience at all — is not solved but dissolved, and replaced by what is called the *illusion
problem*: the task of explaining why physical processing should *represent itself* as phenomenal, why
the impression is so compelling. The term "illusionism" was coined by Keith Frankish
([frankish-2016](../sources/frankish-2016.md)); its most prominent earlier advocate is Daniel Dennett, whose case
against qualia as traditionally conceived ([dennett-1988](../sources/dennett-1988.md)) is its direct ancestor. It is a
thoroughly physicalist, functionalist-friendly view, and so it denies
[that any phenomenal character is left over](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md) once functional role is
accounted for.

### What sets it apart

Illusionism is the common opponent of the *realist* views — those that take phenomenal character to
be real. It stands opposite [dualism](dualism.md), [panpsychism](panpsychism.md), and
[Russellian monism](russellian-monism.md), all of which are realist about felt experience. It
sits closest to strong, role-based [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md), and it rests comfortably
with [substrate independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md), since a function-level story carries
over to any substrate that implements it. It should be distinguished from
[mysterianism](mysterianism.md), which holds that the explanatory gap is real but permanently
beyond human understanding: illusionism holds instead that there is no gap to solve, because there is
nothing of the disputed kind to explain.

### In plain terms

Illusionism's bold move: the "inner feel" of experience — the redness of red, the ache of pain as
something *intrinsic* — isn't real; it only seems real because our brains are wired to report on
themselves that way. So there's no extra mystery about how matter produces feeling, because there's
no feeling-stuff to produce — only the very convincing *impression* of it, which a complete brain
science can explain. The new question becomes: why is the illusion so powerful?
