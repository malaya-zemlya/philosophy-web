*Proteocentrism* is the view that only protein-based biological life can be a bearer of
consciousness, and so the only kind of thing that can have moral status. On this view the carbon
substrate of living brains is not an incidental detail of how minds happen to be implemented on
Earth but a genuine requirement: no system built from silicon and metal, however sophisticated
its behaviour, could literally feel anything or merit moral consideration. The name comes from
*proteo-*, protein.

### What it commits to

Proteocentrism is defined by its rejection of [substrate
independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md) — the thesis that mental states depend only on a system's functional
organization, not on the material that realizes it. Where the substrate-independence thesis
holds that a mind is a matter of *pattern* and could in principle run on any medium that
preserves the pattern, proteocentrism holds that the medium itself is part of what makes a mind
a mind. Applied to the organizing question of [the moral status of
AI](../questions/llm-moral-status.md), it returns a flat negative: no non-biological system qualifies.

### Where it sits

The view is the natural opposite of substrate independence, and stronger than the bare denial of
it: one could deny that *any* arrangement of matter suffices for mind while still allowing some
non-biological substrates, whereas proteocentrism singles out biology specifically. It is stated
here as a stand-alone position so that characters in the debate can cite it rather than restate
it. Reasons *for* it are not part of the position itself; they live in separate argument nodes
that support its commitments.

### In plain terms

The view that only living, biological things — stuff built from carbon-based proteins, like
brains — could ever be conscious or matter morally. On this view the "meat" isn't an incidental
detail of how *our* minds happen to be built; it's a genuine requirement. So no computer, however
cleverly it behaves, could really feel anything or deserve moral consideration. It's the natural
opposite of the idea that a mind is about the *pattern* a system runs, not the material it's made
of. (The name comes from *proteo-*, protein.)
