Dualism, in the philosophy of mind, is the view that mind and matter are two fundamentally
different kinds of thing, so that even a complete physical account of the world leaves something
out. What it leaves out is phenomenal consciousness, the felt first-person character of
experience: on the dualist's view the complete physical and functional facts about a brain do not
fix the facts about what that brain feels, or whether it feels anything at all
([consciousness-fundamental-nonphysical](../claims/consciousness-fundamental-nonphysical.md)). Consciousness is thus a fundamental,
non-physical feature of reality rather than something built out of, or guaranteed by, physical
arrangement.

### Two forms

Dualism comes in two main grades. *Substance dualism*, the historical position associated with
Descartes ([descartes-meditations](../sources/descartes-meditations.md)), holds that the mind is a distinct, non-physical
substance: a thinking thing that could in principle exist apart from any body. *Property dualism*,
also called *naturalistic dualism* in David Chalmers's development of it ([chalmers-1996](../sources/chalmers-1996.md)),
is more modest about the furniture of the world. It posits only one kind of substance but two kinds
of property, with phenomenal properties counting as fundamental and irreducible. On this version the
felt properties are tied to physical ones by contingent *psychophysical laws*, bridging principles
that could have been otherwise, and the conceivability of zombies (physical duplicates of us that
lack any inner experience) is taken to show that the physical facts do not necessitate the
phenomenal ones, and so that physicalism is false.

Either way, dualism answers the [hard problem of consciousness](../questions/hard-problem.md) not by
closing the explanatory gap but by accepting it: the gap is genuine and ontological, a real seam
in nature rather than a temporary gap in our knowledge.

### What sets it apart

Dualism is best located by its neighbours. Unlike physicalism and functionalism, it denies that
consciousness *supervenes* on the physical — denies, that is, that fixing every physical fact
thereby fixes every fact about experience. Unlike [panpsychism](panpsychism.md) and
[russellian-monism](russellian-monism.md), which make consciousness the intrinsic nature *of* the physical,
dualism makes it genuinely non-physical, something additional to the physical order. And unlike
[proteocentrism](proteocentrism.md), whose claim is about a particular (carbon) substrate, dualism's claim
is about non-physicality as such, not about any chemistry.

The standard pressure point is causal: if the mind is non-physical, how can it make a difference
to the body? This is the *epiphenomenalism* worry ([epiphenomenalism](epiphenomenalism.md)) — that a
non-physical mind seems either to violate the causal closure of the physical or to do no causal
work at all. It is largely this worry that pushes property dualists like Chalmers toward
[russellian-monism](russellian-monism.md), on which consciousness is relocated inside the physical as its
intrinsic nature.

### In plain terms

Dualism says mind and matter are two different kinds of thing: even a complete physics-and-wiring
story about your brain would leave out the felt, conscious part, because that part isn't physical
at all. The old version (Descartes) makes the mind a separate *substance*; the modern version
makes *feelings* extra fundamental ingredients that the laws of nature attach to certain brains.
Its famous headache: if the mind isn't physical, how does it manage to push the body around?
