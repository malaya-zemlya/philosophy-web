The *intentional stance* is Daniel Dennett's interpretivist theory of mental content
([1987](../sources/dennett-1987.md)). To adopt the stance toward a system is to predict its behaviour by
treating it as a rational agent: attribute the beliefs and desires it *ought* to have given its
place in the world, and expect it to act accordingly. Dennett's thesis is that being a genuine
believer just *is* being a system whose behaviour is reliably and voluminously predictable from
this stance — there is no further test to pass and no inner mark of "real" meaning to possess
([intentionality-is-stance-relative](../claims/intentionality-is-stance-relative.md)). The view answers the
[problem of intentionality](../questions/intentionality.md) by deflation: the puzzle of how matter gets
unborrowed aboutness dissolves once original intentionality is recognized as a theorist's myth.

### What distinguishes it

Where [teleosemantics](teleosemantics.md) naturalizes content by locating it in selection
history, interpretivism relocates it in the eye of the (idealized) beholder — though not
arbitrarily: the stance works for a thermostat only feebly, for a chess program usefully, and for a
person indispensably, and those differences are objective, "real patterns" in behaviour. Where
Searle insists that minds have intrinsic semantics ([minds-have-semantics](../claims/minds-have-semantics.md);
[1992](../sources/searle-1992.md)) and that a program's states are about nothing until we read them, the
intentional stance denies the contrast has a basis: our own intentionality is evolution's
"derived" product ([intrinsic-derived-intentionality](../concepts/intrinsic-derived-intentionality.md)). The standard objection is that
interpretation presupposes what it explains — an interpreter is already an intentional system, so
stance-relative content either regresses or quietly helps itself to unexplained original meaning.

### In plain terms

How do you know your neighbour *wants* coffee? You can't see wants. What you do is treat her as a
sensible agent — "given what she knows and needs, she'll walk toward the café" — and the
predictions work. This view's bold move is to say there is nothing more to having beliefs and
desires than being the kind of system those predictions work on. Minds aren't containers with
meaning sealed inside; "believer" is more like "valuable" than like "made of carbon" — a status
that shows up from the right point of view, backed by real patterns. The nagging worry: the story
explains meaning in terms of interpreters — but an interpreter is exactly the kind of meaning-laden
thing the story promised to explain.
