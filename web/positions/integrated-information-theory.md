Integrated information theory (IIT) holds that consciousness *is* integrated information: a system
is conscious to the degree that it forms an irreducible whole with intrinsic *cause-effect power*
over itself, and the specific *character* of an experience is the geometry of that cause-effect
structure. The quantity of consciousness is measured by $\Phi$ ("phi"), which captures how much a
system's whole constrains its own past and future states beyond what its parts do independently; a
system that decomposes into parts without loss has $\Phi = 0$ and is not conscious. The theory was
introduced by Giulio Tononi ([tononi-2004](../sources/tononi-2004.md)) and developed with Christof Koch and others
([tononi-2016](../sources/tononi-2016.md)). Its core commitment is the claim that
[consciousness is integrated information](../claims/consciousness-is-integrated-information.md).

### From axioms to substrate

Unusually, IIT is built "from the inside out." It starts from *phenomenological axioms* — features
taken to be self-evident of any experience, such as *intrinsic existence* (an experience exists for
itself), *integration* (it is unified, not a sum of separate parts), and *exclusion* (it has
definite content and definite borders) — and asks what a physical substrate must be like to satisfy
them. It then identifies consciousness with that cause-effect structure directly. This makes IIT an
*identity* theory rather than a functional reduction: it answers the [hard
problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) by saying what consciousness *is*, not by explaining what it does.

### Why substrate matters

Because $\Phi$ is read off a system's *actual* cause-effect architecture rather than its
input-output behaviour or the computation it performs, two behaviourally identical systems can
differ sharply in consciousness. A striking corollary: a purely feed-forward *digital simulation*
of a brain — however faithful its behaviour — can have $\Phi \approx 0$, because its one-way,
modular wiring lacks the dense reciprocal integration $\Phi$ measures. IIT therefore
[rejects substrate independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md) and answers
[substrate-independence](../questions/substrate-independence.md) in the negative for digital computers. It bears directly against
the idea that a language model on standard hardware has genuine mental states
([llm-has-mental-states](../claims/llm-has-mental-states.md)): by IIT's measure such a machine would be barely conscious,
whatever it can say.

### How it differs from neighbouring views

This sets IIT sharply apart from [global-workspace](global-workspace.md) and from functionalism generally,
which locate consciousness in what a system makes available or computes rather than in its intrinsic
wiring. The theory's most discussed embarrassment runs the other way: because $\Phi$ tracks
integration rather than behaviour, simple and even largely inactive grids of components can score
non-zero $\Phi$, giving IIT *panpsychist* consequences that many critics find a reductio.

### In plain terms

IIT flips the usual question: instead of asking what the brain *does*, it asks how tightly a
system's parts make a difference to each other — its "integrated information," Φ. The more a system
is an irreducible whole shaping itself, the more conscious it is, and the *pattern* of those
interactions *is* the specific experience. Striking upshot for AI: a digital computer could
perfectly imitate a person yet, because of how its chips are wired (one-way, modular), have almost
no Φ — so on this theory it would feel almost nothing. Substrate (the actual cause-effect wiring)
matters, not just the behaviour or the program.
