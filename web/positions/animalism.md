*Animalism* is the view that each of us is, strictly and literally, a human animal — a biological
organism of the species *Homo sapiens* — so that our persistence conditions are the organism's:
you last as long as your biological life does, whatever becomes of your memories or character
([identity-is-biological-continuity](../claims/identity-is-biological-continuity.md); Olson, [1997](../sources/olson-1997.md)). It answers the
[question of personal identity](../questions/personal-identity.md) by changing the subject of the verb
*persist*: the thing whose survival is at issue was never a Lockean person-of-psychology but the
animal that sometimes has a psychology.

### What distinguishes it

Animalism's engine is the **thinking-animal argument** ([too-many-thinkers](../arguments/too-many-thinkers.md)): a human
animal is sitting in your chair; that animal, having your brain, thinks your thoughts; if you were
something other than it, there would be two thinkers of every thought — so you are the animal. Its
chief cost is the transplant case: since a detached brain is not an organism, animalism must say
that in the Brown/Brownson scenario you would stay behind with the living body, not go with your
mental life — flatly against the intuition that powers the
[psychological continuity theory](psychological-continuity-theory.md)
([transplant-intuition](../arguments/transplant-intuition.md)). Animalists reply that the intuition tracks what we *care
about* (the psychology, which does travel) rather than what we *are*; the distinction between
identity and what matters, drawn for other reasons by Parfit ([1984](../sources/parfit-1984.md);
[identity-not-what-matters](../claims/identity-not-what-matters.md)), here works in the animalist's favour. On uploading, the
verdict is blunt: an upload of your psychology, however faithful, is not you — no organism, no
survival.

### In plain terms

What are you? Not, says this view, a bundle of memories riding around in a body — you simply *are*
the living body, the animal. It was you before it could think (as an embryo), and it would still be
you if illness ended its thinking. The strongest card: right now an animal is sitting in your
chair, thinking your thoughts — if that thinker isn't you, then two things are reading this
sentence. The awkward card: if your brain were moved to another body, taking your whole mental life
with it, this view says you'd be the mindless body left behind — and that what you care about
(memories, personality) would have gone off with someone else.
