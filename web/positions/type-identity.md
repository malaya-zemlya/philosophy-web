*Type-identity theory* is the classical "mind is the brain" view: each kind of mental state is
literally identical to a kind of brain state, in the way that water turned out to be identical to
H₂O. To be in pain just is to be in some specific neural state — the textbook slogan is that "pain
is C-fibre firing." On this view the identity is not a definition we stipulate but a discovery
science makes: an *a posteriori* identity, established by empirical inquiry rather than by analysing
what the words mean. It was the founding physicalism of mid-century philosophy of mind, set out by
U. T. Place ([place-1956](../sources/place-1956.md)), J. J. C. Smart ([smart-1959](../sources/smart-1959.md)), and Herbert Feigl
([feigl-1958](../sources/feigl-1958.md)). Its core commitment is the claim that
[mental-state types are brain-state types](../claims/mental-states-are-brain-state-types.md).

### How it answers the hard problem

Type-identity dissolves the [hard problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) of consciousness by *identity*:
if a mental state simply is a brain state, there is no gap between the two left to bridge, and so
nothing further to explain once the relevant identities are in hand. It answers the
[substrate-independence question](../questions/substrate-independence.md) in the negative: because a mental
type just *is* a specific physical type, it is not freely realisable in arbitrary materials, so the
theory denies [substrate independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md).

As an a-posteriori-identity physicalism it is the historical seed of what David Chalmers
([chalmers-2003](../sources/chalmers-2003.md)) calls *type-B* materialism ([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md)). It can grant
that a zombie — a physical duplicate of a person with no inner experience — is *conceivable*, while
denying that conceivability is a reliable guide to possibility; so it rejects
[the inference from conceivability to possibility](../claims/conceivability-entails-possibility.md).

### What sets it apart

Unlike [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md), which identifies a mental state with a
realiser-neutral causal *role*, type-identity ties the mind to a physical *type*. That is exactly
the point Hilary Putnam's *multiple-realizability* objection presses ([putnam-1967](../sources/putnam-1967.md)): if
the same mental state can be realised in silicon, in an octopus, or in an alien nervous system, then
it cannot be identical to any one physical type. Historically this argument pushed the field from
identity theory toward functionalism. Unlike [dualism](dualism.md) and
[illusionism](illusionism.md), type-identity is reductive but realist: the mental states
genuinely exist, and they are physical.

### In plain terms

The original "mind is the brain" theory: each kind of mental state simply *is* a kind of brain
state, the way water turned out to *be* H₂O — a discovery, not a definition. So there's no leftover
mystery (pain just is that brain process), but there's a price: if pain *is* a specific brain state,
then something with a very different make-up (silicon, an alien) couldn't have *that* state — which
is the famous objection that gave rise to functionalism.
