Mental-state *types* are identical to brain-state types ([mental-states-are-brain-state-types](../claims/mental-states-are-brain-state-types.md)),
discovered a posteriori as "water = H₂O" was (Place 1956, Smart 1959, Feigl). It answers
[hard-problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) by *identity* — there is nothing to bridge once the identities are in hand —
and answers [substrate-independence](../questions/substrate-independence.md) in the negative: because a mental type just is a
specific *physical* type, it is not freely multiply realisable, so it `rejects`
[substrate-independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md). As an a-posteriori-identity physicalism it is the seed of Chalmers'
**type-B** ([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md)); accordingly it grants the conceivability of a zombie but
`rejects` [conceivability-entails-possibility](../claims/conceivability-entails-possibility.md) (conceivability is no guide to possibility here).

what distinguishes it from neighbouring views: unlike [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md) it identifies mind
with a physical *type*, not a realiser-neutral *role* — which is exactly why Putnam's
multiple-realizability objection pushed the field from identity theory to functionalism. Unlike
[dualism](dualism.md) and [illusionism](illusionism.md) it is reductive-but-realist: the states exist and are
physical.

### In plain terms

The original "mind is the brain" theory: each kind of mental state simply *is* a kind of brain state,
the way water turned out to *be* H₂O — a discovery, not a definition. So there's no leftover mystery
(pain just is that brain process), but there's a price: if pain *is* a specific brain state, then
something with a very different make-up (silicon, an alien) couldn't have *that* state — which is the
famous objection that gave rise to functionalism.
