**Type-B materialism** is the moderate, and today the most populous, wing of physicalism. Unlike
[type-a-materialism](type-a-materialism.md), it *grants* the epistemic gap: it concedes that a
[philosophical-zombie](../concepts/philosophical-zombie.md) is genuinely conceivable, that Mary ([knowledge-argument](../concepts/knowledge-argument.md)) is
genuinely surprised when she first sees red, and that no amount of physical information makes the felt
character of experience deducible *a priori*. What it denies is that this epistemic gap reflects an
**ontological** one. Physical and phenomenal truths are connected, on this view, by identities that are
*necessary but knowable only a posteriori* — on the model of "water is H₂O" or "heat is molecular
motion," truths that could not have been false yet had to be discovered rather than reasoned out. The
mental still *is* the physical ([mental-is-physical](../claims/mental-is-physical.md)); the appearance of a further fact is a
feature of our concepts, not of the world. The decisive move is therefore to reject the bridge from
conceivability to possibility ([conceivability-entails-possibility](../claims/conceivability-entails-possibility.md)): the zombie is conceivable
yet metaphysically impossible, so its conceivability proves nothing about what exists. It is **type-B** in
Chalmers' taxonomy ([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md); [chalmers-2003](../sources/chalmers-2003.md)).

**What distinguishes it from neighbouring views.** Type-B occupies the middle ground and is defined by
both its borders. Against [type-a-materialism](type-a-materialism.md) it insists the gap is real and must be
*explained* rather than denied — typically by the phenomenal-concept strategy, which argues that our
special first-person concepts of experience are cognitively isolated in a way that generates the illusion
of a further fact. Against the anti-physicalist ([dualism](dualism.md)) it insists the gap is *merely*
epistemic and carries no modal force. [type-identity](type-identity.md) is the classic species of type-B: it
identifies mental types with brain-state types as an a-posteriori discovery, exactly the
necessary-but-empirical identity type-B trades on. The standing worry is that the a-posteriori identities
type-B needs look unlike ordinary scientific identities — "water is H₂O" can itself be explained
structurally, whereas "pain is C-fibre firing" seems to leave the very thing in question untouched.

### In plain terms

The "yes it feels like a mystery, but it isn't really one" camp — and the most common view among
physicalist philosophers today. It agrees with the critics on the *feeling*: you really can imagine a
zombie, and Mary really is in for a surprise when she first sees colour. But it says that surprise is
about how our minds grasp the facts, not about there being two kinds of stuff. Compare "water is H₂O":
that turned out to be a discovery, something you couldn't have worked out from the armchair, even though
water could not have been anything other than H₂O. Type-B says consciousness is like that — experience
genuinely *is* some brain process, even though it never *feels* deducible from the physics. So being able
to imagine a zombie tells you about the limits of imagination, not about what could really exist. The
sharper, gap-denying alternative is [type-a-materialism](type-a-materialism.md).
