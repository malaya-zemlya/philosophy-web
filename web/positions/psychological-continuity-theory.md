The *psychological continuity theory* — neo-Lockeanism — is the dominant modern family of answers
to the [question of personal identity](../questions/personal-identity.md): persons persist through time
in virtue of psychological relations, not in virtue of the matter that realizes them
([identity-is-psychological-continuity](../claims/identity-is-psychological-continuity.md)). The lineage runs from Locke's continuity of
consciousness ([1689](../sources/locke-1689.md)) through Shoemaker's transplant-driven refinements
([1963](../sources/shoemaker-1963.md)) to Parfit's canonical formulation in terms of overlapping chains
of connectedness with an appropriate cause ([1984](../sources/parfit-1984.md)).

### What distinguishes it

The view makes identity travel with the *contents of the mind*: in the Brown/Brownson
brain-transplant case, virtually everyone's verdict is that the person goes with the brain because
the psychology goes with the brain ([transplant-intuition](../arguments/transplant-intuition.md)) — the theory takes that
verdict at face value and generalizes it. Against [animalism](animalism.md), it denies that
our persistence conditions are those of the human organism; against
[further-fact views](../claims/identity-is-further-fact.md), it insists identity is *constituted* by
analyzable relations rather than being primitive. Its best-known internal strain is that
psychological continuity can branch — one psychology, two continuers — while identity cannot
([fission](../arguments/fission.md)); the standard repairs (a "no-branching" clause, or Parfit's conclusion that
[identity is not what matters](../claims/identity-not-what-matters.md)) are themselves major positions in
the debate. The theory's stance on uploading and gradual silicon replacement follows its cause
clause: versions demanding ordinary neural continuity say no; versions accepting any reliable cause
say yes.

### In plain terms

This is the "you are your mind" theory of survival. What makes the grown-up you the same person as
the child in the photo isn't the atoms (nearly all replaced) but the mental thread: chained
memories, a continuing character, plans carried through. Most people already think this way —
that's why brain-swap stories in fiction feel instantly intelligible: wherever the memories and
personality go, that's where *you* went. The theory's famous headache is the copying machine: if
your mental thread could be continued by *two* successors at once, they can't both be you — so
either the theory adds a "no-doubles" fine print, or it concludes, with Parfit, that what you care
about in surviving was never strict identity at all
([identity-not-what-matters](../claims/identity-not-what-matters.md)).
