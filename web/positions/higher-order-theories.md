Higher-order theories of consciousness hold that a mental state becomes *conscious* not through
any feature it has on its own, but by being the object of a second state that represents it. In
itself a pain, a perception, or a thought is merely a *first-order* state, directed at the body or
the world; it becomes a conscious pain or conscious perception only when a further, *higher-order*
state takes it as its object — when the mind, in effect, represents itself as being in that state.
The first-/higher-order contrast the view turns on is set out in [order-of-representation](../concepts/order-of-representation.md).

Being conscious is therefore a relational, representational fact about a state, not an intrinsic
glow the state carries by itself. The family divides over what the higher-order state is. On the
*higher-order thought* (HOT) version it is an occurrent thought to the effect that one is in the
lower state ([rosenthal-2005](../sources/rosenthal-2005.md)). On the *higher-order perception* (HOP), or *inner-sense*,
version it is a quasi-perceptual monitoring of one's own mind ([lycan-1996](../sources/lycan-1996.md)). On a
*dispositional* variant the higher-order content need not be actually entertained; it is enough
that the first-order state be poised to produce it ([carruthers-2000](../sources/carruthers-2000.md)). All three are
gathered in the claim that [consciousness is
higher-order representation](../claims/consciousness-is-higher-order-representation.md).

### What it answers

The theory addresses [hard-problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) by reducing *state-consciousness* — what makes a
given mental state a conscious one — to higher-order representation. A pain you are in but in no way
aware of is not yet a conscious pain; it becomes one when a higher-order state registers it.

### What distinguishes it from neighbouring views

Against first-order [representationalism](representationalism.md), it insists that a state
must be *represented* — not merely itself represent the world — in order to be conscious. Against
[global workspace theory](global-workspace.md), it requires a dedicated meta-state, not just
the broadcast of a state's content to many subsystems.

### The standard objection

Critics — among them the residue theorist who holds that [phenomenal
states are not exhausted by role](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md) — object that the theory explains *access*, being aware *of* a
state, while leaving its felt character untouched: precisely the divide marked by
[the access/phenomenal split](../concepts/access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md). Mismatched or empty
higher-order representations (representing oneself as in a state one is not actually in) raise a
further difficulty.

### In plain terms

The idea: a mental state becomes *conscious* when your mind represents *itself* as being in that state
— consciousness is a kind of inner spotlight, a thought about a thought. A pain you're in but in no way
aware of isn't (yet) a conscious pain; it becomes conscious when a higher-order part of you registers
it. The standard worry: that explains your being *aware* that you're in pain, but does it explain the
*hurt*?
