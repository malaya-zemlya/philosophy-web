Epiphenomenalism is the view that phenomenal consciousness is real and non-physical but *causally
inert*: experiences are caused by brain processes yet have no effects of their own
([mental-states-causally-inert](../claims/mental-states-causally-inert.md)). Conscious feeling is a by-product of physical activity,
riding along without contributing to it. T. H. Huxley's image ([Huxley 1874](../sources/huxley-1874.md))
is a locomotive's steam-whistle, produced by the engine but doing nothing to drive the train; the
early Frank Jackson ([jackson-1982](../sources/jackson-1982.md)) defended a version of the view under the heading of
*epiphenomenal qualia*.

### What it buys, and where it sits

The view keeps a *phenomenal residue* ([phenomenal-residue](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md)) — experience that outruns any
causal or functional role — while still honouring the *causal closure of the physical*, the
principle that every physical event already has a sufficient physical cause. In David Chalmers's
taxonomy of options it is the **type-E** position ([materialism-types](../concepts/materialism-types.md);
[chalmers-2003](../sources/chalmers-2003.md)). It answers the [hard problem of consciousness](../questions/hard-problem.md)
by granting a fundamental felt residue that simply does no work. In effect it is the horn of
[dualism](dualism.md) that accepts causal inefficacy rather than positing mind-to-body interaction
(the *type-D* option).

### The signature objection

Epiphenomenalism's standard problems are forms of *self-stultification*. If experiences cause
nothing, it is unclear how our judgements and reports *about* our experiences could be caused by
them, and unclear how we could come to know our experiences at all, since the knowing and the
reporting would then be disconnected from the very states they are supposed to be about.

### In plain terms

Epiphenomenalism bites a strange bullet: your feelings are real, but they're side-effects that
change nothing, like the steam whistle that rides along with the engine without driving the train.
Your brain causes the pain *and* causes you to say "ouch"; the painfulness itself just tags along,
doing no pushing. It's the tidy way to keep both "experience is something extra" and "physics is
causally complete", at the cost of the unsettling thought that your feelings never actually *make
you do* anything (so how do you even come to talk about them?).
