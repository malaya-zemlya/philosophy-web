*Dual-aspect monism* holds that there is a single underlying reality that presents two
irreducible *aspects* — an inner, mental aspect and an outer, physical aspect — with neither
reducible to the other. One thing, seen two ways: the felt and the physical are guaranteed to
co-vary because they are aspects of the very same item. The position commits to the claim that
[reality has two irreducible aspects](../claims/reality-two-aspects-mental-physical.md).

### Historical sources

The classic statement is Spinoza's: there is one substance, God-or-Nature, that we grasp under
the *attribute* of thought as well as under the attribute of extension — two expressions of a
single reality ([spinoza-ethics](../sources/spinoza-ethics.md)). In the twentieth century the physicist Wolfgang Pauli
and the psychologist C. G. Jung developed a dual-aspect view on which the mental and the material
are complementary aspects of one underlying reality ([pauli-jung-1955](../sources/pauli-jung-1955.md)).

### How it answers the hard problem

Dual-aspect monism answers the [hard problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) by treating mind and matter
as one reality seen two ways. The notorious gap between felt experience and physical process
closes because there are not two things to bridge: the felt and the physical are guaranteed to
line up as aspects of a single underlying unity.

### What distinguishes it

Against *property* dualism, dual-aspect monism makes the mental and the physical *aspects of a
prior unity* rather than two kinds of property borne by physical things. Against
[neutral monism](neutral-monism.md), the base is not a neutral stuff of which mind and
matter are mere arrangements but a unity that intrinsically bears *both* aspects. And unlike
[Russellian monism](russellian-monism.md), it does not ground the physical *in* the
phenomenal but holds the two on a par.

### In plain terms

Dual-aspect monism says there's just one reality, but it always comes with two sides — an
"inside" (mental) and an "outside" (physical) — the way a single coin always has heads and tails,
or the way one event can be described in the language of brain cells or in the language of
feelings. Mind and matter aren't two substances and aren't reducible to each other; they're two
faces of the same underlying thing. That's why the felt and the physical line up so neatly: they
were one item all along.
