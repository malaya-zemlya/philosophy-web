Biological naturalism is the view that consciousness is a genuine, higher-level *biological*
feature of the brain — as much a product of neural tissue as digestion is a product of the
stomach. It is at once *physicalist*, holding that mind is wholly caused by and realised in the
brain's physical processes with nothing "over and above" them, and *anti-reductionist*, holding
that the first-person, felt character of experience is not thereby explained away or identified
with anything describable from the outside. The position is principally associated with John
Searle, who named it in *The Rediscovery of the Mind* ([searle-1992](../sources/searle-1992.md)). Its core
commitment is the claim that
[consciousness is a higher-level biological feature](../claims/consciousness-is-biological-feature.md).

### The core commitment

On this view consciousness is a real, higher-level feature *caused by* lower-level
neurobiological processes and *realised in* the brain ([consciousness-is-biological-feature](../claims/consciousness-is-biological-feature.md)).
The favoured analogy is digestion: the stomach's molecular activity both causes digestion and
constitutes it, and no one is tempted to think digestion floats free of the stomach or, conversely,
that it reduces to something non-biological. Consciousness stands to neurons as digestion stands
to the gut — entirely natural, entirely physical, but a feature of a particular kind of living
system.

What does the explanatory work is *causal powers*: the concrete capacities of brain matter to
bring about effects, not the abstract pattern of organisation those powers happen to implement.
Because it is the brain's causal powers that produce mind — not any program it might be described
as running — merely implementing the right *syntax* (formal symbol-manipulation) is not enough for
*semantics* (genuine meaning or understanding) ([syntax-insufficient-for-semantics](../claims/syntax-insufficient-for-semantics.md)). This
is the moral biological naturalism draws from the [chinese-room](../concepts/chinese-room.md) thought experiment and
its [argument](../arguments/chinese-room.md): a person hand-tracing a program for Chinese produces
correct outputs while understanding nothing, so running the program cannot be what understanding
consists in.

### Consequences for artificial intelligence

The stress on causal powers rather than program puts biological naturalism squarely against
"strong AI." It rejects [substrate independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md) — the thesis that
the right computational organisation suffices for mind on any hardware — and rejects the claim that
a large language model running on standard computers thereby has genuine mental states
([llm-has-mental-states](../claims/llm-has-mental-states.md)). Accordingly it answers both [substrate-independence](../questions/substrate-independence.md)
and [llm-moral-status](../questions/llm-moral-status.md) in the negative for any *purely computational* system: a computer
that simulates a brain no more has a mind than a computer that simulates a storm makes anything wet.

### How it differs from neighbouring views

Against [functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md), biological naturalism denies that the right causal
*organisation* suffices independently of the realiser's causal *powers*; sameness of functional
role does not guarantee sameness of mind. Unlike [proteocentrism](proteocentrism.md), the demand is not
for *carbon* as such but for the relevant causal powers — though Searle holds that, as a matter of
fact, only brain-like systems are so far known to possess them. And unlike [type-identity](type-identity.md),
it is *non-reductive*: consciousness is caused by neural processes without being identical to any
neural state-*type*, so it is not the claim that pain just is, say, C-fibre firing.

### In plain terms

Searle's view: consciousness is a biological phenomenon, produced by brains the way digestion is
produced by stomachs — perfectly physical, but the product of a living organ's specific causal
powers, not of any abstract recipe. So you can't get a mind just by running the right *program* on
any old hardware (his Chinese Room makes the point: shuffling symbols by rules gives you correct
outputs with zero understanding). The upshot for AI: a computer simulating a brain no more *has* a
mind than a computer simulating a storm gets you wet — you'd need a system with the right causal
powers, not just the right code.
