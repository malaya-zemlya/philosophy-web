*Neutral monism* holds that the fundamental stuff of the world is *neutral* — in itself neither
mental nor physical — and that mind and matter are two different *arrangements* of these same
neutral elements. There is one basic kind of thing, but it belongs to neither of the two
categories we ordinarily reach for; mind and matter are patterns in it, not fundamental kinds.
The position commits to the claim that [reality's
fundamental constituents are neutral](../claims/reality-fundamental-stuff-neutral.md).

### Historical sources

The view runs through late-nineteenth and early-twentieth-century philosophy and psychology.
Ernst Mach analysed the world into neutral *elements* — colours, sounds, pressures — that count
as physical when considered in their mutual dependencies and as psychological when considered in
their dependence on a living body ([mach-1914](../sources/mach-1914.md)). William James's doctrine of *pure
experience*, the proposal that there is one primal stuff of which both knower and known are
made, gave the position its most famous slogan ([james-1904](../sources/james-1904.md)). Bertrand Russell adopted
and systematised neutral monism, most fully in *The Analysis of Mind* ([russell-1921](../sources/russell-1921.md)).

### How it answers the hard problem

Neutral monism deflates the [hard problem](../questions/hard-problem.md) by denying the divide it
presupposes. The mind/matter distinction reduces to a difference in how neutral constituents are
grouped, so neither is fundamental and neither must be "got from" the other. The demand to
extract mind from matter — or matter from mind — is a trick question: both come from something
underneath that is neither.

### What distinguishes it

Neutral monism is the historical parent of [Russellian monism](russellian-monism.md), but
stays *neutral* where the Russellian view makes the intrinsic base (proto)phenomenal. Unlike
[dualism](dualism.md) there is one kind of fundamental stuff; unlike physicalism and
[idealism](idealism.md) that stuff is neither physical nor mental.

### In plain terms

Neutral monism says mind and matter aren't two ultimate kinds of thing, nor is one built from the
other — instead both are *patterns* in a single, more basic stuff that is in itself neither
mental nor physical. Think of the same dots forming, under one grouping, a "mental" picture and,
under another, a "physical" one. So the hard problem's "how do you get mind from matter (or
matter from mind)?" is a trick question: you get both from something underneath that is neither.
