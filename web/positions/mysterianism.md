*Mysterianism* holds that consciousness has a perfectly natural, physical explanation — there is
some property of the brain in virtue of which experience arises — but that the human mind is
*constitutively* unable to grasp what that property is. The [hard problem](../concepts/hard-problem.md)
of consciousness is therefore real and has a real answer; we are simply built so that we can never
reach it. The position is sometimes called *transcendental naturalism*, the term coined by Colin
McGinn in his 1989 paper "Can We Solve the Mind–Body Problem?" ([mcginn-1989](../sources/mcginn-1989.md)): realism
about the answer, pessimism about our access to it.

The view pairs two commitments. The first is naturalist optimism about the world: the link between
brain and experience is an ordinary physical fact, so consciousness
[has a natural explanation](../claims/consciousness-has-natural-explanation.md). The second is epistemic
pessimism about us: [humans are cognitively closed to
that explanatory property](../claims/humans-closed-to-psychophysical-link.md), much as a dog is closed to arithmetic. Put together, they answer the
question of how the brain produces consciousness ([the hard problem](../questions/hard-problem.md)) with
the formula "soluble in itself, insoluble by us." The reasons offered for this conclusion are set
out in the [cognitive-closure argument](../arguments/cognitive-closure.md).

### What sets it apart

Mysterianism occupies a narrow strip of logical space between the usual camps. Unlike *dualism*, it
is thoroughly naturalist: the psychophysical link is one more physical fact, not the work of a
second, non-physical substance. Unlike *reductive functionalism* and *illusionism*, it grants that
the hard problem is genuine and unsolved rather than dissolving it or declaring it a confusion. And
unlike the anti-physicalist reading of the explanatory gap — the idea that
[felt character outruns any functional role](../claims/phenomenal-states-not-exhausted-by-role.md), pressed in the
[explanatory-gap argument](../arguments/explanatory-gap.md) — it locates the gap in *our cognitive
limits* rather than in *the world*. The felt shortfall, on this telling, is a fact about the reach
of human concepts, not evidence of an ontological divide.

### In plain terms

Mysterianism says: consciousness is a normal, physical part of nature — there's no magic — but our
brains just aren't equipped to ever understand *how* the trick is done, the way a dog can't grasp
arithmetic no matter how long you teach it. So the mystery is permanent, yet there's nothing spooky
behind it. It's a middle path between "we'll crack it eventually" and "consciousness is non-physical."
