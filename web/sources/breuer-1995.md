relevance (method/form): the rigorous, physics-grounded version of the finite self-model bound used in
[finite-self-model-incomplete](../arguments/finite-self-model-incomplete.md). Breuer proves that an observer/apparatus that is a *proper
subsystem* of the system it measures cannot, even in principle, distinguish all of that system's
present states.

key framings to preserve when citing (verbatim from the paper):
- Abstract: "It is shown that it is impossible for an observer to distinguish all present states of a
  system in which he or she is contained, irrespective of whether this system is a classical or a
  quantum mechanical one and irrespective of whether the time evolution is deterministic or
  stochastic." (p. 197)
- §3 (the central argument): "it is impossible for an observer to distinguish all states of a system
  in which he or she is contained. The argument exploits self-reference properties, but it does not
  make any assumptions about the character of the time evolution." (p. 200) The general argument is
  classical-or-quantum and deterministic-or-stochastic; the quantum EPR-correlation result is a
  *separate* §4 corollary.
- Internal/external asymmetry: "the fact that an apparatus outside the observed system in general can
  distinguish all states" (p. 202) — i.e. only the *internal* vantage is bounded. (Directly
  [inside-vantage-access-limited](../claims/inside-vantage-access-limited.md).)
- Not Gödel: Breuer is explicit that "my argument does not have much more in common with Gödel's proof
  than the use of self-reference" (p. 202) — so any Gödel/Penrose framing (e.g.
  [explanation-must-be-internally-traversable](../claims/explanation-must-be-internally-traversable.md)) is a *different* analogy, not this result.
- Mechanism: a measurement is a map relating apparatus states to observed-system states; when the
  apparatus is contained in the observed system, self-reference makes a complete such correlation
  impossible. Lineage: Popper's (1950) non-self-predictability of Laplace's demon; Dalla Chiara 1977,
  Peres & Zurek 1982, Roessler 1987, Finkelstein 1988, Primas 1990.
- Scope: forbids distinguishing *all* states (complete self-measurement); leaves partial measurement
  untouched. §5: absolutely universally valid theories thus "at least partially lack operational
  justification"; theories can be operationally universal only in a "relative, observer-dependent
  sense."
