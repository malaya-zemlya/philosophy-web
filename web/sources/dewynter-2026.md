relevance (content): Adrian de Wynter builds and *trains* a perceptron inside the videogame
*Age of Empires II* — NAND gates made of goats running along grass/bridge rails, ice patches for
synchronization — and proves the game functionally- and Turing-complete. The philosophical payload
is **methodological**, not metaphysical: the anthropomorphic attributes ascribed to large language
models are *empirically non-unique* across substrates (a neural net could equally run on LEGO or the
Greater Boston Area), so assuming such attributes exist or do not, a priori and independent of
substrate, yields circular or uninformative conclusions; he proposes a "null assumption" of
non-uniqueness plus explicit measurement criteria. De Wynter explicitly does *not* claim AoE2 has
human-like attributes. Grounds [aoe2-circuits](../concepts/aoe2-circuits.md), the premises
[perceptron-realizable-in-any-substrate](../claims/perceptron-realizable-in-any-substrate.md) and [apriori-anthropomorphism-circular](../claims/apriori-anthropomorphism-circular.md), the
conclusion [llm-attributes-empirically-non-unique](../claims/llm-attributes-empirically-non-unique.md), and the argument
[aoe2-anthropomorphism](../arguments/aoe2-anthropomorphism.md).
