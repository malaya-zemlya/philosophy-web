relevance (content): the paper in which Hilary Putnam mounts the two classic counterexamples to
*logical (analytical) behaviourism* — the view that to be in a mental state just is to behave, or be
disposed to behave, in certain ways. The **super-spartans** are a community who feel pain but have
trained themselves to suppress every sign of it (the *super-super-spartans* of the "X-world" drop
even pain-talk), so there is pain without the behavioural disposition: behaviour is not *necessary*
for the mental state. The **perfect actor** runs the case in reverse, producing the entire
behavioural profile of pain while feeling none, so behaviour is not *sufficient*. Together they
ground [super-spartan](../concepts/super-spartan.md) and [perfect-actor](../concepts/perfect-actor.md) in this web. Putnam himself took the
moral to be not dualism but the move to a causal-role account of the mental — the seed of the
[functionalism](../concepts/functionalism.md) he went on to develop.

Reference:
- Putnam, H. (1963). Brains and behavior. In R. J. Butler (Ed.), *Analytical philosophy: Second
  series* (pp. 1–19). Blackwell. (Reprinted in Putnam, H. (1975). *Mind, language and reality:
  Philosophical papers, Vol. 2* (pp. 325–341). Cambridge University Press.)
