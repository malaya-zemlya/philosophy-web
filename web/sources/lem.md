relevance (content): Lem develops the point that a being cannot prove its consciousness to another,
and, crucially, that this unprovability is *mutual*: it runs symmetrically between human and machine,
neither party able to demonstrate an inner life to the other. This is the source of the symmetry move
used against [first-person-access](../arguments/first-person-access.md) (see [access-gap-symmetric](../claims/access-gap-symmetric.md),
[access-symmetry-rebuttal](../arguments/access-symmetry-rebuttal.md)).

The locus is the *Cogito paradox* passage of "A History of Bitic Literature." Lem credits Alan Turing
with first seeing that a machine behaving like a human cannot be told apart from a human "in the
psychic respect", so a machine able to converse must be granted consciousness on the same grounds we
grant it to one another. In bitistics the Cogito paradox then returns ironically, as the machines'
*doubt that humans think*. The situation, Lem writes, turns out to be perfectly and bilaterally
symmetric: we cannot prove that a machine thinks and experiences its states as mental, and machines
equally cannot prove that we, their partners, think consciously as they do. Neither side knows what
mental states the other means by the word "consciousness".

Reference:
- Lem, S. (1984). A history of bitic literature. In *Imaginary magnitude* (M. E. Heine, Trans.).
  Harcourt Brace Jovanovich. (Original work published 1973)

key passage (the symmetry; quoted from a Russian translation supplied by the editor, since the
canonical English is Heine's): "Ситуация вдруг оказалась идеально и двусторонне симметричной. *Мы* не
можем иметь совершенной уверенности (неоспоримых доводов), что машина мыслит — и, мысля, переживает
свои состояния как психические… Но и машины точно так же не могут найти доказательств того, что мы,
их партнеры, *мыслим* сознательно — как они. Ни одна из сторон не знает, какие психические состояния
другая сторона подразумевает под словом «сознание»."
