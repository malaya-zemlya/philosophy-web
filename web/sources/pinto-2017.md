relevance (content): the empirical basis for the objection that the split-brain case does not
show a subject dividing. Pinto and colleagues found that callosotomy patients can respond
accurately to stimuli across both visual half-fields with either hand, indicating divided
perceptual access but a single, undivided responding subject. Cited by
[undivided-split-brain](../arguments/undivided-split-brain.md) and its premise
[split-brain-single-subject](../claims/split-brain-single-subject.md), against the flagship base
case of [subjects-can-combine](../arguments/subjects-can-combine.md).

Reference:
- Pinto, Y., Neville, D. A., Otten, M., Corballis, P. M., Lamme, V. A. F., de Haan, E. H. F.,
  Foschi, N., & Fabri, M. (2017). Split brain: Divided perception but undivided consciousness.
  *Brain*, *140*(5), 1231–1237. https://doi.org/10.1093/brain/aww358
