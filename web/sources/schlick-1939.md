The classic *consequentialist* (forward-looking) theory of moral responsibility. In "When is a man
responsible?" (chap. VII) Schlick argues that the question of responsibility is not the retrospective
question *who caused this?* but the practical one *whom can we influence?* — the point of asking who
is responsible is to determine at whom reward and punishment should be aimed so as to shape future
conduct. Punishment, on this view, is not retribution for a free act but "an educative measure," a
means of forming motives. This is the tradition against which
[incentives-require-alternatives](../claims/incentives-require-alternatives.md) is set: it grounds the idea that
responsibility practices are tools others use to steer an agent's behaviour.

Reference: Schlick, M. (1939). *Problems of ethics* (D. Rynin, Trans.). Prentice-Hall. (Original work published 1930)
