relevance (content): the major contemporary defense that minds can *combine* — that several suitably
organized subjects can constitute a further composite subject that contains them as parts, and
conversely that a single subject may be a tight cooperation of mental parts. Roelofs surveys brain
structure, split-brain cases, psychological conflict, and social cooperation and concludes that
composite subjectivity is more common than we assume — a direct, sustained answer to the
[combination problem](../arguments/combination-problem.md). Grounds [subjects-can-combine](../arguments/subjects-can-combine.md),
[subject-unity-graded-by-integration](../claims/subject-unity-graded-by-integration.md), and [subjects-actually-merge-and-divide](../claims/subjects-actually-merge-and-divide.md).

Reference:
- Roelofs, L. (2019). *Combining minds: How to think about composite subjectivity*. Oxford University Press.
