The founding statement of the *reactive-attitudes* account of moral responsibility. P. F. Strawson
argues that resentment, gratitude, indignation, and forgiveness are not conclusions we draw from a
theory of desert, nor instruments we adopt for their social utility, but the natural expressions of
our involvement with one another as persons — and that no general theoretical finding, determinism
included, could rationally dislodge them. This grounds the tradition cited as denying
[responsibility-is-forward-looking](../claims/responsibility-is-forward-looking.md): against the consequentialist,
blame and praise are constitutive of interpersonal life, not tools justified by their effect on
future conduct. (Not to be confused with Galen Strawson, [strawson-1994](strawson-1994.md).)

Reference: Strawson, P. F. (1962). Freedom and resentment. *Proceedings of the British Academy*, *48*, 1–25.
