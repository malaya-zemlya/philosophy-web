The **minimal requirements** for a theory of consciousness set a floor: whatever else it does, a
theory earns the right to be called a *theory of consciousness* only if it discharges two explananda.
They are deliberately thin. They do not tell a theory what to conclude, do not require it to posit or
to deny felt experience, and do not favour physicalism over dualism; they only name the two things
every candidate must address before its verdicts can be assessed.

### The two mandatory explananda

**(a) Access consciousness.** The theory must give an account of
[access-consciousness](access-consciousness.md) — of which information a system can bring to bear on reasoning,
report, and the control of action, which information it cannot, and how the accessing mechanism
works. This is the "easy problem" side of the ledger in Chalmers's sense: hard in practice, but a
matter of function and mechanism, and no theory that stays silent about it has described a mind at
all.

**(b) The meta-problem.** The theory must explain why people are so strongly moved to think there is
a [hard-problem](hard-problem.md) — why we produce talk of an explanatory gap, of ineffable qualia, of an
inner light that no physical story seems to capture. This requirement is *symmetric*, and it binds
every theory whatever its metaphysics:

- A theory that posits **no** phenomenality (an [illusionist](../positions/illusionism.md) or purely
  functional theory) must still explain how creatures with no such property come to be so certain
  they have it, and come to insist it cannot be explained.
- A theory that **does** posit phenomenality (a realist, dualist, or panpsychist theory) does not get
  the meta-problem for free. It must explain how that phenomenality *causes* the judgments, reports,
  and gap-talk in the first place — how the posited experience gets into the causal chain that ends
  in someone saying "but you still haven't explained the redness". Simply having phenomenality on the
  books does not by itself explain the behaviour of talking about it.

This is the demand sharpened by the [paradox-of-phenomenal-judgment](paradox-of-phenomenal-judgment.md) and generalized as the
*meta-problem of consciousness* ([chalmers-2018](../sources/chalmers-2018.md)): our judgments about experience are
physical events with physical causes, so any theory owes an account of those causes that squares with
what it says experience is.

### How common positions answer

The requirements are easiest to see at work as a scorecard. Below, each common position is scored on the two
explananda: a dash (—) marks a box the position characteristically leaves empty, and where it does
answer, the note sketches how. The reading is broad-brush and a determined proponent can always say
more, but the pattern is instructive: nearly every view has an account of access, while the meta-problem
is where they part company — and where several leave a blank.

- [Global workspace theory](../positions/global-workspace.md) — *(a)* access *is* the global broadcast of information to consumer systems; *(b)* —, typically silent on why a residue seems left over.
- [Higher-order theories](../positions/higher-order-theories.md) — *(a)* a state is conscious when suitably represented by a higher-order state, hence reportable; *(b)* partial, in that the same machinery can be turned to why we judge "something more," though that is not its aim.
- [Type-A materialism](../positions/type-a-materialism.md) — *(a)* functional analysis; access is all there is to explain; *(b)* diagnoses the gap-intuition as conceptual confusion.
- [Illusionism](../positions/illusionism.md) — *(a)* functional/representational account of access and report; *(b)* its defining task, to explain why introspection misrepresents us as phenomenally conscious.
- [Eliminative materialism](../positions/eliminative-materialism.md) — *(a)* a functional story of information-uptake survives; *(b)* treats phenomenal talk as a false folk theory to be explained and replaced.
- [Type-B materialism](../positions/type-b-materialism.md) — *(a)* functional; *(b)* the phenomenal-concepts strategy, on which the *appearance* of a gap comes from the structure of phenomenal concepts, not a real gap.
- [Naturalistic / property dualism](../positions/dualism.md) — *(a)* the "easy problems," explained functionally; *(b)* acknowledged (Chalmers named it) but unresolved, with the [paradox-of-phenomenal-judgment](paradox-of-phenomenal-judgment.md) biting hardest here.
- [Epiphenomenalism](../positions/epiphenomenalism.md) — *(a)* functional; *(b)* —, since inert qualia by definition cannot cause the very talk about them.
- [Russellian monism](../positions/russellian-monism.md) / [panpsychism](../positions/panpsychism.md) — *(a)* structure and function, as physics delivers them; *(b)* —, with no account of how micro-phenomenality drives our gap-talk (the combination problem sits upstream).
- [Integrated information theory](../positions/integrated-information-theory.md) — *(a)* —, deliberately orthogonal to access (Φ is not a theory of report); *(b)* —.
- Substance (interactionist) dualism — *(a)* —, access via a non-physical mind, largely stipulated; *(b)* —, the soul's self-knowledge posited, not explained.
- [Mysterianism](../positions/mysterianism.md) — *(a)* grants the access/easy problems are tractable; *(b)* —, declining in principle (the answer is held beyond our cognitive reach).

### What the requirements leave open

The requirements are neutral on the substantive questions. Theories meeting them **may or may not posit
an actual phenomenology** ([phenomenal-consciousness](phenomenal-consciousness.md)); they may disagree about what is
accessible, and they may make different empirical predictions. A theory is also free to introduce
**any additional entities it likes** — souls, a universal ego, irreducible p-consciousness, a quantum
amplitude. When it does, it owes the usual account of why those posits are worth
introducing and what they mean; but that is the ordinary burden borne by *any* theory in *any*
domain, not a special tax levied on theories of consciousness. The requirements add nothing here beyond
standard theoretical hygiene.

### Minimal, not complete

The word *minimal* is meant strictly: (a) and (b) are a floor, not a ceiling. A good theory of
consciousness will usually be far richer — offering an account of the self, of attention, of
introspection, of the unity and temporal flow of experience, of why some functions feel like anything
and others do not. The claim is only that a theory failing to address *either* (a) or (b) has not yet
met the entry requirement, however elaborate its further apparatus.

### In plain terms

If someone hands you a "theory of consciousness", there are two questions it has to answer before it
counts as one at all. First: how does the mind get hold of information — what can you notice, report,
and act on, what stays hidden, and how does that machinery work? Second, and easy to forget: why do we
all feel there's a deep mystery here — why do people say experience is special, private, impossible to
put into words? A theory that says there's no inner glow still has to explain why we're so sure there
is one; and a theory that says there *is* an inner glow still has to explain how that glow makes us
talk about it. Beyond these two, a theory can say whatever it wants and add whatever ingredients it
wants (a soul, exotic physics, anything) — as long as it explains why those ingredients are worth
having, which is just what we ask of any theory anywhere. And this is only the *minimum*: real
theories can and should be much richer.
