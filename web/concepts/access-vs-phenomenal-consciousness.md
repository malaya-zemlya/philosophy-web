primary definition: Ned Block's distinction (Block, "On a Confusion about a Function of
Consciousness", 1995) between two things "consciousness" can mean.

senses:
- *access consciousness (A-consciousness)* — a state is A-conscious when its content is *poised* for
  use in reasoning, for direct rational control of action, and for verbal report. A functional /
  information-availability notion: it is about what the rest of the system can *do with* the content.
- *phenomenal consciousness (P-consciousness)* — the "what-it-is-like" aspect of a state, its
  felt/qualitative character. This is what the hard problem ([hard-problem](hard-problem.md)) is about.

why the distinction matters here: the two can come apart in principle, in *both* directions. A system
could have richly reportable self-models (A-consciousness of its own states) with nothing it is like
to be it (no P-consciousness); conversely, Block argues phenomenal consciousness may *overflow*
access — there may be more in experience than gets globally broadcast for report. So "has A" neither
entails nor is entailed by "has P." This is the pivot on which several moves in the LLM-introspection
cluster turn: the danger of reading a *functional/access* capacity (e.g.
[llm-functional-introspection](../claims/llm-functional-introspection.md)) as a *phenomenal* one, and the observation that *posing* the
hard problem is itself an access-dependent act ([posing-the-gap-requires-access](../claims/posing-the-gap-requires-access.md)) even though
having phenomenal states is not.

### In plain terms

"Conscious" runs together two different things. One is **access**: the information is available to the
rest of your mind — you can reason with it, act on it, and say it out loud. The other is **feel**:
there's something it's like to undergo the state (the redness of red, the hurt of pain). They're not
the same. You could imagine a machine that has all the *access* — it reports its inner states fluently
— with no *feel* at all; and some philosophers think the reverse can happen too, that we feel more
than we can ever report. Keeping the two apart is the main guard against a tempting mistake:
seeing a system talk about its own insides and concluding it must therefore *feel* something.
