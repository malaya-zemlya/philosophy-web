*Philosophy of mind* is the branch of philosophy that asks what the mind is, what mental states are,
and how they fit into the rest of nature. Its subject is everything that falls under "the mental" —
thought, perception, sensation, emotion, belief, desire, and above all *consciousness*, the fact that
for a creature like us there is *something it is like* to be alive. The field studies what these states
are, how they relate to the brain and body, how they can be *about* a world and *cause* behaviour, and
which kinds of system can have them at all. It is one of the core areas of metaphysics and, since the
late twentieth century, the meeting point of philosophy with psychology, neuroscience, and artificial
intelligence.

The discipline is organised around a single central problem — the [mind-body
problem](mind-body-problem.md), the question of how mental phenomena relate to physical ones — but it is wider than that
problem alone. Where the mind-body problem asks specifically how the mental and the physical fit
together, philosophy of mind also takes up questions that can be posed without settling that
relationship: how thought manages to be *about* things, what perception delivers, what makes a person
the same person over time, and whether a machine could think. This entry surveys the field and serves
as the hub from which its problems, positions, and arguments branch.

### The questions of the field

Several connected questions make up the territory. They overlap, but each can be pursued on its own.

- **The nature of mind** — the ontological question at the heart of the [mind-body-problem](mind-body-problem.md):
  is the mind identical to the brain, a distinct substance or property, an illusion, or the intrinsic
  nature of matter? The classic statement is René Descartes' *real distinction* between thinking thing
  and extended thing ([descartes-meditations](../sources/descartes-meditations.md)).
- **Consciousness** — *why is any of this accompanied by experience at all?* Even a complete functional
  account of the brain seems to leave open why it should *feel* like anything: the
  [hard problem](hard-problem.md), framed by the question of [why there is
  experience](../questions/hard-problem.md) and sharpened by Thomas Nagel's "what is it like to be a bat?" ([nagel-1974](../sources/nagel-1974.md)). A
  standing distinction here is between [access and phenomenal
  consciousness](access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md) — between information being available for use and there being a felt quality to it. The
  topic as a whole is surveyed at [consciousness](consciousness.md).
- **Intentionality** — *how can a mental state be about something?* The "aboutness" of thought
  ([intentionality](intentionality.md)) was made central by Franz Brentano, who called directedness upon an object
  "the mark of the mental" ([brentano-1874](../sources/brentano-1874.md)); how any physical state could have it is
  [the problem of intentionality](../questions/intentionality.md).
- **Mental causation** — *how can the mental have physical effects?* If the physical world is causally
  closed, mental causes seem either to collapse into physical ones or to be left idle — the puzzle
  Princess Elisabeth pressed on Descartes ([elisabeth-1643](../sources/elisabeth-1643.md)), posed as
  [the problem of mental causation](../questions/mental-causation.md) and made precise by
  [Kim's causal exclusion argument](../arguments/causal-exclusion.md) ([kim-2005](../sources/kim-2005.md)).
- **Self-knowledge and the first person** — the privileged, non-inferential way a subject knows its own
  states ([first-person-access](first-person-access.md)), and whether introspection is a genuine *observation* or itself
  a fallible representation ([acquaintance-datum-or-representation](../questions/acquaintance-datum-or-representation.md)).
- **Perception and the external world** — what experience puts us in touch with, and whether it warrants
  belief in a mind-independent world ([external-world-knowledge](../questions/external-world-knowledge.md)).
- **Personal identity** — what makes a person at one time the same as a person at another
  ([personal-identity](../questions/personal-identity.md)), and whether identity is even all-or-nothing
  ([personal-identity-admits-of-degree](../claims/personal-identity-admits-of-degree.md)).

### The space of positions

The competing answers are mapped in detail under the [mind-body-problem](mind-body-problem.md); in outline they run
from [dualism](../positions/dualism.md) (the mental is not physical) through the large
[physicalist](../positions/physicalism.md) family (the mental just *is* physical, whether by identity,
functional role, or elimination) to the non-standard monisms ([idealism](../positions/idealism.md),
[neutral monism](../positions/neutral-monism.md), [Russellian monism](../positions/russellian-monism.md) and
[panpsychism](../positions/panpsychism.md)) and reframings such as Aristotle's
[hylomorphism](../positions/hylomorphism.md) and Gilbert Ryle's deflation of "the ghost in the machine"
([ryle-1949](../sources/ryle-1949.md)). Each answer honours some of the data and pays a price on the rest, which is why
the field's central problem has stayed open for four centuries.

### How the field argues

Philosophy of mind works largely through *thought experiments* that pump a verdict from an imagined
case: the conceivability of a [zombie](../arguments/zombie-conceivability.md) physically identical to a
conscious being but with no experience; Mary the colour scientist, who knows all the physics of colour
yet seems to learn something on first seeing red ([knowledge-argument](knowledge-argument.md)); Searle's
[Chinese Room](chinese-room.md), pressing whether running a program could constitute
understanding. These join conceptual analysis, the deliverances of introspection, and — increasingly —
empirical results from the cognitive and brain sciences, which the field both draws on and interprets.
Such cases are powerful but not decisive: their force depends on how far the imagined scenario lies from
the cases our intuitions were trained on.

### The machine turn

A modern centre of gravity, and the focus of much of this web, is the mind in artificial systems. Alan
Turing replaced the "too meaningless" question *can machines think?* with an operational test
([turing-test](turing-test.md); [turing-1950](../sources/turing-1950.md)). [Functionalism](functionalism.md) — minds
individuated by causal role rather than material — opened the possibility of
[substrate independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md), that a mind could run on silicon as well as on
neurons. That possibility turns the field's oldest questions into urgent ones: whether a large language
model could be conscious, whether its reports are evidence of anything, and whether such a system could
have [moral status](../questions/llm-moral-status.md). Here philosophy of mind stops being only a theory of
*our* minds and becomes a theory of *possible* minds.

### In plain terms

Philosophy of mind is the part of philosophy that tries to figure out what minds are. You think, feel,
see colours, want things, and there's something it's like to be you — and you're also just a body and a
brain made of ordinary matter. How do those go together? Is your mind your brain, something your brain
makes, a separate non-physical thing, or even an illusion? And around that big question sit a cluster of
others: how a thought manages to be *about* something, how feelings can make your body move, what you
really know just by looking inward, what keeps you the same person over a lifetime — and, lately, whether
a computer could ever have a mind of its own. The field tackles these partly by careful argument and
partly by vivid "what if" scenarios (a perfect zombie, a colour scientist who's never seen colour, a room
that fakes Chinese), and increasingly by talking to brain science and AI. Almost every proposed answer
explains some of it neatly and stumbles on the rest, which is why the subject is still wide open.
