Mental states can be sorted by their *order* — by what they are *about*. A *first-order* state is
directed at the world or the body: a perception of red, a belief that it is raining, a pain. A
*higher-order* state is directed at one's own *mental* states: a thought or perception *about* a
first-order state, such as the thought "I am seeing red" or an awareness of one's own pain. Orders
can iterate — a third-order state is about a second-order one — but the philosophically loaded
contrast is simply first- versus higher-order.

### Key terms

The theories that use this distinction invoke several more specific notions:

- *First-order representation* — world-directed content; the state represents how things stand outside.
- *Higher-order thought* (HOT) — an occurrent thought about a current mental state ([rosenthal-2005](../sources/rosenthal-2005.md)).
- *Higher-order perception* (HOP), or *inner sense* — a quasi-perceptual monitoring of one's own
  states ([lycan-1996](../sources/lycan-1996.md), [armstrong-1968](../sources/armstrong-1968.md)), an idea with roots in Locke's "inner
  sense" ([locke-1689](../sources/locke-1689.md)).
- *Dispositional higher-order* content — content that need only be poised or available, not actually
  entertained ([carruthers-2000](../sources/carruthers-2000.md)).

### The dispute it feeds

The distinction does its main work in the question of *what makes a state conscious*:

- *Higher-order theories* ([higher-order-theories](../positions/higher-order-theories.md),
  [consciousness as higher-order
  representation](../claims/consciousness-is-higher-order-representation.md)) hold that a first-order state is conscious only if a suitable higher-order state
  represents it — consciousness is the mind representing itself.
- *First-order representationalism* ([representationalism](../positions/representationalism.md),
  [phenomenal character as representational](../claims/phenomenal-character-is-representational.md)) holds
  that phenomenal character is fixed by *first-order* world-directed content, with no higher-order
  state required. [Global workspace theory](../positions/global-workspace.md) likewise needs only wide
  availability, not a dedicated higher-order state.

### Keeping it apart from neighbouring distinctions

*Order* cross-cuts [the access/phenomenal distinction](access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md):
a higher-order state is one way content can become access-conscious, but "order" concerns *what a
state is about*, not access versus feel. *Introspection* and self-monitoring just are a matter of
having higher-order states about one's own mind — the structure behind
[functional introspection in LLMs](../claims/llm-functional-introspection.md) and
[privileged first-person access](../claims/privileged-first-person-access.md). And order (world- versus
mind-directed) is distinct again from the *first-person versus third-person* contrast (whose vantage
one takes): a first-order state can be access-conscious, and a higher-order state can itself be
unconscious.

### In plain terms

Thoughts come in layers depending on what they're *about*. A **first-order** state is about the world
— seeing the apple is red, believing it'll rain, feeling a pain. A **higher-order** state is about
*your own mind* — noticing that you're seeing red, thinking "I'm in pain." (You can stack further — a
thought about that thought — but the key split is just world-directed vs mind-directed.)

Why it matters here: one camp says a state only becomes *conscious* when a higher-order state notices
it (consciousness = the mind watching itself); the rival says the conscious feel is already in the
plain first-order experience of the world, with no inner watcher needed. Several nodes take sides in
that fight — this entry just pins down the vocabulary so you don't need the background.
