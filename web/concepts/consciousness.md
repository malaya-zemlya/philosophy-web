*Consciousness* is the fact that, for some creatures, there is *something it is like* to be them —
Thomas Nagel's phrase ([Nagel](../sources/nagel-1974.md)) for the felt, subjective character of experience.
To be conscious of the smell of coffee, the ache of a bruise, or the blue of the sky is for those states
to *seem* some way *from the inside*. The word covers several things — being awake rather than
unconscious, being aware *of* something, being aware *of oneself* — but its philosophically central sense
is this one: *phenomenal* consciousness, raw felt experience. This entry surveys the topic and serves as a
hub for the cluster of problems, theories, and arguments it generates; the question of how consciousness
fits the physical world is treated under the [mind-body-problem](mind-body-problem.md), of which it is the hardest
strand.

### Access and phenomenal consciousness

A distinction owed to Ned Block ([block-1995](../sources/block-1995.md)) organises much of the field. A state is
*access*-conscious when its content is available to the rest of the system — poised for reasoning, verbal
report, and the rational control of behaviour. It is *phenomenally* conscious when there is something it
is like to be in it ([access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness](access-vs-phenomenal-consciousness.md)). The two usually travel together in
us, which is why they are easily run together; but they come apart in principle, and whether they can come
apart in fact — whether there could be access without any feel, or feel without access — is a live
question. The distinction matters above all because it marks where explanation seems to run out: we can
see how a brain makes information *available*; why that availability should be *felt* is another matter.

### The easy and the hard problems

David Chalmers ([chalmers-1995](../sources/chalmers-1995.md)) sorted the questions into the *easy problems* and the *hard
problem*. The easy problems — how the brain integrates information, discriminates stimuli, reports its
states, controls behaviour — are "easy" only by comparison: they ask for *mechanisms*, and we know what a
successful answer would look like. The [hard problem](hard-problem.md)
([why there is experience at all](../questions/hard-problem.md)) is to say why the performance of any such
functions should be accompanied by experience — why it doesn't all go on "in the dark." Closely related is
the [explanatory gap](../arguments/explanatory-gap.md): even a complete physical account of the brain seems to
leave us with no idea *how* the physical constitutes the felt.

### Theories of consciousness

Scientific and philosophical theories try to say what consciousness *is* or what brings it about. The
[global workspace](../positions/global-workspace.md) view identifies it with information broadcast widely across
the brain for use by many subsystems. [Integrated information
theory](../positions/integrated-information-theory.md) identifies it with a system's integrated information ($\Phi$), making consciousness a structural
feature any sufficiently integrated system would have. [Higher-order
theories](../positions/higher-order-theories.md) hold that a state is conscious when the mind represents *itself* as being in it.
[Representationalism](../positions/representationalism.md) reduces the felt character of experience to its
representational content. [Illusionism](../positions/illusionism.md) takes the boldest line: that phenomenal
consciousness, as usually conceived, is an introspective *illusion* — there seems to be a further felt
residue, but the seeming is all there is. [Biological naturalism](../positions/biological-naturalism.md) treats
consciousness as a higher-level biological feature caused by, but not reducible to, neurobiology.

### Why it resists explanation

Several arguments keep consciousness philosophically intractable by pressing that no amount of physical or
functional fact seems to *entail* the facts of experience. The
[conceivability of zombies](../arguments/zombie-conceivability.md) — beings physically just like us but with no
inner light on — suggests experience is a further fact beyond the physical. The
[knowledge argument](knowledge-argument.md) (Mary, who knows all the physics of colour yet seems to
learn something on first seeing red) presses the same point through what complete knowledge leaves out.
And the bedrock datum of [first-person access](first-person-access.md) — that each subject is
directly acquainted with its own experience — is what makes the gap feel undeniable from the inside, even
as it stays invisible from the outside.

### The machine question

Because consciousness is defined by felt subjectivity rather than by any particular biology, it is an open
question which systems have it. If consciousness depends only on functional organisation, it could be
[substrate-independent](../claims/substrate-independence.md) and arise in artificial systems; if it depends on
the intrinsic causal powers of living tissue, it could not. The stakes are not merely theoretical: whether
an AI is phenomenally conscious bears directly on whether it has interests and
[moral status](../questions/llm-moral-status.md), and the access/phenomenal distinction warns that fluent
self-report is at most evidence of *access*, never by itself of *feeling*.

### In plain terms

Consciousness is the plain fact that your life is *lived from the inside* — there's something it's like to
taste chocolate, stub your toe, or see red, rather than those just being events in a machine with nobody
home. Philosophers split it in two: the "access" part (your brain having information on hand to talk about
and act on) is the kind science is steadily explaining; the "felt" part (why any of it actually *feels*
like anything) is the one nobody can yet explain — the famous *hard problem*. There are many theories
about what produces it, from "information shared widely in the brain" to "it's partly an illusion," and a
family of puzzles — philosophical zombies, the colour scientist who's never seen colour — suggesting the
felt part slips through every physical net. And the same mystery now faces AI: we can tell a machine has
information available, but whether there's anyone *in there* feeling anything is exactly what we don't know
how to check.
