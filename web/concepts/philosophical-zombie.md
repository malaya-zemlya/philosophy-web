primary definition: a *philosophical zombie* is a being **microphysically (and functionally) identical**
to a conscious person but with **no phenomenal consciousness** — all the same physical states,
behaviour, and functional organisation, yet nothing it is like to be it; "all dark inside"
([chalmers-1996](../sources/chalmers-1996.md)). It is not the shambling horror-movie zombie; it is a same-physics twin that
merely lacks experience.

what it is for: the zombie is the engine of the **conceivability argument** against physicalism. If such
a duplicate is even *possible*, then the physical facts do not fix the facts about experience, so
phenomenal consciousness is something *over and above* the physical ([consciousness-fundamental-nonphysical](../claims/consciousness-fundamental-nonphysical.md)).
The whole dispute concentrates on the move from *conceiving* a zombie to its *being possible*
([conceivability-entails-possibility](../claims/conceivability-entails-possibility.md)).

relation to neighbours: a limiting case of *absent qualia*; cousin to the *inverted spectrum*. It
sharpens what the explanatory gap ([explanatory-gap](../arguments/explanatory-gap.md)) gestures at (that argument's premise
already leans on zombie conceivability), and it is exactly the intuition the phenomenal-concept strategy
([gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts](../arguments/gap-deflated-by-phenomenal-concepts.md)) tries to defuse by granting conceivability while
denying possibility.

### In plain terms

A "philosophical zombie" is an imagined being that's an atom-for-atom copy of you — same brain, same
behaviour, says "ouch" when pricked, talks about its feelings — except there's no one home: no inner
experience at all, total darkness inside. The thought isn't that such things exist; it's that *if you
can coherently imagine one*, then experience must be something extra that the physical facts alone don't
pin down. Nearly the whole fight is over that "if you can imagine it, it's genuinely possible" step.
