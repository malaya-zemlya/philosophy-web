primary definition: Searle's thought experiment ([searle-1980](../sources/searle-1980.md)). A monolingual English speaker
is sealed in a room with a rulebook (a program) that lets him manipulate Chinese symbols by their
shapes alone. Fed Chinese questions, he returns, by following the rules, Chinese answers
indistinguishable from a native speaker's — yet he understands no Chinese. The system has the right
*syntax* (formal symbol manipulation) without any *semantics* (understanding, meaning, intentionality).

what it targets (and what distinguishes it from neighbours): the Chinese Room attacks **strong AI** /
computational functionalism — the claim that *implementing the right program* is sufficient for a mind.
Distinct from the lookup-table / "Blockhead" case ([lookup-table-mind](lookup-table-mind.md)): that attacks the
sufficiency of *behaviour* and has *no* internal processing, whereas the Room *does* run a program and
targets the sufficiency of *computation* for *understanding* specifically. Distinct from the
[chinese-nation](chinese-nation.md): that population realises a mind's functional organisation and is pressed
against *qualia*; the Room is pressed against *intentionality/semantics*. Three different experiments,
three different sufficiency theses.

characteristic replies (each an objection-in-waiting): the **Systems Reply** (the *person* doesn't
understand, but the whole *system* — person + rulebook + room — does); the **Robot Reply** (embed the
program in a body with causal commerce with the world, supplying grounding); the **Brain Simulator
Reply** (let the program simulate the actual neuron-by-neuron activity of a Chinese speaker). Searle
rejoins each.

### In plain terms

Imagine you're locked in a room with a giant instruction book. Notes in Chinese slide under the door;
you can't read a word of Chinese, but the book says "if you see these squiggles, copy out those
squiggles," so you shuffle symbols and pass back replies that fool fluent speakers outside. You're
behaving exactly like someone who understands Chinese — yet you understand nothing; you're just moving
shapes around. Searle's point: a computer is in the same boat. Running a program is *shuffling symbols
by their shapes*, and no amount of that, he says, adds up to actually *understanding* what the symbols
mean. (The famous comeback: maybe *you* don't understand, but the whole room-plus-rulebook system does.)
