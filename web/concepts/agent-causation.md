*Agent causation* is the thesis that a free action is caused not by prior events — desires,
beliefs, neural states — but by the agent herself, a persisting substance, exercising an
irreducible power to originate. On this view, when you freely raise your hand, the cause of the
decision is not an earlier state of you; it is *you*, full stop. Roderick Chisholm gave the
doctrine its classic modern statement: in free action each of us is "a prime mover unmoved,"
initiating causal chains without being caused to do so
(Chisholm, [1964](../sources/chisholm-1964.md)).

The view is the most metaphysically ambitious form of libertarianism
([libertarianism](../positions/libertarianism.md)). Its motivation is a felt inadequacy in event-causal pictures of
agency: if a decision is just one more event caused by earlier events, the agent seems to be a
place where causation happens rather than a source of it. Agent causation answers by adding a new
primitive to the ontology — substance-causation by a self — designed to make the agent the
un-preempted author of her acts.

Its standard difficulties are two. First, *intelligibility*: critics doubt that "the agent caused
it, but no event involving the agent did" describes a coherent causal relation, rather than a label
for the absence of one. Second, *timing and rate*: nothing about a substance, as opposed to its
states, seems apt to explain why the action happened *now* rather than earlier or later. Derek
Pereboom's [disappearing-agent objection](../arguments/disappearing-agent.md) (Pereboom, [2014](../sources/pereboom-2014.md)) presses a related
worry against libertarianism generally: given all the prior conditions, nothing about the agent
*settles* which decision occurs, so the agent as originator vanishes from the story.

### In plain terms

Most explanations of what you do point to earlier happenings: you chose salad because you felt
health-conscious, because of this morning's resolution, because of yesterday's news. Agent
causation says that for genuinely free choices the buck stops somewhere new — not at an earlier
happening, but at *you*, the person, who simply starts the chain. Fans say this is the only picture
on which you are truly the author of your actions; critics say "the person caused it, but nothing
about the person made it happen" is a mystery dressed up as an explanation.
