A *Frankfurt case* is a thought experiment designed to show that a person can be morally
responsible for an action even though she could not have done otherwise. Harry Frankfurt introduced
the recipe (Frankfurt, [1969](../sources/frankfurt-1969.md)) against the **principle of alternate
possibilities** — the long-standing doctrine that responsibility requires the ability to do
otherwise.

The canonical case: Black, a manipulator, wants Jones to perform a certain action. Black has
implanted a device in Jones's brain that will force the action *if* Jones shows any sign of
deciding against it — but Jones decides to perform the action entirely on his own, and the device
never fires. Jones could not have done otherwise (Black guaranteed the outcome), yet everything
that actually happened flowed from Jones's own deliberation, exactly as it would have with no
device present. Intuitively, Jones is fully responsible. The *counterfactual intervener* who never
intervenes deletes the alternatives while leaving the actual sequence untouched.

The moral usually drawn is that what matters to freedom and responsibility is the character of the
**actual sequence** — that the action issued from the agent's own reasons-responsive agency — not
the existence of unrealized alternatives. This reorientation grounds actual-sequence
compatibilisms, most prominently Fischer and Ravizza's account of *guidance control* without
*regulative control* (Fischer & Ravizza, [1998](../sources/fischer-ravizza-1998.md);
[compatibilism](../positions/compatibilism.md)), and puts pressure on the leeway thesis that freedom requires open
alternatives ([alternatives-genuinely-open](../claims/alternatives-genuinely-open.md)). Libertarians respond that the cases smuggle
in determinism or that a flicker of freedom — the sign Black monitors — survives as the needed
alternative.

Within the [consciousness–free-will duality](consciousness-freewill-duality.md), Frankfurt
cases are the output-side thesis that freedom survives the deletion of alternative options; their
proposed input-side dual is the ganzfeld objection — whether consciousness survives the deletion of
differentiated contents ([frankfurt-dualizes-to-ganzfeld](../claims/frankfurt-dualizes-to-ganzfeld.md)).

### In plain terms

Suppose a hidden device would force your hand if you were about to choose differently — but you
never trigger it, because you choose that very thing on your own. You couldn't have done otherwise,
yet the choice was entirely yours. Cases like this convince many philosophers that being free is
about *how your actual choice came about*, not about whether other options were really open.
