The *Münchhausen trilemma* is the claim that any attempt to fully *justify* a belief — to give a reason,
then a reason for the reason, and so on — must end in one of three unsatisfying ways:

1. an **infinite regress** of reasons, each needing a further reason, never completed;
2. a **circle**, in which some reason eventually rests on the belief it was meant to support;
3. a **dogmatic break-off**, stopping at some reason simply asserted without further justification.

None of the three looks like the secure grounding the demand for justification wanted. The name is Hans
Albert's ([albert-1985](../sources/albert-1985.md)) — after the Baron who pulled himself out of a swamp by his own hair — for
what is essentially the ancient regress already catalogued among the *modes of Agrippa* in Greek
skepticism. Albert's own moral is radical: the demand for full justification (*justificationism*) should be
abandoned, not satisfied.

The trilemma is the skeleton of the whole problem of [the external
world](../questions/external-world-knowledge.md) and of justification generally, and the standard positions are best read as each *choosing a horn*:

- **[foundationalism](../positions/foundationalism.md)** accepts the third horn but reframes it: the stopping points are not
  arbitrary dogmas but [basic beliefs](basic-belief.md) that are justified non-inferentially.
- **[coherentism](../positions/coherentism.md)** embraces the second horn, denying it is vicious: justification is the
  *mutual support* of a whole body of beliefs, a virtuous circle rather than a linear chain.
- **[infinitism](../positions/infinitism.md)** accepts the first horn, holding that justification really is an infinite,
  non-repeating chain of reasons.
- **[fallibilism](../positions/fallibilism.md)** rejects the question: since no belief is ever conclusively justified, we
  should stop seeking foundations and instead expose conjectures to criticism
  ([all-knowledge-is-conjectural](../claims/all-knowledge-is-conjectural.md)).
- A Wittgensteinian adds that the chain stops at [hinges](hinge-proposition.md) that are not
  justified at all because they are what justifying presupposes.

### In plain terms

Ask "how do you know?" and whatever answer comes back, you can ask it again of that answer. Keep going and
only three things can happen: the questions never stop (infinite regress), the answers eventually loop back
on themselves (circle), or someone finally says "that's just where I stop" (a brute assertion). Every
theory of knowledge is, at bottom, a choice about which of these three to live with — or, like the
fallibilist, a decision that the whole "justify it all the way down" demand was a mistake to begin with.
