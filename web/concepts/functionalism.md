*Functionalism* is the view that what makes a mental state the state it is — a pain, a belief
that it is raining, a desire for coffee — is the *functional role* it plays, not the stuff it is
made of. A mental state is defined by its causal relations: what typically causes it, and what it
in turn causes in behaviour and in other mental states, rather than by the physical material that
happens to realise it.

The standard illustration is pain. On a functionalist account, pain is whatever state is
typically caused by bodily damage, that in turn causes wincing, attention to the injury, and the
desire to make it stop, and that interacts in the usual way with one's other beliefs and desires.
Anything occupying that role counts as pain, whether it is implemented in neurons, in silicon, or
in some other medium. The view was launched in large part by Hilary Putnam's argument that one and
the same mental state can be *multiply realised* in physically very different systems, so the state
cannot be identical to any single physical type ([putnam-1967](../sources/putnam-1967.md)).

### Senses of the term

Functionalism is a family of theories that differ over *how* the relevant role is specified.

- *Machine-state functionalism* (Putnam's early version) identifies mental states with the states
  of a Turing-machine table: the role is given by a formal specification of which inputs and
  current states produce which outputs and successor states.
- *Role functionalism* identifies a mental state with whatever actually occupies a given causal
  role — the state is the *occupant* of the role, picked out by the relations it stands in.
- *Analytic* or *commonsense functionalism* holds that the relevant roles are fixed by the
  platitudes of folk psychology — the everyday truisms about how beliefs, desires, and sensations
  interact — so that the meaning of mental terms is given by their place in that commonsense theory.

### The live dispute

A question that recurs throughout the philosophy of mind is whether functional roles can be
realised in non-biological substrates — whether, as [substrate
independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md) asserts, the physical medium does no individuating work. Functionalism is naturally
hospitable to that idea, but it does not settle it on its own: everything turns on the *grain* at
which a role is specified, which the bare definition leaves open (see [grain](grain.md)).

### In plain terms

What makes a thought a thought? Functionalism's answer: what it *does*, not what it's made of. A
mental state — say, pain — is defined by its job: it's caused by certain things (injury), it causes
certain things (wincing, the wish to make it stop), and it links up to your other states. Anything
that fills that job counts as pain, whether it runs on brain cells, silicon chips, or something
else. The usual analogy: being a *clock* isn't about being made of brass or quartz — it's about
keeping time, and anything that keeps time is a clock. Functionalism says minds are like that. (The
named variants above are just different views about how strictly to spell out the "job.")
