*First-person access* is the direct, non-inferential acquaintance a subject has with its own
conscious states: the way you know your own pain without watching your behaviour or reasoning your
way to it, simply feeling it from the inside. It is standardly contrasted with *third-person
access*, the inferential route by which anyone comes to beliefs about *another* subject's mental
states — reading them off behaviour, testimony, or theory. The concept names a lopsidedness built
into knowledge of minds: your own current experience is given to you directly, while every other
mind you reach only at a remove.

### Two senses

The term is used in two related but separable ways, and arguments that invoke it should say which
they mean.

In the *epistemic* sense, first-person access is a privileged *warrant*: a subject's reports about
its own present experience are non-inferential, and on the strongest versions *incorrigible* — they
cannot be mistaken. When you sincerely judge that you are in pain, no further evidence is needed and
(on this strong reading) none could overturn it.

In the *metaphysical* or *acquaintance* sense, it names a relation of direct *presentation*: the
state is simply given to its subject, not merely tracked by a reliable belief-forming channel. This
acquaintance notion descends from Bertrand Russell's distinction between knowledge *by acquaintance*
and knowledge *by description* ([russell-1911](../sources/russell-1911.md)) — the difference between being directly
presented with a thing and merely knowing truths about it.

### The disputes it feeds

The asymmetry between knowing one's own states directly and knowing others' only by inference is put
to work in two opposite directions. Pointed *outward*, it is offered as a ground for a moral-status
asymmetry: that direct acquaintance with one's own suffering, absent for any machine, licenses
regarding oneself as a bearer of moral status in a way one is not licensed to regard the machine
(the move made in [first-person-access](../arguments/first-person-access.md), which leans on
[acquaintance-necessary-for-moral-status-warrant](../claims/acquaintance-necessary-for-moral-status-warrant.md) and bears on the
[moral status of LLMs](../questions/llm-moral-status.md)). Pointed back at that move, the same asymmetry is
argued to prove too much — since the access gap holds between *any* two subjects — and so to single
out nothing (the reply in [access-symmetry-rebuttal](../arguments/access-symmetry-rebuttal.md)).

A neighbouring debate turns on access to one's *own* qualia: whether introspecting one's experience
is itself a functional process or a species of acquaintance. That question drives
[dancing-qualia-presupposes-functional-introspection](../claims/dancing-qualia-presupposes-functional-introspection.md) and the
[phenomenal-concept trade-off](../claims/phenomenal-concept-isolation-tradeoff.md). This entry only names
the term those bodies use in passing; it takes no stand on the nature of self-access.

### In plain terms

There's a lopsidedness in how you know about minds. You know your *own* pain directly — you don't
watch your own behavior and deduce "I must be in pain," you just feel it from the inside. But anyone
else's pain you only ever know *indirectly*, from the outside: their wincing, their words, your best
guess about them. That special, direct, inside knowledge of your own experience is what
"first-person access" names. The debates it feeds ask: does that privileged inside view actually
settle anything about who deserves moral consideration — and is your access to your *own* feelings
itself something special, or just more of the mind's ordinary machinery?
