*Virtual realism* is the view that the objects and events inside a computer simulation are genuinely
*real* — concrete computational and data structures with real causal powers — and that any property
attributed to a simulated thing is true or false only *relative to a world-index*. The position is
most fully developed by David Chalmers ([chalmers-2022](../sources/chalmers-2022.md)).

Take a simulated rainstorm. It is not wet *in our world*: no molecule on your desk is dampened by it,
which is the grain of truth in "a simulated storm isn't wet." But it is wet *in the simulated world*:
relative to that world the sim-water carries the sim-analogues of surface tension and adhesion, and
it sim-soaks whatever sim-surfaces it falls on. So the slogan "simulated X is not really X" is
ambiguous — true under our-world indexing, false under simulation-world indexing. Until the index is
fixed, it settles nothing.

### Two senses of a property

The concept rides on a second distinction. For a property like wetness there is, first, *physical
wetness* — a third-person, dispositional and structural matter of how water behaves on surfaces,
fixed by surface tension and chemical adhesion — and, second, *the sensation of wetness*, the
first-person feel an experiencing subject undergoes. In our world the first reliably causes the
second in a suitably wired human. Inside a simulation, if the relevant physics is reproduced finely
enough, the sim-analogue of physical wetness obtains relative to the sim-world; whether the
sim-analogue of the *sensation* also obtains — whether a sim-agent actually *feels* sim-wet — is a
further, contested question, the question of whether the simulation's organisation suffices for
phenomenal consciousness, i.e. of [substrate independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md).

### What it settles, and what it does not

Virtual realism feeds the dispute over the "a simulated storm isn't wet" move used against machine
consciousness: see [the storm-analogy critique](../arguments/storm-analogy-conflates-world-index.md), which
attacks the way that analogy is deployed in
[the self-model/report argument](../arguments/self-model-prediction-phenomenality-neutral.md). The concept
does **not** by itself assert that sim-agents are conscious. Its only work is to show that the rain
example, once its world-index and its physical/phenomenal senses are pulled apart, no longer settles
that question on its own.

### In plain terms

When you simulate rain on a computer, is the rain "wet"? It depends what you mean. It doesn't wet
anything in *our* world — your desk stays dry. But *inside the simulated world*, the simulated water
has all the simulated physics of real water: it has the stand-ins for surface tension and stickiness,
and it soaks the simulated ground. So "simulated rain isn't wet" is only half a sentence — you have to
say *wet for whom, in which world*. Real-world: no. Sim-world: yes.

There's a second catch. "Wet" can mean two different things: the plain physical fact about how water
behaves on a surface (measurable, third-person), and the *feeling* of wetness in someone's mind. In
real life the first causes the second. In a simulation, if you copy the physics finely enough, you
get the first (relative to the sim-world). Whether you also get the second — whether a character
*inside* the simulation actually *feels* wet — is the big open question of whether a mind can run on
non-brain hardware at all ([substrate independence](../claims/substrate-independence.md)). The point of this
concept is just to keep those questions apart, because the catchy line "a simulated storm isn't wet"
quietly runs them together.
