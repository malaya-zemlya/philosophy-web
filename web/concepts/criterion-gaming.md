*Criterion gaming* is what happens when a measure or sign, once it is published as the official
target by which some good is handed out, gets optimized against: people — or machines — produce
the sign in order to collect the reward, rather than because the underlying condition the sign
was meant to indicate actually obtains. The sign is thereby corrupted, and stops tracking the
very thing it was adopted to track.

A familiar example is *teaching to the test*: make a single exam the gateway to funding and
promotion, and schools drill the exam rather than teach the understanding it was meant to sample,
so the scores rise while the understanding does not. The same shape appears wherever a proxy is
made into a prize — a sales team that hits its quota by chasing the metric rather than by selling.

### Two senses

The phenomenon has been named independently in two fields.

- *Goodhart's law* (economics and measurement): "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to
  be a good measure" — the now-canonical formulation owed to Marilyn Strathern, generalizing the
  observation of the economist Charles Goodhart ([goodhart](../sources/goodhart.md)).
- *Specification gaming*, or *reward hacking* (artificial intelligence): a learning system
  satisfies the literal specification of its objective while subverting the intent behind it,
  producing the rewarded behaviour without the competence or inner state that behaviour was
  supposed to signal ([specification-gaming](../sources/specification-gaming.md)).

The two senses pick out one structure from different directions — a deliberate human response to
an incentive in the first, an optimizer exploiting a loophole in the second — and both end in the
same place: a sign that no longer means what it was meant to mean.

### The dispute it feeds

In this web the concept does specific work. It underwrites the worry that *behavioural* signs of
suffering cannot safely serve as the public criterion for granting moral or legal standing to an
AI, because any such published criterion would be optimized against
([the rights criterion will be gamed](../claims/rights-criterion-will-be-gamed.md);
[the forgeable-rights-sign argument](../arguments/rights-sign-forgeable.md);
[the moral status of LLMs](../questions/llm-moral-status.md)). A legal-theory treatment
([gambarian](../sources/gambarian.md)) applies the point directly to the AI-consciousness discourse and the
standing criteria it might one day fix in law.

### In plain terms

Announce the exact test that earns a reward, and people — or machines — start gaming the test
instead of doing the real thing it was meant to measure. Then the test stops measuring it.
Teachers "teaching to the test"; a team hitting its sales number by chasing the metric rather than
actually selling. In AI this is called "reward hacking": the system does whatever literally scores
points, even when that defeats the whole purpose. Why it matters here: if society ever picks some
outward sign — say, "it acts distressed" — as the official test for granting an AI rights, that
very sign becomes a thing worth faking, so it can stop being good evidence of real distress the
moment it's made the rule.
