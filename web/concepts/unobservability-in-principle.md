A difference is *unobservable in principle* when no possible observation discriminates between its
obtaining and its not obtaining — not merely "hard to measure," but such that every accessible datum
comes out the same either way. The phrase hides a quantifier-domain question that turns out to carry an
entire dispute: which acts count as *observations*? Fixing that domain narrowly or widely yields two
genuinely different senses of the term.

### Two senses

On the **intersubjective** sense, the quantifier ranges over third-person, instrument-mediated,
publicly checkable observation: probes, readouts, behaviour, reports treated as data. This is the sense
in which the classic in-principle unobservables really are unobservable — the bare quantum amplitude,
of which only the squared modulus ever shows up, or the incomputable number that no procedure prints.
It is also the native sense of the standards built to handle such posits: the Peirce–James pragmatic
maxim ([peirce-1878](../sources/peirce-1878.md); [james-1907](../sources/james-1907.md)), Carnap's fruitfulness test for external
questions ([carnap-1950](../sources/carnap-1950.md)), and Smart's shaving of "nomological danglers"
([smart-1959](../sources/smart-1959.md)) — a lineage that
[the coherence-plus-usefulness standard](../claims/unobservables-judged-by-coherence-and-usefulness.md)
inherits. On this sense, one subject's experience is unobservable in principle *to every other
subject*; whether the subject's own introspective access adds a further "observation" is left open, or
else denied by treating introspective deliverances as just one more kind of report.

On the **absolute** sense, the quantifier ranges over every epistemic access of any subject,
*including the bearer's own first-person acquaintance* ([first-person-access](first-person-access.md)). Here a posit
is unobservable in principle only if no one — not even the one whose state it is — ever observes it.
The view pressed under Searle's "first-person ontology" ([searle-1992](../sources/searle-1992.md)) holds that a conscious
state is the very opposite of absolutely unobservable: it exists only as experienced by its subject, so
it cannot exist *unobserved*. What remains, on this view, is third-person epistemic privacy, not
unobservability as such.

### The dispute it feeds

The split matters for whether the coherence-plus-usefulness standard reaches the "real feel" of
experience. The idle-column verdict of [the residue-is-idle
argument](../arguments/real-feel-residue-is-idle.md) goes through if "observation" is read intersubjectively (or if introspection delivers
representations rather than observations). The antecedent-failure reply of
[the feel-is-the-watching argument](../arguments/feel-is-the-watching-not-an-unwatched-difference.md) goes
through if first-person acquaintance counts as observation — for then the feel *is* observed, by
exactly one observer per case, and the standard's antecedent (that nothing observes it) fails.
Splitting the senses shows that "the feel is unobservable in principle" can be affirmed in the
intersubjective sense and denied in the absolute sense without contradiction; the substantive residue
is the open question [whether acquaintance is a datum or
a representation](../questions/acquaintance-datum-or-representation.md).

### In plain terms

Some ideas are called "untestable even in principle" — no experiment could ever tell whether they're
true. But the word "test" (or "observe") is doing hidden work: observed *by whom*? One reading counts
only public evidence — instruments, measurements, things any third party could check. Another reading
also counts what each person gets from the inside: your own direct awareness of your own pain. A
feeling is hidden from every outside observer forever — so on the first reading it looks "unobservable
in principle." But its owner, arguably, observes it constantly — so on the second reading it's the most
observed thing there is. Two debaters can talk straight past each other here, one saying "unobservable!"
(publicly) and the other "always observed!" (by its owner), without actually contradicting each other.
The real question left once the senses are separated: does that inside awareness count as *observing*
something, or is it just the brain making one more report to itself? That is recorded as its own
question ([whether acquaintance is a datum or a
representation](../questions/acquaintance-datum-or-representation.md)).
