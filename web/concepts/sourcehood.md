*Sourcehood* is the condition that a free agent be the ultimate **source** or **originator** of her
actions — that when the action is traced to its roots, the trail ends *in the agent* rather than in
factors outside her control. It is one of the two great answers to the question of what freedom
requires. The rival *leeway* condition locates freedom in open alternatives — the agent could have
done otherwise ([alternatives-genuinely-open](../claims/alternatives-genuinely-open.md)). The source condition instead looks past the
alternatives to the action's **provenance**: what matters is not that other options were available
but that *this* option issued from the agent as its genuine origin. The everyday slogan is that,
for a free act, the buck stops with the agent.

The two conditions can come apart, and much of the modern free-will debate turns on prising them
loose. [frankfurt-cases](frankfurt-cases.md) are the standard wedge: a counterfactual intervener who never
intervenes deletes the agent's alternatives while leaving her actual deliberation untouched, so if
she remains responsible, responsibility must rest on the character of the actual sequence — on
sourcehood — not on leeway. This motivates **source incompatibilism**: the view that freedom
requires ultimate origination, that determinism destroys it (because the ultimate source then lies
in the laws and the distant past, not the agent), and that it does so whether or not any
alternatives remain (Pereboom, [pereboom-2014](../sources/pereboom-2014.md)). Source incompatibilism is thus the
half of incompatibilism that survives even if the [frankfurt-cases](frankfurt-cases.md) succeed.

### How an agent might be the source

Sourcehood is a *condition* to be met, not yet a mechanism for meeting it, and the libertarian
options divide over the mechanism. **Agent-causal** libertarianism ([agent-causation](agent-causation.md))
makes the agent a substance that originates action without being caused to — the most direct way to
put the agent at the causal head of the chain. **Event-causal** libertarianism keeps causation
among the agent's own states but adds indeterminism, and secures sourcehood *historically* rather
than at the instant of action: Robert Kane's **ultimate responsibility** (Kane,
[kane-1996](../sources/kane-1996.md)) holds that one can be the ultimate source of an action one could not avoid,
so long as the character and motives it flows from were themselves shaped by earlier undetermined
*self-forming actions*. On this reading the buck need not stop at the present choice; it must stop
somewhere in the agent's own formative history.

### The regress objection

The strongest attack on the condition is that ultimate sourcehood is unattainable in principle. To
be the *ultimate* source of an action, Galen Strawson argues, one must be the source of the will
from which it springs; but to be responsible for that will one must have formed it by some earlier
choice, which sprang from a still earlier will, and so on without end — one would finally have to be
*causa sui*, the cause of oneself, which nothing can be (Strawson, [strawson-1994](../sources/strawson-1994.md)). If the
argument holds, no agent ever fully satisfies the source condition, and a determinist or not, the
libertarian's ultimacy is a mirage. Defenders reply that responsibility can be built up
incrementally across a life of self-forming actions without any single self-creating act, so the
regress never has to bottom out in a *causa sui*.

### In plain terms

There are two different things people can mean when they call an action "free." One is *you could
have done something else* — the road forked and both branches were really open. The other is *it
really came from you* — you were the true origin of it, not just the last domino in a line of
dominoes reaching back before you were born. Sourcehood is the second idea. It matters because of
cases where the first seems absent but we still hold the person responsible: if a hidden chip would
have forced your hand but never had to, because you chose that very thing yourself, then you
couldn't have done otherwise and yet the choice was authentically *yours*
([frankfurt-cases](frankfurt-cases.md)). The hard question, pressed by Galen Strawson's regress, is whether
anyone is ever the *ultimate* origin of anything: to have truly made yourself the source of your
choices, wouldn't you have had to create your own character — and who created the you that did
*that*?
