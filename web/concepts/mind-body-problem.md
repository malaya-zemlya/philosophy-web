The *mind-body problem* is the question of how mental phenomena — thoughts, sensations, beliefs, the felt
quality of experience — are related to physical phenomena, and in particular to the body and brain. We seem
to be, at once, biological organisms made of the same matter as everything else, and subjects for whom
there is *something it is like* to be alive: we feel pain, see colour, mean things, decide. How the second
fits with the first — whether the mental just *is* the physical, or is produced by it, or is something else
again — is the problem. It is the central question of the philosophy of mind, and one of the oldest in
metaphysics.

The modern version is owed to René Descartes ([descartes-meditations](../sources/descartes-meditations.md)), who argued that mind and
body are *really distinct* substances: the body is *res extensa*, extended stuff governed by mechanics,
while the mind is *res cogitans*, an unextended thinking thing. This sharpened the problem into its enduring
shape, because it immediately raised the question Descartes could not answer — pressed at once by Princess
Elisabeth of Bohemia ([elisabeth-1643](../sources/elisabeth-1643.md)): if mind and body are so utterly different in kind, how
can they *interact*, as they plainly do when a decision moves a hand or an injury causes pain?

### Not one problem but several

What goes under the single name is really a family of connected difficulties, and confusion often comes from
running them together.

- **The ontological question.** *What is the mind?* Is it identical to the brain, a distinct substance, a
  property of physical systems, an illusion, or the intrinsic nature of matter? This is the question the
  positions below answer.
- **The problem of mental causation.** *How can the mental have physical effects?* If the physical world is
  [causally closed](causal-closure.md) — every physical event having a sufficient physical cause —
  then mental causes seem either to be identical to physical ones or to be left with no work to do
  ([the causal exclusion problem](../claims/irreducible-mental-causally-excluded.md)). This is Elisabeth's
  objection, made precise.
- **The problem of consciousness.** *Why is any of this accompanied by experience at all?* Even a complete
  account of the brain's functioning seems to leave open why it should *feel* like anything — the
  [hard problem](hard-problem.md), the part of the mind-body problem that has proved most stubborn.
- **The problem of intentionality.** *How can physical states be about things?* A thought reaches out to its
  object ([aboutness](intentionality.md)); how a lump of matter does so is a further puzzle.

### The landscape of answers

The positions can be mapped by what they say minds and bodies fundamentally *are*.

**Dualisms** hold that the mental is not physical. [Substance dualism](../positions/dualism.md) (Descartes) makes
mind a distinct substance; *property dualism* makes it a distinct, non-physical *property* of physical
things — the modern form Chalmers defends, on which experience is a further fundamental feature of the world.
Dualism honours the apparent gap but inherits the interaction problem and the threat of
[epiphenomenalism](../positions/epiphenomenalism.md) (mind as a causally idle by-product).

**Physicalisms (materialisms)** hold that the mental just *is* physical ([physicalism](../positions/physicalism.md);
[mental-is-physical](../claims/mental-is-physical.md)). The family is large: the [identity theory](../positions/type-identity.md) equates
mental states with brain states; [functionalism](functionalism.md) individuates them by causal role,
allowing [multiple realisation](../claims/substrate-independence.md) in other substrates; [eliminativism](../positions/eliminative-materialism.md)
denies that the folk-psychological mind (beliefs, qualia) exists to be reduced; [illusionism](../positions/illusionism.md)
treats phenomenal consciousness itself as an introspective illusion; [biological naturalism](../positions/biological-naturalism.md)
makes consciousness a higher-level biological feature caused by, but not reducible to, neurobiology. The
[type-A / type-B / type-C](materialism-types.md) taxonomy sorts physicalists by how they handle the
appearance of an explanatory gap.

**Monisms beyond standard physicalism.** [Idealism](../positions/idealism.md) reverses the priority, making the
physical a construction out of the mental. [Neutral monism](../positions/neutral-monism.md) makes both aspects of
one underlying stuff that is itself neither. [Dual-aspect monism](../positions/dual-aspect-monism.md) gives one
substance two irreducible aspects. [Russellian monism](../positions/russellian-monism.md) locates consciousness
in the *intrinsic nature* that physics, describing only structure, leaves blank — shading into
[panpsychism](../positions/panpsychism.md) (experience is fundamental and ubiquitous) and its top-down cousin
[cosmopsychism](../positions/cosmopsychism.md).

**Other framings.** [Hylomorphism](../positions/hylomorphism.md) (Aristotle) treats the mind as the *form* of a
living body, sidestepping the substance dichotomy. Gilbert Ryle ([ryle-1949](../sources/ryle-1949.md)) dismissed the whole
Cartesian picture as the dogma of "the ghost in the machine," a *category mistake* — the deflationary
impulse behind behaviourism and much of functionalism. And [mysterianism](../positions/mysterianism.md) is the
meta-position that the problem, though real, lies permanently beyond human understanding.

### Why it resists solution

Several arguments keep the problem alive by pressing on the physicalist and dualist alike. The
[zombie/conceivability argument](../arguments/zombie-conceivability.md) and the
[knowledge argument](../arguments/knowledge-argument.md) press that no amount of physical fact seems to *entail*
the facts of experience. The [explanatory gap](../arguments/explanatory-gap.md) notes that we have no idea *how*
physical processing could constitute feeling. From the other side, the causal exclusion problem squeezes any
view on which the mental is both real and non-physical toward epiphenomenalism. The persistence of these
pressures from both directions — the mental seeming neither cleanly reducible to the physical nor safely
separable from it — is what makes the mind-body problem hard.

### In plain terms

You are a physical thing — a body, a brain, atoms — and also a someone: there's something it's like to be
you, to feel cold or see red or want lunch. The mind-body problem asks how those two stories fit. Is your
mind just your brain doing its thing? Something the brain *produces*? A separate non-physical part? An
illusion? Or is even matter itself somehow a bit mind-like underneath? Descartes said mind and body were two
different kinds of stuff — which immediately raised the puzzle a sharp princess put to him: then how does
one *push* the other? Every answer on offer pays a price somewhere, which is why, four centuries on, the
problem is still open.
