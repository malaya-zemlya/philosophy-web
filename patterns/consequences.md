title: Argument from Consequences
aka: [practical reasoning from outcomes, argument from good/bad consequences]
source: Walton, Reed & Macagno (2008), Argumentation Schemes, ch. 8
inference_form: defeasible
---

## Form
- Premise (Link): If action A is brought about, consequences G follow.
- Premise (Value): G is good (resp. bad).
- Conclusion: A should (resp. should not) be brought about.

## Critical questions
1. **likelihood** — How likely is it that G actually follows from A? → attacks the *Link* premise.
2. **evidence** — Is there evidence that the cited consequences will occur? → attacks the *Link*
   premise's support.
3. **counter-consequences** — Are there consequences of A pointing the *other* way (offsetting
   bad effects of a "good" action, or vice versa)? → raises a competing argument.
4. **value** — Is G really good/bad, and to whom? → attacks the *Value* premise.

## Notes
A normative pattern: its conclusion is a *should*-claim, so it typically lives in the
neighbourhood of `position` nodes. Beware using it where a descriptive question is at stake —
that an idea has unwelcome consequences is no evidence it is false (that misuse is the
"argument from [un]desirable consequences" fallacy; flag it rather than citing this pattern).
