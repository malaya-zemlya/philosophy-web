## Form
- Premise (Data): E is observed (a finding, or set of facts, in need of explanation).
- Premise (Explanation): Hypothesis H would, if true, explain E.
- Premise (Comparison): No available rival hypothesis explains E as well as H does.
- Conclusion: H is (plausibly, defeasibly) true.

## Critical questions
1. **fit** — Does H actually explain E, and how well? Does it explain all of E or only a
   convenient part? → attacks the *Explanation* premise.
2. **rivals** — Is there a rival hypothesis H′ that explains E as well or better? → attacks
   the *Comparison* premise (the decisive question; one live rival downgrades the conclusion
   to a disjunction).
3. **search** — How thorough was the hunt for alternatives? Is "best" merely "best of a lazy
   shortlist"? → attacks the *Comparison* premise's warrant (best-of-a-bad-lot worry).
4. **data** — Is E itself secure — accurately observed, fairly described, not an artefact of
   the method that gathered it? → attacks the *Data* premise.

## Notes
The conclusion inherits the comparative nature of the premises: IBE never establishes H
outright, only H's superiority over the rivals actually on the table, so CQ-rivals and
CQ-search are the standing attack surface. Distinct from `sign`: a sign argument is a
localized, one-step IBE from a conventional indicator, whereas this scheme weighs whole
hypotheses against a body of evidence and makes the comparison premise explicit — cite `sign`
for "S indicates T" moves and this pattern where the body actually argues that rivals fail.
