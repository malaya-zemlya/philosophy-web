## Form
- Premise (Instances): a, b, c … are instances that have property P.
- Premise (Representativeness): they are typical of the class K.
- Conclusion: (Generally) members of K have P.

## Critical questions
1. **support** — Do the cited instances actually have P? → attacks the *Instances* premise.
2. **representativeness** — Are the instances typical of K, or a biased/small sample? → attacks
   the *Representativeness* premise.
3. **counter-examples** — Are there known members of K that *lack* P? → raises a refuting case
   (the existential attack; one clear counter-example downgrades a universal generalisation to a
   hedged one).

## Notes
Mirror image of `thought-experiment`: this pattern *builds* a generalisation from cases, while a
counterexample *breaks* one. If a single imagined case is meant to refute a universal claim, that
is a thought-experiment, not an inductive generalisation — cite `thought-experiment` and aim
CQ-counter-examples' logic in reverse.
