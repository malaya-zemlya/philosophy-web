## Form
- Premise (Observation): Sign S is present.
- Premise (Correlation): S is a (reliable) indicator of state T.
- Conclusion: T (plausibly) obtains.

## Critical questions
1. **reliability** — How strong is the correlation between S and T? → attacks the *Correlation*
   premise.
2. **alternatives** — Could S instead be a sign of some other state T′? → attacks the inference
   (S underdetermines T).
3. **forgeability** — Can S be present without T — produced, faked, or coincidental? → attacks
   the *Correlation* premise's sufficiency.

## Notes
Distinct from `cause-to-effect`: a sign needn't be caused by T (smoke is a sign of fire and is
caused by it; a falling barometer is a sign of a storm but doesn't cause it). CQ-forgeability is
the decisive one wherever the sign is used as a *criterion* (e.g. taking a behavioural or
biological marker as the test for mind — cf. `web/arguments/rights-sign-forgeable.md`): a
forgeable sign is unfit as a criterion even if usually reliable.
