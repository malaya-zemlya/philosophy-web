## Form
- Premise (Generalisation): Generally, if cause C occurs, effect E follows.
- Premise (Instance): C occurs (or obtains) in this case.
- Conclusion: E (plausibly) occurs in this case.

## Critical questions
1. **strength** — Is the causal generalisation C→E actually true, and how strong? → attacks the
   *Generalisation* premise.
2. **other-causes** — Could E here be produced by something other than C? → attacks the
   inference (E underdetermines C; defeats abductive runs of this move).
3. **interference** — Is some factor in this case blocking C from producing E? → attacks the
   inference via a defeater/exception.
4. **correlation** — Is the C–E link genuinely causal, or merely correlational? → attacks the
   *Generalisation* premise at its root.

## Notes
For effect→cause (abductive) runs — "E is here, so C caused it" — CQ-other-causes is the live one
and should almost always be addressed in the body. Keep the causal claim and the instance claim
as **separate** premise nodes (atomic-claim rule); CQ-strength attacks the former, CQ-instance
issues the latter.
