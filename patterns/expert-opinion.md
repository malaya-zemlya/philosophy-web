## Form
- Premise (Expertise): E is an expert in domain D.
- Premise (Assertion): E asserts that A, and A is within D.
- Conclusion: A is (plausibly, defeasibly) true.

## Critical questions
1. **expertise** — Is E a genuine expert in D? → attacks the *Expertise* premise.
2. **field** — Is A actually within D, or has E strayed outside their competence? → attacks the
   fit between *Assertion* and *Expertise*.
3. **opinion** — Did E really assert A (quoted accurately, in context, not hedged away)? →
   attacks the *Assertion* premise.
4. **trustworthiness** — Is E personally reliable here, or biased/conflicted? → attacks the
   inference (a *legitimate* ad hominem, because testimony rests on trust).
5. **consistency** — Do other experts in D disagree with A? → raises a counter-authority
   (a competing argument, not a premise defeater).
6. **backup** — Is E's assertion grounded in evidence E can give, or bare say-so? → attacks the
   inference's strength.

## Notes
Weakest point is usually CQ-field (overreach) or CQ-consistency (expert dissensus). In a
philosophy web, prefer to argue from the expert's *reasons* (cite the `source` and reconstruct
its argument) rather than from authority alone; an unanswered CQ-backup is a standing signal that
the argument should be replaced by the first-order case.
