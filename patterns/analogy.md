## Form
- Premise (Similarity): Case C1 is similar to case C2 in respects R.
- Premise (Base verdict): A holds in C1.
- Conclusion: A (plausibly) holds in C2.

## Critical questions
1. **relevance** — Are the shared respects R the ones *relevant* to A, or incidental? → attacks
   the *Similarity* premise (similar, but not in the way that matters).
2. **disanalogy** — Are there important differences between C1 and C2 that bear on A? → attacks
   the inference by introducing a defeating difference.
3. **base** — Does A really hold in C1, for the reasons assumed? → attacks the *Base verdict*.
4. **counter-analogy** — Is there a third case C3, at least as similar to C2, in which A *fails*?
   → raises a competing analogy (a rival argument).

## Notes
The whole game is CQ-disanalogy: a single relevant difference can sink an analogy that a hundred
similarities can't save. When citing this pattern, name the respects R explicitly in the body so
the disanalogy attack has a target. Many thought experiments are analogies in disguise (the
target case is imagined) — if so, consider whether `thought-experiment` is the better `pattern:`.
